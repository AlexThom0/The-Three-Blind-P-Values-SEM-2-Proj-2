By designing experiments that utilized various stimuli—faces, objects, and scenes—I aimed to investigate whether people could accurately distinguish between familiar and unfamiliar stimuli without being able to articulate the source of their familiarity. Participants would be shown a series of images, some of which they had encountered before and others that were new. After viewing these images, they would undergo a recognition test where they had to indicate whether they had seen each image previously. However, I included a crucial twist: participants would also be asked to rate their familiarity with each image, even if they were uncertain about having seen it before. This setup was intended to tease apart the layers of memory that allow for recognition without commitment to a specific recollection. In line with the source-monitoring framework, I hypothesized that individuals might exhibit varying degrees of confidence in their familiarity ratings even when faced with previously encountered images. This would highlight the complex interplay between implicit and explicit memory systems. Encouraging participants to elaborate on their reasoning could further illuminate the nuances of how recognition operates. For instance, if a participant felt a strong sense of familiarity but was unable to recall specific details about the image, it could indicate that their implicit memory systems were engaged, while the explicit memory systems remained inactive. This distinction could provide deeper insight into how these systems interact during recognition tasks. Moreover, I was particularly intrigued by the role that contextual factors might play in this dynamic. Would environmental cues or emotional states enhance or hinder the ability to recognize an image without recalling its source? To investigate this, I planned to vary the context in which the recognition tasks were performed, introducing differing levels of distraction and emotional stimuli. By manipulating these variables, I aimed to uncover the environmental inputs that might either bolster or impede the recognition process. Early studies in memory recall indicated that context often plays a pivotal role in retrieval success, suggesting that understanding these influences could be significant for both cognitive science and practical applications in educational settings or therapeutic contexts. Additionally, contrasting these phenomena with conditions like amnesia could yield a clearer picture of how memory operates under different circumstances. Previous research had indicated that individuals with certain types of amnesia still exhibit signs of recognition, hinting at dissociations between implicit and explicit memories. By incorporating participants with varied memory capabilities into my studies, I hoped to enrich the data and ensure that my findings would resonate across a broad spectrum of cognitive experiences. Ultimately, this research aimed not only to elucidate the mechanisms of recognition without identification but also to contribute to a growing body of work that seeks to navigate the intricacies of human memory. As I embarked on this endeavor, I felt optimistic that the convergence of insights from my prior studies and Brown's hypothesis would pave the way for compelling discoveries regarding the both the phenomenology and the underlying mechanisms of memory.
