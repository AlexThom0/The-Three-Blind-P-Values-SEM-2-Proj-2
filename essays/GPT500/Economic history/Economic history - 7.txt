A shift towards neoliberal policies began to take root in many societies, championing deregulation, privatization, and a reduction in government intervention in economies. These reforms were accompanied by an increasing belief in the efficacy of free markets as mechanisms for growth, which resonated with both developed and developing countries that sought to emerge from the shadow of debt and stagnation. In this context, the Washington Consensus emerged as a guiding set of principles for economic reform, advocating for liberalization measures intended to reinvigorate economies crippled by debt and isolation. Countries like Mexico, which had been trapped in a cycle of borrowing and austerity, sought to harness these new economic ideologies. By promoting exports and attracting foreign capital, Mexico embarked on a series of trade agreements that would alter its economic landscape. The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) framework, pivotal for multilateral negotiations, started to gain traction in Latin America and beyond, signaling a reorientation towards global markets. The expectation was that by aligning local economies with global trade practices, nations could extricate themselves from the debilitating effects of debts tied to external creditors. In the wake of this policy shift, there emerged a renewed interest in developing countries as potential markets rather than mere sources of raw materials. Nations such as Brazil and Argentina began participating in regional trade agreements that favored mutual growth, which simultaneously offered new avenues for investment. These changes revitalized economies, drawing foreign investment into sectors beyond traditional agriculture and manufacturing. Countries formerly constrained by their economic tragedies began to envision a future where they could participate meaningfully in the global economy, transforming their positions from passive recipients to active participants in trade. However, this newfound optimism came with a dual edge. As markets opened, the vulnerabilities tied to dependence on foreign investment became apparent. Fluctuations in global capital markets could quickly reverse fortunes, leading to capital flight which, in turn, risked exposing countries to renewed financial crises. The interconnectedness fostered by globalization did not guarantee stability; rather, it often led to greater exposure to the whims of international investors. Furthermore, the focus on liberalization and deregulation placed many economies at risk of neglecting social welfare, as governments prioritized investor confidence over the needs of their populations. In this volatile environment, countries were confronted with the challenge of balancing the demands of global markets with the pressing social needs of their citizens. A substantial portion of the population in many developing nations continued to live in poverty, prompting questions about the equity of policies intended to promote growth. As discussions surrounding trade negotiations intensified, the necessity for incorporating social and environmental considerations into trade agreements became increasingly apparent, laying the groundwork for more holistic approaches to globalization that would follow.
