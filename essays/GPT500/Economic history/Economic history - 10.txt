The underlying belief was that every individual counted was a testament to the state’s capability, its reach extending to every corner of society. This belief was not merely quantitative; it was imbued with a sense of ideological legitimacy. By counting every person, the state affirmed its authority and the presumed homogeneity of its people, thus sidestepping complexities inherent in social hierarchies and differences. The rejection of probability-based methodologies illustrated a conscious choice to elevate certainty over uncertainty, even at the cost of ignoring potential variations within populations. The prominence of complete enumeration illuminated the tension between theoretical idealism and practical reality. While exhaustive counting promised an illusion of absolute clarity, it ultimately obscured the nuanced, often chaotic nature of human behaviour. Ignoring the inherent unpredictability present in social dynamics, statisticians created a framework that demanded conformity to a singular narrative—a narrative that was often crafted to serve political ambitions rather than reflect true social conditions. This undermining of nuance meant that critical demographic shifts, irregularities, and transitory phenomena were at risk of being either overlooked or misrepresented. Furthermore, the rigid hierarchy of methods imposed by the statisticians reinforced a culture of compliance within data gathering agencies and local administrations. Officials were incentivised, both socially and politically, to deliver numbers that aligned with expectations, altering or excluding data to fit the narrative of progress and unity. The quest for statistical legitimacy sometimes turned into a contest to present the 'right' answers, further propagating a cycle of misinformation and ignorance about the actual lived experiences of citizens. Individual voices could easily be drowned out in a cacophony of aggregate data, diluting the rich tapestry of individual experiences into uniform statistics devoid of human context. This harvesting of data became an exercise in legitimizing predetermined conclusions, with entire datasets constructed around ideological imperatives. Reports were crafted not just to inform but to justify state policies, thereby turning numbers into instruments of governance rather than tools of understanding. Such an approach not only raised ethical questions about the integrity of data collection but also set a dangerous precedent for how social realities were constructed and maintained. As scrutiny of these methods increased, criticisms mounted regarding the limitations of relying solely on exhaustive counts. Scholars began to advocate for the inclusion of more varied methodologies, arguing that an understanding of social realities could not solely rely on exhaustive enumeration. They raised the issue of whether a more nuanced approach, incorporating probabilistic techniques alongside qualitative measures, might provide a more authentic representation of the social fabric. By embracing a blend of methodologies, the complexities and contradictions of human experience could emerge, offering insights that comprehensive counts simply could not reveal. Through this lens, the gap between numbers and lives could be bridged, allowing for a richer, more meaningful exploration of Chinese society.
