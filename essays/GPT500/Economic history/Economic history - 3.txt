The landscape of higher education began to transform under the pressures of market logic, which not only emphasized competition among institutions but also reframed the student’s role from an engaged learner to a consumer. This shift fostered an environment in which educational equity, once a goal of student activism, began to crumble under the weight of rising tuition costs and the commodification of knowledge. The enthusiasm for education was increasingly tempered by the realization that degrees could lead to insurmountable debt rather than secure employment. Institutions began to prioritize profit margins over pedagogical integrity, creating a hierarchy within academia where elite universities flourished at the expense of less prestigious colleges. This divide exacerbated the disparities faced by students from lower socio-economic backgrounds, who found themselves entering universities that lacked the resources to adequately support their success. The burgeoning reliance on student loans as a primary means of funding education turned the act of learning into a gamble, one which many young people, especially those from marginalized communities, were ill-equipped to navigate. As borrowings increased, the burden of student debt became a cross-generational crisis, hindering the financial autonomy of graduates and perpetuating cycles of poverty. The ramifications of this issue extended beyond individual graduates, as the workforce began to reflect these inequities. Employers, increasingly valuing degrees over experience, perpetuated a system where educational attainment was synonymous with class privilege. Consequently, young people who might have contributed to a more equitable society found themselves trapped in jobs that did not require degrees but still demanded repayment of their educational loans. Moreover, as universities adopted a more corporate approach to education, the holistic aspects of learning—critical thinking, creativity, and social engagement—began to erode. A culture of competition proliferated, compelling students to prioritize grades and job placement over genuine intellectual exploration. This environment cultivated a transactional understanding of education, stripping students of the agency once fought for during the civil rights movements and leaving them vulnerable to the dictates of market trends. The commodification of education also fueled a rise in predatory practices by for-profit institutions, which targeted low-income and minority students, promising opportunity while delivering debt without substantial educational returns. Consequently, the ethos of student activism, which once sought to dismantle oppressive structures, was compromised by an overarching narrative promoting individual success at a steep collective cost. As neoliberal principles infiltrated higher education, the campus experience evolved into a reflection of broader societal inequities, where access to knowledge remained disproportionately privileged. Thus, the idealism of the past clashed with a new reality defined by economic vulnerability and constrained opportunities, ultimately reshaping the relationship between students, universities, and society at large.
