This deep engagement sparked a kind of dialogue with the piece, allowing me to explore interpretations that had eluded me when reading from the score. I found that memorisation transcended mere technical proficiency; it encouraged emotional investment and a nuanced understanding of the narrative embedded within the music. This realisation forced me to reassess the traditional views on memorisation propagated through decades of pedagogical thought. In previous generations, memorisation was often viewed as a path toward artistic authenticity; now it seemed to be reducing our interaction with the score to mechanical repetition rather than a lived experience. I began examining the opinions of esteemed musicians, like Vladimir Horowitz, who once stated that memorisation was an act of love, an understanding that only emerges fully when one completely immerses oneself in the music. Each practice session turned into an exploration, a quest for deeper artistic insight. Yet, the ghosts of earlier eras haunted this process. The idea that memorisation was somehow an affront to the score loomed large. While some musicians might argue that a heavy reliance on memory can lead to unintentional misinterpretations—perhaps even an artistic dishonesty—in my experience, the act of understanding a piece through memorisation opened new avenues for expression. I began to question whether the musicians of the past, including those like Schumann, who hastily dismissed memory as an obstacle to authenticity, might have inadvertently stifled their interpretative abilities by not fully embracing the personal journey that memorisation can provide. Moreover, I discovered parallels in other performing arts. Actors, for example, often rely on their ability to memorize lines in order to inhabit their characters more fully. This necessity for memorisation fosters an intimacy with the text that is vital for authentic performance. In a similar vein, for musicians, allowing the music to inhabit the mind and body opens up creative pathways previously stifled by pedantic adherence to the score. It becomes evident that the line between authenticity and arrogance is, in fact, more blurred than traditional narratives suggest. Additional insights emerged as I began collaborating with fellow musicians. Observing how each approached memorisation led me to appreciate the diversity in methods, from mental visualization to physical embodiment of the music. Musicians’ varied strategies not only enhanced their individual performances but also enriched collective interpretations during rehearsals. This collaborative environment reinforced that memorisation is not merely a solitary endeavor but rather a communal journey that fosters shared understanding and creativity. Thus, I started to view my ongoing practice less as a challenge to my memory and more as a vibrant exploration of musical identity, one that bridged past practices with present experiences. Each repetition became a step toward a more profound connection with the music, validating Clara Schumann's idea of memorisation as an expansive, liberating force rather than a limiting one.
