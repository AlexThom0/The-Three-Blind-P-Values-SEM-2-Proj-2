De Bury's relationship with books serves as a complex lens through which we can examine the evolving attitudes toward reading and ownership that characterized the late medieval period. The growing accessibility of texts during this time democratized literacy, creating a cultural shift where books became not just items of elite luxury, but also objects of fascination and reverence for emerging middle classes. As literacy spread, so did the notion that books were keys to wisdom, empowerment, and social mobility. The increase in book production, spurred by advancements in printing technology, further fueled this shift, allowing for a proliferation of texts that reached a broader audience than ever before. While de Bury’s obsession might seem frivolous or superficial, his actions reflect deeper societal changes: the transformation of books from mere status symbols to vital tools for intellectual and social advancement. This period was marked by the gradual transition from manuscript culture to printed material, which not only altered the physical characteristics of books but also shifted how they were perceived and interacted with. The rich ornamentation of manuscripts gave way to the less elaborate but infinitely reproducible printed texts, which were more accessible and often less expensive. Notably, the impulse to collect became intertwined with the desire to curate knowledge, turning the act of reading into a communal endeavor. Book clubs and reading societies began to emerge, fostering discussion and debate around texts that had previously been the province of solitary scholars or the upper classes. This cultural shift was critical in breaking down the barriers that had long kept literature confined to the elite, giving rise to a new literary culture where diverse voices and ideas could flourish. Scholars began to explore texts not only for their content but also for their capacity to engage different perspectives, inviting interpretations from a wider audience. The expansion of the reading public inspired a new wave of authors who catered to emerging tastes, leading to the production of works that appealed to varying levels of literacy and interest. This democratization of literature not only transformed the reading experience but also encouraged a more robust dialogue surrounding texts themselves and the ideas they propagate. As literacy rose, so too did the complexities surrounding authorship and ownership. Literature was no longer the exclusive domain of the author; it became an interactive space where readers could become co-creators of meaning through their interpretations and reflections. The delicate interplay between reader, book, and author prompted an evolving conception of literary value, advocating for a more nuanced understanding of who gets to claim authority over a text. Exploring these multifaceted relationships reveals the progressive nature of literary culture during this period, highlighting how individual experiences of reading began to reshape collective understandings of knowledge and identity. This evolution not only emphasized the importance of books as cultural artifacts but also established a precedent for later debates over authorship and intellectual property.
