Woolf's insistence on capturing the subtleties of human consciousness clashed with Bennett’s pragmatic approach rooted in realism. This divergence is particularly noteworthy when considering the backdrop of societal change and the shifting dynamics of readership, which saw the rise of mass media and the proliferation of genres that aimed to appeal to both the educated elite and the burgeoning middle classes. The modernists were, in a sense, in a race against time, grappling with the challenge of crafting works that would endure beyond their immediate context while simultaneously pushing against the established norms that defined literary merit. Bennett’s work, often seen as embodying the ‘middlebrow’, focused on the familiarities of everyday life, appealing to a broad readership. This approach, while successful in its own right, was viewed by Woolf and her contemporaries as a dilution of artistic integrity. Woolf’s skepticism towards the middlebrow aesthetic is significant, as it articulates a desire to reach for a higher truth that transcended the mere portrayal of social scenarios. The artistic tension reflected a concern for the ‘sincerity of experience’—a theme Woolf continually explored in her writings, where she sought to articulate the complexities of individual perception and emotion, often through innovations in narrative form. These competing views on literature were not merely theoretical but formed a critical part of the dialogue within literary circles. The dichotomy of high art versus popular literature mirrored broader cultural shifts, as the modernist project sought to redefine the boundaries of what constituted 'literature' in a rapidly industrializing world. Woolf’s advocacy for subjective experience, as expressed in her essays, can be seen as a direct response to what she perceived as Bennett’s limitations. Her critique was not simply aimed at the content of Bennett’s work but at the broader implications of how literature could reflect and shape human experiences in an era increasingly defined by alienation and fragmentation. The challenge posed by Bennett’s accessible style resided in its implications for writers who aspired to push boundaries. By framing his narratives around recognizable characters in relatable situations, Bennett inadvertently positioned himself as both a gateway and barrier for future literary innovation. Woolf’s allusions to streams of consciousness and her explorations of time were responses to a literary world that seemed too comfortable with predictability. In this context, Woolf's radical narrative techniques emerged not only as a rejection of traditional storytelling but as a necessity born of an urgent need to connect with the reader on deeper, more intricate levels. Ultimately, the interactions and tensions between these authors reveal a rich tapestry of artistic endeavor, where the lines between high art and popular culture blur, capturing the essence of an era in flux. Their literary exchanges continue to resonate, offering insights into the processes of creation and the ongoing evolution of literary forms.
