In this context, Yanagita’s recording of legends served not only as a preservation of folklore but as a counter-narrative to the encroaching ideologies of a mechanized society. The narratives of Tōno became repositories of a rich, localized understanding of existence that resisted the homogenizing forces of modernity. The villagers’ interactions with spirits—through rituals, festivals, and oral storytelling—served as vibrant expressions of a worldview where the supernatural was interwoven with daily life. Ghosts, for these communities, were not mere phantoms of fear to be avoided but meaningful figures that embodied historical trauma, collective memory, and cultural continuity. The storytelling tradition itself, then, acted as a conduit for communal identity, wherein each tale offered layers of meaning that were not easily translatable into the sterile language of modern rationality. The shift in narrative perspective owed much to the tensions between rural traditions and urban advancements, reflecting a broader societal struggle to maintain cultural integrity amidst modernization. In the eyes of the villagers, the myths and legends they hoarded were alive, embedded with the power to evoke memories and instill lessons that had weathered generations. This preservation of the supernatural was not simply a nostalgic longing for a bygone era but a deliberate act of resistance against the reduction of human experience to data points and quantifiable analyses. Encounters with the supernatural enriched the fabric of community life, as stories were told around fires, passed down through the generations, signaling that what was once alive continued to thrive in shared spaces and collective imagination. Moreover, these ghost stories often served as reflections of societal anxieties and aspirations, embodying the conflicts between the old ways and the new realities, the rural and the urban. Unresolved tensions in these narratives pointed toward a communal yearning for stability in the face of an ever-accelerating world. The very act of recounting such tales not only reinforced existing cultural norms but also provided spaces for questioning and critique, prompting listeners to reflect on their beliefs and fears within a rapidly changing landscape. As the specter of modernity loomed larger, the traditional narratives of the supernatural, particularly in the hands of storytellers like Yanagita, began to crystallize into forms that both celebrated and challenged the encroaching changes. In this light, the supernatural narratives capture a spectrum of human emotions and experiences, from fear and loss to hope and resilience. By grounding these stories within the framework of lived experience, Yanagita illuminated the dynamic relationship between belief and understanding, emphasizing that acknowledging the intangible aspects of life did not diminish one's grasp of reality but rather enriched it. The tales of spirits and the uncanny evolved into a means through which communities could articulate resistance to reductionist interpretations of the human condition, asserting the relevance of what happened beyond the material world. In the face of modernity, honoring these traditions became a way not only to keep the stories alive but also to fortify the very essence of what it meant to be human.
