In this context, the significance of medieval literature extends beyond mere historical interest; it becomes a pivotal instrument in shaping and reinforcing national identity. Authors such as Tolkien and Lewis not only drew from ancient texts but transformed them into platforms for wider discourse on morality, loyalty, and cultural heritage. Their narratives offered a romanticized vision that celebrated virtues aligned with the burgeoning nationalism of their time, intermingling the mythical with the socio-political landscapes of early 20th-century England. This blending facilitated a sense of belonging and continuity amid the rapid industrial and social changes that marked the era. By revisiting these ancient narratives, they sought to instill in their contemporaries a shared ethos—an understanding of a collective past that illuminated their present struggles and aspirations. Furthermore, the stylization of medieval texts served a dual purpose: it provided escapism while simultaneously reinforcing the status quo. The glorification of chivalric ideals and noble quests resonated with an audience yearning for clarity and purpose in an increasingly fragmented world. The ethical dimensions of these stories—heroes overcoming monstrous evils, the valor in defending one’s homeland, and the importance of kinship ties—resonated deeply, promoting narratives that paralleled current nationalistic sentiments. This selective engagement with history not only sought to educate but also to embolden a generation, crafting a mythos that fostered pride and identity in a time marked by uncertainty. The works adopted by the Oxford curriculum exemplified this cultural phenomenon, where medieval constructs were lauded not merely for their literary merit but for their capacity to produce idealized visions of Englishness. The scholarly emphasis on these texts as reflections of national character encouraged students to internalize these lessons, to see themselves as inheritors of a rich, albeit fictionalized, legacy. Such educational frameworks undoubtedly influenced the students' perceptions and values, ultimately shaping their roles not just as scholars, but as bearers of a nationalistic narrative. Additionally, this amalgamation of literature and identity formation predisposed students to view the past through a lens that favored their own cultural narratives, often disregarding the multitude of voices and experiences that diverged from the dominant story. The very choice of which texts to elevate and how to interpret them inherently privileged specific perspectives while marginalizing others. Thus, the careful curation of medieval texts within the academic landscape of Oxford can be interpreted as not just an intellectual exercise but as an ideological project—one that aligned with broader colonialist ambitions to affirm English superiority through cultural heritage. This approach paradoxically reinforced both a collective identity and a narrow vision of what constituted English literature, thus raising questions about inclusivity and representation within the canon. The implications of this literary canonization are profound, as it continues to impact contemporary understandings of national identity, effective citizenship, and cultural heritage in an increasingly globalized world, where the boundaries of identity and literature are constantly being redefined. Understanding these dynamics serves as a crucial step in unpacking the layered connections between education, culture, and power.
