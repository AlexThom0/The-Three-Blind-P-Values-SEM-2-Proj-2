If ethics is to align itself more closely with the empirical world, one avenue is to explore the ways in which ethical terms can be construed in naturalistic terms without diluting their significance. The construct of natural goodness provides a compelling framework for such exploration. By linking ethical concepts to the practical and observable dimensions of human life, we bolster the claim that ethical evaluations can be grounded in the tangible world, circumventing the perceived pitfalls of abstract moralism. Consider the role of evolutionary biology in this context. The evolutionary narrative presents a continuum whereby our moral instincts and social behaviors can be seen as adaptations, serving to enhance cooperation and cohesion within groups. This perspective allows for a naturalized foundation of ethics that does not invoke transcendent properties; rather, it emphasizes the biological and social underpinnings that foster moral considerations. The capacity for altruism may emerge from evolutionary pressures that reward cooperative behavior, leading to a form of ethical reasoning that is ingrained in our very nature as social beings. In this light, moral sentiments are not merely arbitrary or culturally constructed but might reflect deeper, inheritable traits shaped by our species’ survival needs. Furthermore, the appeal to naturalistic foundations suggests a pathway for addressing moral disagreement. When disputes arise over what constitutes the 'good', locating our discussions within the context of shared human experiences and biological necessities can pave the way for more productive dialogue. Instead of debating abstract moral universals, a focus on the conditions that promote human flourishing can guide discussions and highlight commonalities across differing ethical paradigms. What constitutes a ‘well-functioning’ life can vary, yet the broader recognition of certain intrinsic human needs—such as health, security, and the capacity for social bonds—remains a shared concern that transcends individual perspectives. Critically, this approach necessitates a reconciliation of subjective experiences with objective realities. The challenge is to articulate a vision of ethics that is both responsive to the myriad of individual values while remaining anchored in a consensus about human conditions. Engaging with empirical data from various disciplines can enrich our ethical frameworks, allowing us to draw upon insights from psychology, sociology, and even anthropology. For example, understanding the social determinants of health can inform ethical considerations about justice and equity in healthcare, transforming moral debates into practical assessments of human welfare. Ultimately, the drive to establish an ethical theory that resonates with our lived realities can serve as a powerful motivator for further philosophical inquiry. The interplay between naturalism and ethical discourse is not merely an academic exercise but rather a necessary endeavor that seeks to bring ethics back into the realm of human experience. By grounding our discussions in the natural, we can aspire toward ethical constructs that honor both the complexities of individual experience and the shared realities of human existence. Thus, the aspiration for a naturalized ethical foundation emerges not as a disservice to moral thought, but as a potential path toward greater understanding and unity in our ethical considerations.
