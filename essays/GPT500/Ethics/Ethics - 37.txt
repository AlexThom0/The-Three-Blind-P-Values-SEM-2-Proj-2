Morgen’s return to the investigation underscores a pivotal moment in the relationship between the SS and the judiciary; it highlights the complexities of loyalty and ideological fervor within the ranks of the Nazi regime. By bringing Morgen back into the fold, Himmler was not merely attempting to combat corruption but was also signaling a shift in how he intended to manage the moral dilemmas posed by the concentration camps. The very nature of the camp system was fraught with conflict: on one hand, it served the regime’s brutal agenda, while on the other, it engendered a culture of greed and crime among those tasked with carrying out its directives. This paradox was perhaps most visible in the rampant theft and exploitation surrounding the appropriation of property from the victims. As the camps expanded, so too did the opportunities for petty criminals within the SS to enrich themselves at the expense of the very people they were meant to oversee. Morgen’s investigations often led him to confront not just isolated cases of corruption, but a systemic issue embedded deeply within the camp infrastructure. He uncovered instances where guards would send their families money and goods seized from prisoners, effectively transforming the camps into a secondary source of income for many who operated them. The intersection of duty and self-interest became increasingly blurred, mirroring the regime’s overall moral decay. Despite the inherent risks of defying his fellow officers, Morgen's dedication to integrity within the judiciary motivated him to confront these SS members, albeit carefully and often covertly. However, the ramifications of his actions were not limited to mere administrative consequences. The environment of fear and loyalty within the SS meant that accusations against a fellow officer could easily lead to retaliation. The landscape was further complicated by the increasing desperation felt by many as the war turned against Germany; survival instincts often trumped loyalty to the ideological tenets that had initially driven these men. As they faced battles on the eastern front and dwindling resources, some SS officers reconciled their complicity in the atrocities with a misguided sense of patriotism. The existential struggle for power within the SS mirrored the external conflict with the Allies, leading to a culture where betraying a comrade—especially one involved in theft—could seem as perilous as fighting the advancing Soviet forces. Consequently, Morgen’s role evolved into that of an investigator not merely hunting down malfeasance but also navigating a dangerous maze of loyalty and self-preservation. The moral fabric of the SS, intertwined with pervasive corruption, illustrated how far the once-noble pursuit of justice had eroded under the weight of war. Each case Morgen pursued revealed deeper fissures, both in the organization and within himself. He was caught in a moral quagmire, a reflection of the broader moral conflict enveloping a society that was increasingly marked by violence and betrayal. Through this lens, Morgen's investigations became emblematic of the tragic intertwining of ambition and degradation that characterized the Nazi regime during its final years.
