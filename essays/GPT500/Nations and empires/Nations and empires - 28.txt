The utilization of the term ‘Anglo-Saxon’ served not just as an identifier but also as a tool of cultural hegemony, enabling a narrative that contrasted the supposed superiority of Anglo-Saxon civilization against other ethnicities. This narrative was reinforced by the burgeoning scientific disciplines of anthropology and sociology, which were beginning to popularize ideas around race as biologically determinative of cultural traits and societal progress. This ideation was not quarantined to France; rather, it resonated throughout Europe and into the Americas, feeding into imperialistic ideologies that sought justification for colonial conquests under the guise of civilizing missions. The notion of ‘civilization’ was increasingly equated with the socio-political and economic dominance exhibited by Anglo-Saxon nations, intertwining their identity with narratives of progress and development that systematically marginalized non-Anglo-Saxon peoples. The cultural implications were manifold, impacting literature, political discourse, and even social policies, as the mythos of the Anglo-Saxon became a means for both justification and rationalization of expansionist conduct. As this mythos solidified, it began to pave a road towards a broader acceptance of racial hierarchies, complicating the international dialogue on race and identity that would unfold in subsequent decades. Furthermore, the rise of the industrial revolution added fuel to the fire; with it came the concomitant rise of a burgeoning middle class, eager to assert their identity in contrast to what they perceived as less civilized populations. This created a fertile ground for the adoption of these racialized identities not merely as markers of national pride but also as frameworks for exclusion and discrimination. Hence, the social construct of the Anglo-Saxon began to influence everything from immigration policies to the educational curricula, perpetuating stereotypes that often overlooked the complexities of the ethnic and cultural tapestries existing within these societies. Racialized language shaped public opinion, painting those outside the Anglo-Saxon sphere as lesser beings, often designated to serve the socio-economic ambitions of their supposed betters. The interplay of these elements underscores the importance of critically examining the historical contexts that birthed such terms. It was not solely a linguistic evolution but a manifestation of deeper ideologies that served to reinforce social hierarchies and intergroup dynamics that continue to resonate today. Thus, the term 'Anglo-Saxon' evolved not only within the academic confines but as part of a broader, more insidious pattern of racial and cultural division that shaped political outcomes and social policies in both the Old World and the New.
