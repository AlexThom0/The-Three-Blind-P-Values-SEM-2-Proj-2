This macabre practice exemplified a broader trend of dehumanization, as the physical bodies of Native Americans were treated as mere specimens for scientific inquiry, reflecting a disturbing intersection of racial theory and imperialism. The anatomical obsessiveness of these scholars did not solely seek to understand differences—it was deeply entwined with efforts to justify policies of removal and extermination. The justifications for such actions were couched in a language that claimed to be objective and enlightened, yet they belied an inherent bias that viewed Indigenous peoples as obstacles to progress. In this pursuit, writers like Lewis Henry Morgan and others argued that such racial hierarchies were not only natural but also a necessary part of civilization's advance. They posited that Native Americans were at a primitive stage of development, incapable of assimilation into the more 'advanced' cultures of European descent. This intellectual framework did not exist in a vacuum; it was complemented by a deeply embedded cultural mythology that portrayed Indigenous peoples as an archaic remnant in the path of America’s manifest destiny. The comparison with Jewish identity and experiences served to underscore a peculiar irony; both groups were simultaneously revered and reviled, viewed through the dual lenses of nostalgia and disdain. While Native Americans were romanticized in literature as noble savages by figures like Cooper and Longfellow, they were also seen as impediments to a civilizational agenda. This manipulation of narratives reflected societal contradictions—America’s self-image as a land of freedom was built upon the subjugation of those very groups that were deemed different or lesser. Scholars utilized these racialized ideologies to advocate for various solutions. Some recommended 'civilizing' Native Americans through education and Christianization, suggesting that enlightenment could be imparted and that such efforts would ultimately help them assimilate. Others, steeped in more extreme views, endorsed physical removal to sustain what they perceived as social order and progress. This bifurcated vision stemmed from a broader anxiety about the preservation of a national identity tied closely to European ancestry. As Native American presence compromised that identity, thinkers grappled with how to extinguish what they deemed an existential threat. The actual policies enacted reflected these complex attitudes, leading to a series of tragic acts that reshaped both landscapes and peoples. The Indian Removal Act of 1830, spearheaded by figures such as Andrew Jackson, was a direct manifestation of this racial thinking, resulting in the forced relocation of thousands. Similarly, the ideologies underpinning these decisions foreshadowed later policies during moments of national crisis. As the United States grappled with its identity in terms of race, culture, and belonging, the consequences for marginalized communities were often dire and lasting, setting a precedent for future generations and ongoing struggles for recognition and rights.
