This critique was often framed in terms of progress and civilization, where the British Empire’s self-image relied heavily on the juxtaposition of enlightenment ideals against the reality of its institutions, such as slavery. Prominent figures like Adam Smith and others began to question the economic rationale behind slave economies, positing that a nation’s true wealth was derived from the freedom and productivity of its labour force rather than from the exploitation of enslaved individuals. This shift in perspective not only appealed to economists but also resonated with a burgeoning sense of moral obligation among British citizens who began to view the condition of enslaved people through a humanitarian lens. As more voices from various segments of society—including merchants, abolitionists, and ordinary citizens—converged on this issue, a multifaceted movement emerged. The framework of abolition was shaped by a myriad of influences: the Enlightenment critique of slavery, religious conviction, and economic argumentation combined to create a compelling narrative against the institution. In this environment, grassroots mobilization became essential; pamphlets, petitions, and public meetings spread awareness and galvanised public opinion. The Atlantic world was becoming increasingly interlinked, and the horrors of slavery were no longer isolated incidents but rather topics of widespread discussion, fueled by testimonies and accounts from formerly enslaved individuals. Simultaneously, within Parliament, the divisions among lawmakers regarding the abolition of slavery mirrored the ideological battlegrounds emerging in society at large. While some legislators recoiled from the potential economic ramifications of abolition, fearing that it would destabilize the colonial markets, a growing faction began to advocate passionately for change. Personal stories from the enslaved, such as those recited by Olaudah Equiano, served to humanize the issues at stake, creating powerful emotional appeals that parliamentarians could no longer ignore. Such narratives transcended sheer economic argumentation, appealing directly to the moral conscience of those in power. The interplay of these factors, coupled with changing economic realities—notably the inefficiencies tied to slave labour—further incentivized the re-evaluation of slavery as a viable institution. This evolving discourse did not occur in a vacuum; it was set against the backdrop of global revolutions, notably the American and Haitian revolutions, which showcased the potential for radical change. These monumental uprisings became touchstones for abolitionists, emphasizing both the capability and the agency of the enslaved. Inspired by these instances of self-liberation, advocates in Britain harnessed the revolutionary spirit to challenge the status quo, arguing that freedom was not merely a right reserved for some but an inevitable trend of progress that would sweep across societies. The intersection of moral, economic, and political arguments forged a new landscape in British attitudes toward slavery. Activists found common ground that bridged class divides, uniting disparate groups in their shared conviction that the time for change had arrived, further pressuring Parliament to confront the contradictions inherent in a civilized, modern nation that still upheld such a brutal system.
