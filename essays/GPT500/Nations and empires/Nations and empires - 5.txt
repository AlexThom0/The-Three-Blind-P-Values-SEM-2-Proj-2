The interaction between Jewish communities and their surroundings in the Ottoman Empire was characterized by a relative coexistence that would later become overshadowed by European narratives. While the Empire allowed a certain latitude for cultural and religious expression among its varied subjects, European nationalism propelled Jews to envision a state that was entirely separate from their historical experiences in the Levant. This divergence set the stage for the competing nationalist movements that would emerge in the modern era. The Jews of the Ottoman realms often considered themselves Ottomans first, with their identities intricately woven into the religious and cultural tapestries of the times. They benefitted from the system of millet, which granted various religious communities a degree of autonomy in matters of personal law and community affairs. This environment fostered a unique blend of cultural influences, allowing Ottoman Jews to participate actively in trade, philosophy, and politics, often collaborating with not just their fellow Jews but also Muslims and Christians. Their contributions to the urban economies and multicultural life of cities like Istanbul and Salonica were considerable, complicating any simplistic narratives of oppression or exclusion. In contrast, the rise of modern nationalism in Europe catalyzed a shift in Jewish identity. As antisemitic sentiments solidified in European thought, many Jews began to see the formation of a separate national identity as a necessity for survival. This was a radical departure from the earlier, more integrated existence within the Ottoman framework. The questioning of Jewish identity in Europe revealed deep-seated insecurities about belonging, prompting leaders like Herzl to advocate for an entirely new direction. Yet the cultural memory of the Ottoman past lingered, offering a stark contrast to the hostile environments many Jews now faced in Europe. This historical paradox—where Ottoman tolerance coexisted with European exclusion—shaped the disparate paths that Jewish communities followed. The legacy of the Ottoman Empire continued to inform Arab perceptions of Jews, complicating the narrative of dispossession that would arise in the wake of Zionist aspirations. Arab populations, while caught in their own nationalistic upheaval, often viewed Jewish claims to land through the lens of their own experiences of colonialism and territorial loss. As Jewish settlers arrived in Palestine, they were confronted by a very different reality than the one that had existed under the relatively peaceful coexistence in the Ottoman Empire. Therefore, the intricate dynamics of identity, culture, and loyalty that characterized Ottoman Jewry laid the groundwork for the multifaceted responses to Zionism in the Arab East. In the struggle for both Jewish national aspirations and Arab self-determination, the Ottoman past served as both a reference point and a cautionary tale of how coexistence could unravel into conflict. Understanding this complexity is essential in analyzing the shifting relationships that would define the region's history in the decades to follow.
