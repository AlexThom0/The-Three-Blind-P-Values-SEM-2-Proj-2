They believed that transforming the image of Turks from that of barbarity to one of civility was essential for the nation’s progress and acceptance in the global arena. The reform movement advocated for Westernization not only in government and military practices but also in education, culture, and social norms. An essential component of this transformation involved adopting Western legal frameworks and practices, which were seen as markers of a civilised society. Schools began to emphasize modern curricula, teaching science and foreign languages, while promoting secularism as a way to distance Turkish identity from the perceived barbarism of the Ottoman era. This ambitious agenda resulted in sweeping changes in numerous facets of Turkish life. The abolition of the caliphate and the introduction of civil law made significant strides toward modern governance and women's rights. Women were granted the right to vote and participate in public life, roles that had previously been largely reserved for men in traditional Ottoman society. These reforms served as an assertion of progressiveness intended to counteract the 'Terrible Turk' image. The new narrative aimed to construct a more favorable international perception of Turkey as a nation rooted in moderation and enlightenment rather than one steeped in brutality. Cultural production also played a pivotal role in this narrative shift. Turkish artists, writers, and intellectuals began to create works that celebrated the newfound secular approach and embraced Western aesthetics, thereby redefining Turkish identity through the lens of modernism. Literature, theater, and visual arts flourished under the auspices of this reformist zeal, as they adopted styles and themes that resonated more profoundly with European and American tastes. This cultural renaissance aimed not only to reshape the Turkish self-image but also to forge connections that transcended historical animosities between the East and West. Moreover, the shift in educational policies reflected an overarching desire to cultivate a generation of Turks who were literate, modern, and engaged with global ideals. The establishment of language reforms catered to this goal, as Turkish nationalists promoted a simplified and purged version of the language that would serve as a unifying force for the newly formed republic. The emphasis on a singular national language symbolised a break from the diverse linguistic landscapes of the Ottoman Empire, reinforcing the idea of a cohesive national identity. Yet, such reforms were not simply about internal mechanics; they were also a strategic response to external perceptions that had historically marginalized, if not vilified, Turkish people. By repositioning their cultural narratives and societal structures, Turkish reformers sought to assert that their roots lay not in the bloody legacy of the past but in a commitment to enlightenment principles. The transformation of the Turkish identity was deeply intertwined with a rejection of historical images that had long depicted Turks as an aberration of civilization. In this new light, Turkish modernisers envisioned a society that could engage with the world, not as a 'Terrible Turk,' but as a proud member of the global community.
