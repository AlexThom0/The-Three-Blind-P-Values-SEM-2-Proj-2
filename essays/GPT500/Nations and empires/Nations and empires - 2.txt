Bakunin's fascination with the potential of the South Caucasus as a conduit for tropical products was deeply rooted in the broader imperial ambitions of Russia. The cultivation of exotic plants became emblematic of a much larger project aimed at showcasing Russia’s territorial reach and agricultural prowess. This fixation on tropical goods also mirrored the nation’s desire for modernity and progress, placing it in a competitive stance against other imperial powers. As various agricultural experiments unfolded, bureaucrats and agronomists began to see the South Caucasus not simply as a frontier, but as a prime area for implementing the latest innovations in agronomy that were emerging from Europe and beyond. The intersection of local knowledge and scientific inquiry led to a remarkable fusion, where traditional farming practices met with newly introduced methodologies. As agricultural tours and studies took place, local farmers became reluctant participants in the process of empire-building, often caught between their ancestral agricultural traditions and the demands of budding imperial interests. While the administrators and scientists aboard their missions endeavored to catalog and classify the region’s plant life, local cultivators offered them insights into the soil's nuances and the climate's realities that could not be gleaned from texts alone. This enriched perspective shaped how the empire envisioned its agricultural landscape, lending credence to the idea that the South Caucasus could emerge as a central hub for a range of tropical commodities, serving both local consumption and export requirements. Furthermore, the narrative surrounding these developments was laced with a romanticized view of the region, as the varied topography and climate were likened to far-off paradises that were typically associated with colonial visions. The ambition was not merely to extract resources but to create a space that not only reflected the grandeur of the Russian Empire but also the exoticized tropics that many metropolitan elites hoped to embrace. The imagery of lush plantations, flourishing spice gardens, and coconut palms became intertwined with the imperial ethos, shaping public perceptions and national aspirations. Demonstrating a blend of agricultural ingenuity and imperial zeal, Bakunin and his contemporaries crafted visions that extended beyond agronomic interest, reaching into the realms of national identity and cultural exchange. Amidst this backdrop of agricultural transformations emerged a paradox: while the empire sought to impose its structures upon a diverse region, it simultaneously became influenced by the very traditions it aimed to supersede. The South Caucasus evolved into a site of hybrid identity, representing both the struggle for imperial dominance and the resilience of local practices. Against this tapestry of ambition and adaptation, educational institutions and agricultural societies also began to play a crucial role. They facilitated the dissemination of knowledge and popularized the cultivation of tropical crops, striving to align the interests of local populations with the imperial agenda. Through workshops and demonstrations, Moscovite agricultural experts traveled southward, promoting techniques that would ultimately reshape the culinary and economic landscape of the region. These interactions underlined the complex interplay between local agency and imperial objectives, paving the way for an increasingly interconnected relationship between the South Caucasus and the broader imperial enterprise. Such dynamics rendered the region not merely a passive recipient of imperial dictates but an active participant in the negotiation of its own agricultural destiny.
