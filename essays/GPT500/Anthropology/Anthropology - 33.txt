I wanted to create something that would honor the stories and traditions of our people, something that would resonate with our ancestral spirits and invoke the essence of our culture. As I listened, I realized that Isaac’s craft went beyond mere artistry; it was an act of reverence, a way to preserve and transmit both personal and collective memory through the medium of wood. Each stroke of his chisel was not just a physical alteration of the material, but a dialogue with the past, a negotiation between the living and the spirit world. He spoke of the importance of understanding the materials he used — the types of wood, their textures and grains — and how these choices were imbued with symbolism in Tanimbarese culture. Isaac explained how certain woods were favored for specific types of carvings, particularly those that portrayed deities, figures from mythology, or ceremonial objects. He taught me how to listen to the wood, to feel its energy and rhythm, as if it had its own voice waiting to be heard. This deep connection to the material was fundamental to the respect for adat. Each carving, he noted, was governed by an unspoken covenant between the artist, the materials, and the traditions that dictated their use. The artistic process unfolded like a ritual, with every tool bearing a weight of significance, ensuring that each creation served a purpose beyond decoration. Adat infused not only the creation but also the interpretation of art within the community. For the Tanimbarese, a sculpture was often a vessel for stories, a means to communicate ideas and emotions that transcended the spoken word. The act of carving was imbued with spiritual significance, a practice rooted in the belief that the ancestors were present during the creative process. Isaac spoke reverently of the ancestors, whom he believed guided artisans in “finding the shape that wishes to be born,” a phrase that resonated deeply with me. As we continued our conversation, I understood that my presence in Isaac’s workshop was not incidental; it was a part of a broader narrative of cultural transmission, rooted in reciprocity and respect for tradition. Through this lens, my role evolved from that of a mere observer to one of an engaged participant in the shared responsibility of honoring the past while contributing to the future. Every artist, every piece created, sat within a web of relationships that linked the community to its heritage, generations past and future intertwined in the fabric of existence. This notion of connectedness was palpable in Isaac’s movements, his focused concentration as he carved the wood. I realized that understanding adat meant recognizing the profound weight that ritual law carried — both as guidance to protect the community and as a framework to foster creativity and expression. Isaac’s vision revealed a world where art could serve as an embodiment of life itself, shaping the social landscape in ways that echoed through time and space, making tangible the values and beliefs that defined the very essence of Tanimbarese identity. In this way, the creation was as much a celebration as it was a preservation of a rich cultural legacy, one in which I found myself slowly awakening.
