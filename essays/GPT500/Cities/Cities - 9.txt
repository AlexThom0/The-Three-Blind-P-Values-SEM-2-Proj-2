While the intention behind such representations may be to underline the interconnectedness of urban life with the natural world, they often simplify the intricate relationships between humans and other species. This reductionist view overlooks the various ways in which nonhuman elements not only adapt to urban environments but actively reshape them. For example, cities are increasingly witnessing the rise of urban wildlife, with species like foxes, raccoons, and peregrine falcons finding niches within human infrastructure. These creatures are not merely accidental byproducts of urbanization; they possess their own agency and influence on the urban ecosystem, challenging the notion of a strictly human-dominated environment. This dynamic interactions leads to a reevaluation of how we conceptualize urban spaces, urging us to consider these environments as shared and co-created rather than exclusively designed by humans. An essential aspect of this discourse is the growing recognition of ecological urbanism which prioritizes biodiversity while fully acknowledging the contributions of nonhuman actors in shaping our spaces. This approach calls for policies that integrate green architecture, sustainable design, and urban planning that supports diverse habitats within cities. The presence of green roofs, urban forests, and wildlife corridors illustrates efforts to integrate nature into the urban fabric rather than relegating it to the periphery. Such strategies endeavour to transcend the binary of human and nonhuman, fostering a multispecies perspective that embraces the need for coexistence. Importantly, this shift urges us to listen to the voices of nonhuman actors—acknowledging their roles and adapting our practices accordingly. Further complicating the narrative is the fact that urban landscapes have always been sites of entanglement between nature and culture. The evolution of public spaces shows how human interventions have often resulted in unforeseen ecological consequences, transforming our understanding of what it means to 'manage' urban environments. The story of urban parks, for instance, is one of intentional design overshadowed by uncontrolled biological proliferation. In some cases, invasive species can obscure original landscapes, highlighting the irony embedded within our attempts to curate nature in cities. Such instances provide a stark reminder that urban spaces are dynamic and often unpredictable, where human authority can be contested by the resilience and adaptability of other species. Moreover, framing urbanity exclusively in terms of human agency may inadvertently perpetuate an anthropocentric worldview that dismisses the agency of other beings. To move towards a more inclusive understanding of urban spaces, it is crucial to analyze the cultural narratives that have historically defined them. Art, literature, and media can serve as platforms for acknowledging the diverse experiences and lifeways of other species that inhabit these spaces alongside us. They can illuminate the complexities of these relationships and inspire new pathways for coexistence. By engaging with the perspectives of nonhuman inhabitants, we can begin to dismantle the narrative that positions humanity as the sole architect of urban life. In doing so, we open up possibilities for reimagining our cities as ecosystems of interdependence rather than mere backdrops for human activity.
