This intersectional approach underscores how planning decisions not only shape landscapes but also impact the lives of the people who inhabit them. As I delved deeper into the readings required for my coursework, I began to realize that these issues extend beyond mere historical analysis; they present urgent ethical implications for current and future urban planners. The implications of Berman's work resonate profoundly within the context of contemporary urban challenges, where the principles of equity and justice are frequently sidelined in favor of profit. This neglect manifests in various dimensions, such as the displacement of long-standing communities in favor of real estate development designed to cater to affluent newcomers. The narrative of progress often celebrated in planning discourses overlooks the voices of those systematically excluded. This dynamic displays a troubling trend: the more urban spaces are viewed as commodities, the more they seem to detach from the real human experiences they house. Consequently, developments are often justified under a guise of revitalization while perpetuating cycles of inequality, a theme echoed in countless case studies throughout urban studies literature. One cannot ignore the mounting evidence that highlights a direct correlation between urban design choices and health outcomes in these disenfranchised neighborhoods. For instance, the placement of highways through low-income areas not only divides communities but also increases exposure to toxic air pollutants. This kind of environmental racism serves as a stark reminder of the historical neglect faced by these populations, cementing existing disparities rather than alleviating them. Consequently, emerging planners must critically interrogate the relationship between infrastructure and the lived experiences of those most impacted by it. As I began my studies, I was struck by how seldom the curriculum addressed these pressing socio-environmental realities. The focus tended to remain on technical aspects of planning, with limited engagement with the ethical dimensions that should accompany such work. Instructors rarely facilitated discussions about the implications of decisions made in planning rooms far removed from the neighborhoods they affect. This gap in education highlighted an urgent need for a more holistic, justice-oriented approach to urban planning, where the voices of marginalized communities are not just heard but actively integrated into decision-making processes. Moreover, the push for sustainability often fails to connect with the need for social equity. Sustainable development is too frequently conceptualized in isolation, neglecting the fact that ecological crises and social injustices are interlinked. To address one without the other risks perpetuating forms of exclusion that undermine the very resilience we seek to build. Seeking alternatives to this paradigm, there is a clear demand for planners who are equipped to confront the intersections of environmental degradation and social inequality. The journey of understanding these complexities has been both enlightening and disheartening, revealing patterns that repeat across cities. It has reinforced my conviction that effective urban planning cannot simply revolve around technical proficiency; it requires a deep commitment to justice and an unwavering refusal to disregard the needs of the most vulnerable. Engaging with this material on a personal level has fostered a relentless pursuit of how planning can serve as a transformative tool rather than an instrument of oppression.
