Fictional discourse, unlike lying, depends on a shared understanding between the author and the audience regarding the nature of the text. Readers engage with fiction under the assumption that they are entering a realm of imaginative license where the represented world operates under its own set of rules. This engagement is informed by cultural norms and conventions that categorize works as fictional, setting them apart from assertions made in everyday language that are intended to convey truth. In this light, the relationship between fiction and the literal meaning of the words used becomes more nuanced. For instance, when an author describes a dragon soaring through the sky, the reader does not attempt to reframe this statement into a factual claim about flying reptiles; instead, the reader suspends disbelief, momentarily embracing the impossibility of the scenario. This suspension does not confer truth upon the narrative but highlights the collaborative nature of fiction that is distinctly absent from deception. While a liar constructs their narrative with the deliberate intent to mislead, the fiction writer crafts a tapestry of possibility, inviting readers to explore worlds that operate beyond the constraints of factual reality. Additionally, fictional narratives often draw upon established tropes, archetypes, and motifs, further signaling to readers the interpretive lens through which the text should be approached. This shared literary understanding creates an implicit contract, whereby the reader recognizes the author's intention to entertain or provoke thought rather than to deceive or misrepresent. The constructed nature of these narratives makes them tools for exploring complex themes, revealing truths about the human condition, and offering insight into subjective experiences. Thus, the fiction author's relationship with their audience is fundamentally collaborative, engaged in a dance of meaning-making rather than an act of manipulation or deceit. In contrast, the structures of lying lean heavily on violation—an intentional breach of trust that leads to an erosion of social bonds. In instances of deception, the liar is aware of the reality they obscure; they exist in opposition to the audience's understanding. This awareness underpins the act of lying, resulting in a different kind of interpersonal dynamic, one colored by distrust and revelation. When lies proliferate, they engender a fracturing of communication, whereas fiction fosters a communal exploration of imagination. Even within the context of mixed-authority texts, where fiction interweaves with truth—a biography that incorporates creative elements, for instance—the distinction remains salient. Readers are often alerted to the presence of these mixed modalities through cues. These cues can range from authorial prefaces that acknowledge creative liberty to stylistic shifts that indicate a departure from strict factuality. Such structures signal to the audience that the journey through the text requires not just knowledge, but a readiness to engage with varied levels of truth and invention.
