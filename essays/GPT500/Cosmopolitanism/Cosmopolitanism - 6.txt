There is a compelling irony at play when discussing the fervor for global governance in the nuclear age. The very mechanisms introduced to mitigate risk could inadvertently become catalysts for instability. Consider how nations would perceive ceding their nuclear capabilities to a centralized authority. For many, such a concession would prompt fears of domination—where a singular body, detached from the complexities of regional politics, wields unprecedented control over a nation's fate. This fear could ignite nationalistic sentiments, prompting countries to resist compliance and explore their own paths to nuclear capability as a means of self-defense. The delicate balance of power often hinges on mutual understanding and respect for sovereignty; stripping nations of their nuclear deterrents could undermine that balance and foster an atmosphere of distrust. Diplomatic efforts might quickly devolve into chaos if countries feel cornered, leading to a resurgence of arms proliferation as states scramble to protect their interests. Moreover, the legitimacy of any global institution assigned the responsibility of overseeing nuclear arsenals would be continuously questioned. In a world characterized by divergent political ideologies and conflicting national interests, who gets to decide the criteria for approval of nuclear strikes? The potential for bias or perceived partiality would breed resentment and may even justify unilateral military actions taken in self-defense. The reaction could mirror past dilemmas presented during the Cold War; nations might feel a pressing need to act preemptively rather than wait for a delay in bureaucratic proceedings, effectively transforming the institution meant to safeguard peace into a catalyst for preemptive strikes. The establishment of such a global authority could thus inadvertently prompt a new arms race, where nations feel compelled to enhance their military capabilities in anticipation of an inability to rely on a collective system. Counterproductive arms development could lead to a paradox: states convinced that they must protect themselves from the very framework that was intended to ensure their safety. As alternative defense strategies are reevaluated, conventional military might could rise in prominence, undermining the goal of disarmament altogether. This situation is compounded by the varied cultural attitudes toward nuclear weapons, with some nations viewing them as symbols of prestige and sovereignty rather than existential threats. The perception of nuclear might as a safeguard against foreign intervention complicates the narrative in which nations commit to disarmament. For example, countries with deep-seated historical grievances might view relinquishing their nuclear arsenal as a surrender of their agency, while others could perceive it as a prudent step towards global stability. In such a fractured landscape, devoid of trust in a centralized authority's commitment to fairness, the proposed vision of a nuclear-free world risks devolving into a fight for survival amidst escalating tensions. Ultimately, any effort to legislate nuclear disarmament must account for the intricate interplay of power, identity, and security that defines the global landscape, lest it mimic past attempts at collective security bound for failure due to the very divisions they sought to bridge.
