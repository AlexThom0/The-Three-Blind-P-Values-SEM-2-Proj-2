Both psychoanalysis and intelligence-testing forged new pathways in the understanding of child development, but their approaches led to markedly different implications for the treatment and categorization of conditions like autism. While psychoanalysis regarded behaviors associated with autism as potential reflections of unresolved inner conflicts, intelligence-testing began to categorize these behaviors as deviations from a normative standard of cognitive ability. This divergence reveals the tension between subjective experiences and objective measurements—a theme that would continue to play a crucial role in the study of developmental disorders. As the 1940s approached, entrenched beliefs about child behavior increasingly encountered the pragmatic concerns of a society eager to apply scientific principles in the quest for efficiency and order. The rise of psychology as a discipline sought not only to illuminate individual paths but also to impose order on the multi-faceted experiences of childhood. In practice, educational systems began to incorporate intelligence tests which, despite claims of impartiality, often perpetuated social biases. As a result, children deemed “intellectually deficient” faced institutionalization or marginalization, while those who conformed to expected developmental benchmarks were celebrated and nurtured. This systematic categorization often overlooked the nuanced realities of child development, particularly as they pertained to atypical behaviors. Children on the autism spectrum exhibited traits that fell outside the rigid frameworks established by intelligence tests, which were designed to uphold a singular narrative of success based on cognitive performance. In effect, the medical model posited by intelligence-testing implicitly affected parental and societal expectations about giftedness versus disability. Simultaneously, the psychoanalytic perspective, with its emphasis on individual narrative and emotional depth, offered an alternative yet equally problematic interpretation, suggesting that a child’s struggles were inherent to complex emotional conflicts rather than innate neurological variations. The coalescing ideologies around autism began obtaining distinct narratives within academic and clinical discourse; the struggle to identify precise diagnostic criteria became evident. The implications of these definitions had profound consequences, leading to division not only within the psychological community but also among families and advocates for children with autism. As various stakeholders sought a more unified understanding, competing theories began to arise, each vying for recognition and legitimacy. These included biological, environmental, and psychological explanations that laid the groundwork for increasingly interdisciplinary approaches to understanding autism. Both perspectives—psychoanalytic and psychometric—were not merely academic constructs but influenced the lived experiences of children and their families. Institutions, educators, and clinicians utilized these frameworks to guide their interactions with children labeled as “different,” yet the real and tangible experiences of those children too often became obscured or misrepresented in the ensuing discussions. Understanding autism, therefore, required navigating a complex interplay of scientific ambition and human reality, as the very term evolved and expanded in meaning. This evolving discussion would ultimately set the stage for more inclusive theories and practices in the realm of developmental psychology.
