This shift was marked by a departure from explanations rooted in purpose and intention. The focus transitioned to observable phenomena, where the workings of the universe were explained through physical laws and mathematical equations rather than teleological musings. Figures such as Galileo and Newton radically redefined the understanding of motion and matter, stripping away the metaphysical layers that had characterized earlier philosophical thought. This new framework posited that nature could be likened to a vast machine, operating under consistent laws, rather than a living organism with inherent purposes and goals. As the wheel of scientific advancement turned, concepts from Aristotelian thought—like final causes—were relegated to the margins of academic inquiry. Instead of seeking the why behind natural events, attention shifted to the how, concentrating on mechanisms and tangible interactions. For instance, while Aristotle would have approached the motion of celestial bodies with an inquiry into their purpose within the cosmos, Newton's laws rendered such questions obsolete, emphasizing instead the forces at play, described mathematically. The gravitational pull that governed the planets’ orbits became an explanation detached from any innate purpose, thus mechanizing the cosmos in a profound way. Moreover, this change was accompanied by the emergence of a new philosophy that embraced materialism and empiricism, laying the groundwork for modern scientific disciplines. The material world became the only focus of inquiry, which had significant implications for notions of life, existence, and purpose. Without the lens of purpose, the natural world was understood purely in terms of its components and their interactions. The integration of mathematics into the sciences also played a crucial role in this transformation. It allowed for predictions and descriptions of natural phenomena that were independently verifiable, thereby reinforcing the mechanistic view. However, this paradigm shift did not go unchallenged. Early critics of the new science often attempted to reconcile these empirical notions with a more teleological approach. Some sought to argue that, while the universe operated according to mechanical principles, there still lay a possibility for higher purposes or meanings inherent within the cosmos. Yet, as the fledgling disciplines matured, the challenge became increasingly difficult amidst the burgeoning success of mechanistic explanations. As the Enlightenment progressed into the modern era, philosophers like Descartes and later, Kant, found themselves grappling with the implications of an increasingly impersonal universe. The idea that nature functioned mechanically led to philosophical inquiries surrounding the consciousness and agency of human beings. If the universe were just a machine, where did that leave humanity's place in it, and what of the notion of free will? These questions became central as scientific thought began to influence not only the physical but also the philosophical dimensions of human existence, reshaping the discourse on life and agency in ways that Aristotle and Plato might never have anticipated.
