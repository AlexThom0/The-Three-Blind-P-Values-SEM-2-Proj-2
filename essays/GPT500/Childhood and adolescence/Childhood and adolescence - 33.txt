Many Lithuanians felt the need to forge a distinct path that would not only reclaim their artistic heritage but also redefine it within a contemporary framework. As Laužikaitė immersed herself in the cultural landscape, she recognized that the remnants of Soviet-era policies still influenced current perceptions and practices. The narrative of recovery was often overshadowed by hesitation and fear, leading many to gravitate towards established norms instead of exploring innovative ideas. This hesitation manifested in the arts through a reluctance to collaborate and share resources, with many artists working in silos rather than pooling their strengths for collective benefit. Furthermore, the fragmentation of the cultural sector mirrored wider societal issues. There was a pervasive tendency among various stakeholders—artists, educators, and policymakers—to engage in parallel discussions without meaningful intersections. Laužikaitė's experience underscored the necessity for a cohesive dialogue that transcended disciplinary boundaries. She began to envision a network, a platform where creative minds could congregate, support one another, and work towards a unified goal of cultural revitalization. This vision aimed not only to bridge gaps but also to foster an environment where experimentation was encouraged, and new ideas could flourish without the weight of legacy constraints. To begin implementing this vision, Laužikaitė organized a series of workshops aimed at bringing together artists from different mediums, as well as educators and cultural activists. The initial response was lukewarm; many participants expressed skepticism, rooted in the belief that their voices would remain unheard in the broader conversation. Recognizing this skepticism, Laužikaitė made it a priority to highlight success stories from both Lithuania and abroad—instances where collaboration led to tangible advancements in the arts and cultural sectors. She understood that by showcasing these examples, she could help shift the narrative from a focus on isolation to one of communal achievement. As these workshops progressed, something remarkable began to unfold. Participants who initially viewed each other as competitors started to recognize shared challenges and strengths. Collaborations blossomed, leading to projects that combined various forms of art, with themes that resonated deeply with the missed opportunities of the past while addressing contemporary issues head-on. The power of collective creativity began to dismantle decades of siloed thinking, replacing it with an eagerness to innovate and disrupt the status quo. This cultural awakening was not just about reclaiming lost narratives but also about reimagining what Lithuanian culture could embody moving forward. Laužikaitė’s passion and dedication quickly transformed not just the workshops but the atmosphere surrounding the ongoing dialogue in Lithuania. Conversations became more vibrant, filled with infectious enthusiasm and a newfound belief in the potential of collaboration. As barriers of distrust and reluctance began to diminish, a flourishing communal identity took shape, illustrating how the legacy of the past need not dictate the possibilities of the future.
