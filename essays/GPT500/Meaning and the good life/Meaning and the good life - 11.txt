This enabled a select portion of society to detach from the immediate struggle for survival and instead engage with abstract concepts of individuality and personal liberty. The wealth derived from exploitative practices was not merely a means to accumulate capital; it served as the foundation for the philosophical musings that would later frame the narratives surrounding democracy and citizenship. Colonialism created a starkly divided world where the privileges afforded to the few were sustained by the subjugation of the many, resulting in a deeply embedded hierarchy that continues to shape social structures today. Thus, as Ghosh points out, the benefits of modernity are unevenly distributed, intimately tied to historical injustices that fuel environmental degradation and climate inequity. The very systems that inform our understanding of rights and freedoms were built on the backs of those who were denied basic human dignity. This paradox raises pressing questions about the sustainability of current lifestyles and our collective responsibility toward those relegated to the margins of this global narrative. Contemplating this dynamic, it becomes apparent that acknowledging the legacies of colonialism is essential for an honest reckoning with present-day crises, particularly climate change. The disastrous consequences of colonial exploitation are not just historical footnotes; they manifest in the socio-economic vulnerabilities faced by the Global South, where environmental degradation and climate vulnerability intersect. This intersectionality forces a reconsideration of environmental justice, which must include a direct dialogue with the historical context that produced and perpetuates inequalities. The urgency of climate action cannot be divorced from these colonial legacies, as the call to address climate change is, at its core, a call to address the disparities fostered through centuries of exploitation. As marginalized communities around the globe face the consequences of climate change—rising sea levels, droughts, and extreme weather events—the historical roots of these challenges become increasingly stark. The decision to externalize the costs of industrialization and environmental degradation onto the most vulnerable populations underscores the moral failing at the heart of contemporary climate discussions. In seeking solutions to these expansive challenges, we must reframe the narrative surrounding modernity itself. It should cultivate an awareness that the pursuit of profit cannot be isolated from the human costs often borne by communities in the Global South. Global solidarity must emerge as essential if we are to forge pathways toward equitable climate resilience and sustainable practices that include all voices. Confronting this history is not merely an academic exercise but a foundational step toward making the principles of equity, sustainability, and justice a lived reality for everyone. Only by dismantling the oppressive structures that underpin both past and present can we hope to create a rigorous framework that fosters true progress and inclusion in the fight for a livable planet.
