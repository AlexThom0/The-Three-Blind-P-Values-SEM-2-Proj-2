Living without the privileges of a full professorship, his intellectual contributions continued to resonate within the philosophical community, albeit from a distance. His focus shifted towards writing and correspondence, employing letters as a means to maintain connections with past students and contemporaries. This new phase of his life underscored a poignant dichotomy: while he was isolated from the formal academic structure that had defined his earlier years, he remained a pivotal figure in the discourse on philosophy and psychology. His theories, particularly on intentionality, gained traction, prompting debates that transcended the geographical and institutional boundaries he faced. The lack of academic authority did not diminish his authority as a thinker; instead, it highlighted the resilience required to continue engaging with philosophical questions amid personal and professional adversity. Brentano's writings from this period reflect a deep introspection, often addressing the implications of his earlier theories in light of his changing circumstances. He examined the concepts of perception and judgment more closely, leading to nuanced arguments about the nature of consciousness. His isolation proved to be a crucible for refining his thoughts, generating ideas that would later influence not only his students but also the wider philosophical community. This intellectual resilience attracted a new generation of thinkers who sought him out, eager to learn from his wisdom, even if in an informal context. Their visits enabled Brentano to maintain a sense of purpose and engagement, emphasizing the importance of dialogue and mentorship, even outside traditional academic settings. Additionally, the interplay of his personal life and philosophical inquiry served to humanize his work. Each correspondence with students became a vessel for transmitting not just ideas, but a rich tapestry of his life experiences—grief after his wife’s death, reflections on identity and displacement, and a commitment to pursuing truth despite the limitations placed upon him. This emotional depth added layers to his philosophical positions, resonating with a broader audience dealing with their own existential questions in rapidly changing social landscapes. Brentano also witnessed the intellectual ferment surrounding him in Florence, where artists, literary figures, and fellow philosophers congregated, each grappling with questions of modernity. The city’s vibrant cultural milieu provided fertile ground for discussions on ethics and aesthetics, themes he had long explored yet now approached with a renewed urgency. His interactions in this dynamic environment fueled his reflections on the morality inherent in human consciousness, and he began drawing connections between personal experience and philosophical inquiry in ways that were previously unarticulated. Therefore, while Brentano's formal academic power diminished, his intellectual legacy grew more robust as he turned outward, engaging with the world and nurturing the minds of future generations even in his later years. Ultimately, this era of his life exemplified the notion that philosophical inquiry is not merely an academic endeavor but a deeply personal journey, shaped by life’s trials and transformations.
