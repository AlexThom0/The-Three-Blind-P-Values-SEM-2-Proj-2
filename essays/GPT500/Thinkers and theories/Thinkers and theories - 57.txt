This exclusion was not merely about participation but rather about the characterization of women's intellectual capabilities. The disdain highlighted in the critiques of the Bergsoniennes mirrors historical attitudes that have perpetuated the stereotype of women as frivolous or incapable of serious thought. Scholars such as Simone de Beauvoir and later, feminist theorists, would argue that these perceptions were rooted in a patriarchal ideology that sought to maintain control over the public and private spheres, positioning women as ornamental rather than substantive participants in intellectual life. As the movement surrounding Bergson gained traction, it invoked both admiration and scrutiny, revealing a tension between emerging feminist thought and entrenched societal views. Women flocked to Bergson not only as a reaction to the intellectual climate but also as a means to assert their place within it. They found in his philosophical explorations a voice that resonated with their experiences, challenging the prevailing notion that intellect was inherently masculine. Critiques of the female fascination with Bergson's philosophies often lacked a nuanced understanding of the complex realities facing women who sought knowledge at a time when their pursuits were belittled. Instead of dismissing this intellectual engagement as mere social climbing, it is vital to recognize the cultural shifts taking place. The popularity of Bergson among women signified a break from traditional confines and indicated a desire for a broader conversation that transcended established gender roles. By embracing philosophical thought and pursuing advanced studies, these women were participating in a quiet revolution, reshaping not only their own identities but also the narrative surrounding female scholarship. While the male-dominated intellectual community may have viewed these women’s ventures as frivolous, the act of attending lectures and engaging with complex ideas was, in itself, a form of resistance against the societal norms designed to suppress feminine agency. The women devoted to Bergson’s ideas were not merely exchanging social pleasantries; they were grappling with profound questions about life, existence, and the nature of consciousness—subjects that had long been relegated to the shadows of the intellect. This dynamic serves as a reflection of the historical struggle many women faced in claiming their intellectual space, often fighting against the very structures that sought to marginalize them. In the context of Bergson’s philosophy, time and consciousness became more than abstract concepts; they were essential to understanding the human experience, especially for those women who, through their participation, carved out room for new forms of expression and thought. This reclamation of intellectual agency was both an act of personal empowerment and a broader challenge to the established order, showcasing the capability and complexity of women’s contributions to philosophical discourse. Their engagement with philosophy was not just about prestige; it was about fundamentally redefining their relationship with knowledge and authority in a world that had long sought to limit their voices.
