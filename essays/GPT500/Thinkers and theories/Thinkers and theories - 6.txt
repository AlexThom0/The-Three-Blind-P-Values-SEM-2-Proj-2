This oversight can be traced to various factors, including issues surrounding the recognition of women's contributions to philosophy during the late 19th century. The societal biases of the time clearly undervalued women's intellectual capabilities, leading to a systematic underrepresentation of their work in philosophical discourse. As a result, texts authored by Jones and her contemporaries were often relegated to obscurity, despite their rigorous arguments and innovative ideas. The marginalization of women's voices in philosophy not only stifled their contributions but also shaped the historical narrative in ways that would take decades to rectify. Re-examining Jones's ideas alongside Frege's reveals not only the depth of her thought but also highlights how her perspectives remain relevant today. Her nuanced approach to language and meaning foreshadows later developments in semantics and underscores the importance of considering diverse viewpoints in philosophical research. Moreover, the distinction she draws between determination and denomination offers a framework that could enrich contemporary discussions on the nature of meaning, particularly as the field grapples with the implications of context and speaker intent. The lack of recognition for philosophers like Jones serves as a reminder of the preconceived notions that often dictate which ideas gain traction in intellectual circles. An alternative path, where women's contributions were equally acknowledged, might have led to a more inclusive understanding of philosophical inquiry. Consequently, the legacy of such exclusion highlights the need for modern scholars to actively seek out and integrate these overlooked voices, ensuring that future philosophical landscapes reflect a broader array of perspectives. As discussions about marginalization in academia continue to evolve, revisiting Jones's work becomes ever more pertinent. It invites us to explore how societal structures influence not just the reception of ideas but the very formation of those ideas themselves. The engagement with historical figures like Jones can illuminate the biases inherent in the philosophical canon and promote a richer, more inclusive dialogue on meaning and language.
