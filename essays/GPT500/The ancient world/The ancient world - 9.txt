Carlyle declared that the "war of freedom" was a war of white men against black men, using historical precedents established by Roman expansionism to fortify his arguments. The appropriation of Roman ideals served not merely as a political tool but also as a moral shield, allowing proponents of slavery to cloak their oppression in the glowing mantle of ancient valor and civilizational progress. This phenomenon reveals a critical interplay between historical interpretation and contemporary ideology, wherein the triumphal myths of Rome were recontextualized to serve a distinctly modern agenda. Advocates of white supremacy drew parallels between the Roman elite’s purported benevolence towards conquered peoples and their own colonial ambitions, suggesting that their subjugation was a benevolent enterprise, akin to Rome's civilizing mission. Such perspectives not only reflect a misinterpretation of history but also lay bare the deep-seated fears and aspirations that animated 19th-century American society. The allusions to a glorious Roman past inadvertently illuminate the fractures within the American psyche, as the very ideals that inspired revolutionary zeal were simultaneously employed to justify the exclusion and dehumanization of large segments of the population. This selective engagement with classical texts demonstrates a deliberate manipulation of historical narratives to create a vision of society that is both aspirational and exclusionary. As thinkers like Frederick Douglass later contended, the purported “lessons” of Roman history were often contorted to obscure the realities of oppression and inequality. While the founding fathers lauded the democratic ideals of Rome, they often overlooked the empire’s reliance on chattel slavery and military conquests. This inconsistency reflects a broader tendency in American thought where the veneration of classical antiquity coexists with a persistent disregard for its more uncomfortable truths. The creation of a sanitized narrative around Rome allowed figures of authority to champion liberty while simultaneously enacting policies that confined it to a privileged few. Such contradictions are foundational in understanding the ideological underpinnings of American nationalism that arose in the wake of independence, particularly as they relate to race and power. Moreover, the legacy of this appropriation continues to manifest in contemporary discourses, where fragments of classical rhetoric are wielded to both challenge and reaffirm dominant social hierarchies. As the 19th century progressed, the use of Roman rhetoric by various factions exemplified an ongoing dialogue around freedom, authority, and race, complicating the narrative of progress that has long characterized American exceptionalism. The complexities of this dialogue remind us that history is not merely a backdrop against which contemporary discussions occur, but a living, breathing force that shapes our understandings of justice and identity. As this intricate tapestry of history unfolds, it becomes evident that the ways in which we engage with the past must be scrutinized with both clarity and critical awareness.
