One day, during one of these frantic searches, I encountered an elder from my husband’s community. His serene countenance and steady presence momentarily tempered my anxiety. I mustered the courage to ask him why the neighbours felt so compelled to take my son without informing me. His response was layered with cultural nuance, offering insights that I had overlooked in my initial bewilderment. He explained that the act of communal parenting among the Runa is not merely a tradition, but a philosophy that celebrates the interconnectedness of the community. Each child belongs not only to their immediate family but is regarded as a shared responsibility of everyone within the village. This perspective opened a new understanding for me. The disconnect I had experienced—this clash between my Western individualism and the collective ethos of my husband's culture—became more evident. As I took in his words, I felt a profound sense of admiration for the way this community supported one another. Yet, even with this new understanding, the pang of anxiety lingered whenever my son was out of sight. I recognized the tension between my ingrained protective instincts and the cultural practices that embraced a broader sense of communal safety and care. Children, I learned, were seen as a blessing for everyone; thus, their well-being and upbringing extended beyond the immediate family circle, tightening the fabric of relationship and responsibility among the villagers. However, as enriching as these cultural practices were, they also evoked a feeling of loss over the autonomy I once had as a parent. This deep-seated need to protect my son at all costs often felt at odds with the Runa's trust in their social structure. Each interaction with the neighbours came with undertones of bewilderment, as they seemed unbothered by my stress, viewing their actions as natural. I often found myself wondering if this alien perception was rooted in their undeniable faith in community or simply their indifference to a mother’s anxiety. When the neighbours would return my son, he would often be giggling, completely unbothered by my turmoil, as if sharing those moments with them were a normal extension of his experiences. Nevertheless, that laughter cut through my worries, intertwining joy with fear in a perplexing way. I began to gauge their contentment in being involved, contrasting sharply with my inner turmoil. The thought that I was sacrificing a level of control—a parent’s innate desire—yet witnessing my child's happiness complicated my emotional landscape. It left me in a constant state of ambivalence, a feeling that I was both gaining insight and losing ground in the way I understood motherhood.
