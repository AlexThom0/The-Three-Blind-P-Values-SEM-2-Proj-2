Healthy children are a coveted commodity in society, and the exchange of children reflects not only the desperation of poverty but also the societal values placed on offspring. Despite the flaws in this transactional view of children, the reality is that families must prioritize survival above emotional attachment. In a world where resources are scarce, the notion of family expands beyond traditional bounds, allowing for a community-driven approach in the raising of children, albeit one that often views them as mere assets. It is essential, then, to consider the implications of such a mindset on broader social structures. By treating children as commodities, families reinforce a hierarchy where the worth of an individual is measured against their potential economic contributions. This places pressure on caretakers to evaluate every child born not through the lens of love and nurture, but of practicality and utility, thus erasing the intrinsic value of human life. In turn, this perspective cultivates a culture in which the abandonment of the weak or less favored is rationalized, perpetuating cycles of neglect and devaluation. As children are raised in such an environment, this perception can shift their values and self-worth. Children who grow up knowing they are expendable may internalize this message, leading to lifelong implications for their mental health and societal interactions. Conversely, those who survive and are accepted into the household are often burdened with the expectation of proving their worth, conscious of the precarious balance of love and utility that governs their existence. This dynamic can breed resentment and anxiety, as the very essence of childhood—innate worthiness and unconditional support—becomes conditional. Furthermore, this commodification of children can impact gender roles. Boys, viewed as potential providers, may receive more attention and investment than girls, who are often seen as less valuable due to their future obligations in marriage. This discrepancy fosters a gender disparity that is rooted in economic realities rather than cultural values, emphasizing the need to understand the motivations behind such decisions. At the societal level, as families align their values with economic viability, the collective moral compass may tip in favor of survival over compassion, leading to community norms that enable and justify these harsh practices. By framing children as participants in an economic strategy, the community unwittingly becomes complicit in an ethics of utilitarianism that disregards emotional bonds. The poverty cycle will continue as long as society fails to recognize the need for a shift toward valuing human life in and of itself, rather than in terms of economic output. This calls for a re-examination of the conceptualizations surrounding family and child-rearing practices, urging a more humane approach that prioritizes intrinsic value and emotional connection over financial considerations. In seeking to create a healthy society, addressing these attitudes becomes imperative.
