His framework, known as TeVeS (Tensor-Vector-Scalar gravitation), not only provided a different perspective on gravitational interactions but also offered a potential reconciliation between MOND and the established tenets of relativity. In this model, the additional degrees of freedom introduced by the scalar and vector fields modify the gravitational force under specific conditions, particularly in weak-field scenarios like those found in galactic dynamics. This reconciliation sparked renewed interest in MOND, as it suggested that the apparent discrepancies in galaxy rotation curves could be understood without invoking unseen matter. As researchers delved deeper, they explored the implications of Bekenstein's modifications, particularly in the context of large-scale cosmic structures. Observations of bending light around massive clusters challenged the predictions made by MOND alone, necessitating a precise articulation of how baryonic matter interacts within this new framework. The debate intensified as groups of cosmologists began to corroborate or refute different models of gravitation. Advocates for conventional dark matter theories frequently argued that the observed gravitational lensing effects in high-density regions, where galaxy clusters are found, were best explained by the presence of significant amounts of non-baryonic dark matter. The need to empirically validate MOND's assertions thus became paramount. Researchers employed observational strategies to gather data that would test the predictions of each theory against the actual behavior of the universe. The use of gravitational lensing as a litmus test became crucial; it provided a unique opportunity to compare how light bends around massive objects in accordance with the varying predictions of MOND and dark matter theories. Studies initiated to observe lensing events sought to determine whether the distribution of mass inferred through lensing aligned more closely with dark matter models or Bekenstein’s TeVeS. As astronomical instruments advanced, such as the implementation of large-scale surveys like the Sloan Digital Sky Survey and the upcoming Vera C. Rubin Observatory, the ability to capture high-definition images of the sky increased exponentially. These advancements allowed researchers to focus on faint and distant objects, such as the so-called “Einstein rings” or elongated arcs, which serve as telltale signs of gravitational lensing. Each discovery added another piece to the puzzle, contributing valuable insights that could either substantiate or undermine the case for dark matter or MOND. Through rigorous mathematical modeling and observational scrutiny, theorists began to identify scenarios in which Bekenstein's framework and MOND could provide viable alternative explanations for gravitational phenomena. Yet, as the research progressed, the question remained whether these alternatives could consistently account for the myriad of observations that dark matter proponents argued were essential. The landscape was shifting, revealing the ongoing tensions between competing narratives and underscoring the complexity of celestial mechanics and the quest to understand the universe's fundamental nature.
