In the centuries leading up to this shift, various philosophical and cultural traditions portrayed animals in a more nuanced light, seeing them as integral members of the biosphere rather than mere automatons. The teachings of Pythagoras and Platonists celebrated the kinship between humans and animals, advocating for a respect that extended to vegetarianism and the avoidance of harm to other sentient beings. These perspectives challenged the anthropocentric worldview that later intellectuals would adopt, suggesting a complex interplay of consciousness across species. Fables and folktales often personified animals, granting them agency and emotion, thereby fostering an early recognition of their emotional lives. The medieval bestiaries portrayed animals as moral exemplars, leveraging their behaviors to convey lessons about human ethics. In these narratives, the distinctions between human and animal consciousness were blurred, allowing a space for empathy and ethical consideration that rationalists would later dismiss. As scientific inquiry advanced, however, the mechanistic view relegated animals to the status of mere objects, whose purpose was defined solely by human utility. This shift, tied to the rise of modern science, devalued the moral and philosophical discourses surrounding animals by placing human rationality as an unassailable pinnacle of existence. The implications of such ideations were profound, paving the way for exploitation that justified practices such as factory farming and vivisection. A disconnection formed, leading society to increasingly ignore the communicative and emotional capacities of other species, which had been acknowledged earlier in history. Within the philosophical debates that emerged, the perspectives of Hobbes, Spinoza, and Voltaire began to redefine the parameters of moral consideration, arguing that sentience—in whatever form—should afford beings some degree of ethical regard. Hobbes posited that while animals operated on instinct, they nonetheless experienced desire and aversion, suggesting a more layered understanding of their emotional landscapes. Spinoza pushed these ideas further, arguing for a panpsychist view that assigned some form of consciousness to all of nature, including the animal kingdom. Voltaire, leveraging his wit, often critiqued the arbitrariness of human-centered morals, advocating for a more compassionate approach to our non-human counterparts. Despite these counterarguments, their voices struggled to gain traction against the prevailing currents of rationalism that dominated the intellectual landscape. It was partly because of this prevailing mindset that the ethical implications of animal treatment became obscured, paving the way for later philosophical movements to grapple with the contradictions inherent in a rationalistic worldview that denied the emotional experiences of animals. The Enlightenment's flourishing of science and reason came at a cost. It not only redefined the physical relationship between humans and animals but entrenched a separation that would echo through the industrial age and into contemporary society, necessitating a reevaluation of our ethical frameworks. As debates around welfare and rights began to emerge, the need to reconcile these views with a deeper understanding of animal consciousness became ever more critical and relevant.
