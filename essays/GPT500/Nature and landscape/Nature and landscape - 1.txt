This shift from formal to informal garden design not only reflected evolving aesthetic preferences but also indicated deeper societal changes. With the rise of the Industrial Revolution, the rapid urbanization and growth of the middle class altered perceptions of nature and leisure. Landscapes became not just sites of recreation but statements of identity, mirroring the fluidity of social structures. Gardens evolved into spaces where one could display wealth and creativity, yet they also served as retreats from the encroaching modern world. The pastoral aesthetics embraced by Brown aligned with contemporary Romantic ideals, which celebrated the sublime and the wild as antidotes to industrial monotony. By integrating natural features and designing landscapes that seemed to grow organically, Brown sought to re-establish a connection between humanity and the natural environment, a stark contrast to the rigid, manufactured gardens of the past. This not only appealed to those seeking a sense of pastoral peace but also echoed philosophical movements of the time that emphasized the spiritual and restorative qualities of nature. Visitors to estates like Longleat were encouraged to wander through these idyllic settings, experiencing a version of nature that was artistically curated yet felt spontaneous and unspoiled. In this context, the garden became an embodiment of the era’s contradictions: it hinted at the wildness of the natural world while firmly asserting human control through careful design. Brown’s influence extended beyond aesthetics; his landscapes often served as a political statement, subtly reinforcing the landowners’ authority while symbolizing their role as stewards of nature. The landscape was understood as a canvas upon which social aspirations could be painted, and his designs firmly planted this notion. Contemporary historians have begun to explore how these trends in garden design paralleled shifts in English attitudes toward land ownership and environmental responsibility. The romantic escape into nature that Brown promoted was not merely an indulgence; it also carried the weight of ecological mindfulness, albeit within a framework that favored elite tastes. This newfound appreciation of landscape reflected broader cultural movements whereby nature was seen as a source of moral and aesthetic clarity contrasting sharply with the frenetic pace of urban life. As the gardens adopted a more naturalistic approach, they became settings for social interactions and cultural events, further embedding them into the fabric of society. They were no longer just extensions of the estate, but vital venues for the performance of identity. Open-air soirées, literary gatherings, and even political discussions unfolded under the boughs of carefully arranged trees, combining recreation with the cultivation of social ideals. Thus, the shift to an informal landscape was both a response to changing tastes and a conscious effort to reposition the garden as a site of cultural significance, reflecting the intertwined evolution of society and space.
