While a worm navigates its existence through instinctual behaviors, I engage in a rich tapestry of desires, motivations, and aspirations. This differentiation becomes particularly important when considering the ethical implications of how we treat various beings. My ability to envision future scenarios and make choices affects not only my own well-being but also how my life is interwoven with the lives of others, human or otherwise. In my interactions with the world, I can engage in reflective thought—assessing past experiences, considering the consequences of potential actions, and adjusting my behaviors accordingly to nurture my goals. This reflective capacity allows for a dynamic approach to life's challenges, which presents a stark contrast to the static existence of a worm, confined to its immediate needs without the breadth of understanding that characterizes sentient beings. Furthermore, this complexity implies a moral responsibility, as my capacity for welfare elevates the stakes of my interactions. In weighing decisions, I must consider the well-being of others, especially those with limited agency. My higher cognitive functions permit not just a personal pursuit of happiness, but also an empathy that extends to other beings capable of suffering. Thus, the ethical considerations surrounding my existence and actions necessitate a framework that acknowledges the varied interests and needs of both agents and non-agents. Such a framework fosters a more humane society, recognizing the inherent value in all experiences, even those seemingly simpler than my own. Moreover, this interconnection of agency and welfare prompts deeper questions about the nature of happiness itself. If happiness is a product of both experience and agency, it follows that the quality of one's existence can be enriched by opportunities to make choices and pursue personal goals. A life devoid of choice may lack not only fulfillment but also the opportunity for deeper emotional engagement. For instance, the pursuit of knowledge, art, or relationships can dramatically heighten one’s sense of well-being, suggesting that agency itself contributes to a more profound form of happiness, one that transcends mere satisfaction of basic needs. This indicates that the elements defining a good life cannot merely be measured by immediate gratification but must incorporate the potential for growth, exploration, and the ability to reflect on the journey itself. Thus, the different capacities for agency and welfare highlight the moral imperatives of recognizing and respecting diverse experiences across living beings. Just as my existence as an agent grants me a sphere of influence over my own welfare, it also extends an obligation to safeguard and respect the welfare of others, encouraging a more compassionate and purposeful interaction with the world around me. Understanding these distinctions informs not only how I approach my own life but also how I relate to and advocate for the welfare of those who may lack the same agency, thus fostering a more inclusive ethical perspective.
