The perceptions introduced by Widgery exemplify a broader colonial mindset that sought to impose Western notions of civility and cleanliness on Indian society. This ideology was inherently paternalistic, reflecting a belief that European ways of life were superior and that non-European practices, particularly those rooted in religious and cultural traditions, were primitive or backward. The Jains' reverence for all living creatures, for instance, was often dismissed as a flaw rather than understood within its spiritual context. This discrepancy reveals deeper tensions in colonial discourse, where the moral high ground claimed by colonizers paradoxically overlooked their own violent practices against perceived nuisances, such as stray dogs. Such actions not only highlighted colonial hypocrisy but also reinforced an arbitrary hierarchy of value among living beings, which prioritized human life over animal welfare. As the Victorian era progressed, debates surrounding animal welfare began to intersect with the colonial project. In response to the rampant cruelty towards street animals, including the indiscriminate killing of stray dogs, reformist movements emerged within British society, focusing on humane treatment ethically justified by some as an extension of Christian morality. These movements often framed their campaigns in a way that positioned British cultural values as progressive, seeking to ‘civilize’ not only the people of the colonies but also their relationship with animals, which was depicted as brutal and unrefined. Simultaneously, there existed an unsettling dissonance: while advocating for animal rights at home, British officials were involved in widespread acts of violence against animals in the colonies. This juxtaposition underscored a contradiction within the imperial narrative—where moral imperatives clashed with the often brutal realities of colonial rule. The treatment of dogs in India thus became emblematic of a larger struggle, a microcosm reflecting colonial attitudes toward the indigenous populations and their intertwined beliefs about life and death. The colonial gaze not only sought to critique local customs but also served to justify the often brutal enforcement of new regulations imposed by colonial authorities. Amid these discussions, the role of the media cannot be overstated. Newspapers served as a platform for both reinforcing stereotypes and challenging the status quo. They frequently published sensational accounts of strays, framing them as public health concerns that required immediate action. This narrative not only fed into fears of disease but also constructed a moral landscape wherein the plight of street dogs became intertwined with notions of urban decay and social disorder. In portraying these animals as a nuisance threatening moral and physical hygiene, colonial writers like Widgery and Kipling contributed to a discourse that marginalized complex social realities, reducing a multilayered relationship between communities and animals to simple binaries of worth and threat. Ultimately, the depiction of stray dogs within this colonial context reveals how broader ideologies around governance, morality, and civilization were deeply intertwined. They illustrate the lengths to which colonial powers would go to impose their values, often at the expense of both human and animal lives, as they endeavored to reshape societies in their image. The consequences of these attitudes, and the systemic violence that accompanied them, are legacies still evident in discussions around urban wildlife and community responsibility today.
