In many families, traditional practices act as cultural cornerstones, imbued with generational significance. As a result, challenging these beliefs can lead to familial discord, complicating personal stances against TCM. The argument for scientific medicine, therefore, is not merely about efficacy but also about identity, as the very act of questioning TCM risks severing connections to one’s cultural past. This cognitive dissonance produces interesting social dynamics, where individuals feel pressured to conform to the beliefs of those around them, even when they internally grapple with their own convictions. The prevalence of TCM in day-to-day life, from herbal remedies bought in street markets to acupuncture sessions offered at local clinics, illustrates its pervasive influence. Popular health narrative frameworks often elevate anecdotal evidence above scientific discourse, leading many to perceive TCM as an inviolable truth rather than a contested or debatable domain. In this context, practitioners often become defenders of a lifestyle that is seen as holistic and culturally rich, drawing sharp lines against those who question or critique.
