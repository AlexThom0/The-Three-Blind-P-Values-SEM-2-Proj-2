The curriculum emphasizes metrics and key performance indicators, techniques for maximizing shareholder value, and the illusion of agility within an inherently static system. As the discourse of efficiency overshadows the original notion of managing, the essential qualities of empathy, foresight, and ethical stewardship are often downplayed or ignored. This has profound implications not just for the individuals pursuing careers in management, but also for the wider society that these future leaders will shape. Instead of cultivating a deeper understanding of societal needs and sustainable practices, the focus remains predominantly on the immediate financial outcomes that frequently align with quarterly expectations set by stakeholders. The emphasis on profitability can compel managers to overlook the larger context of their decisions, fostering a disconnect between business practices and broader societal issues such as environmental degradation or social inequality. This myopic view of success is perpetuated through the repetitive cycles of case studies that highlight profit-driven narratives, sidelining those that consider a company's social responsibilities or ecological impact. In many ways, this reflects a troubling paradox; while the tools of management evolve, often incorporating advanced quantitative techniques or digital platforms, the underlying principles remain tethered to outdated models of consumption and growth. Consequently, the aspirational goals of future managers become enshrined in a framework that privileges return on investment above all other indicators, leading to a profound gap between ambition and the ethical considerations that ought to guide leadership. When the foundational teachings of management overlook the interconnectedness of business decisions with human and environmental welfare, they contribute to a short-sighted worldview that is not only detrimental to community and planet but also compromises the longevity of the businesses themselves. Moreover, this emphasis on traditional business metrics can stifle creativity and innovation, as managers become more like calculators than visionaries. They are tasked with navigating a sea of metrics that often fail to capture the complexities of human interaction and ecological systems. Those who might envision alternate futures—grounded in cooperation and sustainability—are marginalized or, worse, discouraged from pursuing their insights. The management ‘expert’ risks becoming a mere cog in the machine, perpetuating a cycle that prioritizes immediate gains over long-term solutions. As the trajectory of managerial education continues to privilege economic viability over social responsibility, the ramifications extend well beyond the boardroom. The consequences are felt in the communities that suffer from corporate negligence and in the environment that bears the weight of unsustainable practices. In turn, this calls for a significant recalibration of what it means to manage, requiring a reimagining of the role that future managers will play not just in businesses, but in society as a whole. Such a transformation is necessary if we are to reclaim the original essence of management—one that balances profitability with a conscientious commitment to the future of our world.
