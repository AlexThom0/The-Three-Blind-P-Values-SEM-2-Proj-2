The stark reality of societal bias often manifests itself in the most insidious ways, affecting perceptions and interactions without those involved always realizing it. For my son, engaging passionately in public discourse came with a different set of stakes than those faced by his white peers. His vocal stance against injustice, fueled by his belief in equality and radical change, could easily be misconstrued or overreacted to in a world where he was viewed through the lens of racial stereotypes. The impending protest, where we were headed, represented a microcosm of those tensions, a vivid tapestry of ideology, race, and civil rights. Each shouted slogan and counter-slogan would encapsulate a spectrum of beliefs, but my son’s experience as a young Black man infused that atmosphere with a palpable weight. As he stepped into the fray, I felt both pride and trepidation. Witnessing him passionately articulate his beliefs was striking; invigorating, even. Yet I couldn’t suppress the flicker of fear that accompanied it—at any moment, the simplistic narratives of those around him could overshadow the complexity of who he was. The counter-protesters, passionate and often incendiary, would be rallying for their own reasons, oblivious or dismissive of the silent battles faced by young Black men. Their fervor might either invigorate Carson or put him in harm’s way, depending on how their passions clashed with the primal fear that sometimes drove responses from those in authority. I worried about the police presence as a wild card in this scenario. Every interaction in those charged environments would be colored by the social dynamics in play. Carson might find himself either emboldened to fight for his beliefs or struggling to navigate a landscape riddled with biases. Such an experience could serve as a reckoning, perhaps compelling him to confront the duality of existence that he hadn't yet fully grasped. He operated with a bravado typical of youth, emboldened by the tangible discussion of rights and wrongs he’d often engaged in online and in school. Yet, at that moment, it wasn’t just information that he needed but wisdom—the kind that could only come from being tested amid the crucible of a divided society. This protest wouldn’t merely be about Westboro Baptist’s hateful rhetoric; it would also be about grappling with the implications of those words. Carson stood on the precipice of understanding that activism could morph into something dangerous if not approached with an awareness of the landscape around him. Activism, after all, is as much about the audience as it is about the message. Each individual present would interpret his enthusiasm through their lenses—his color, his passion, the circumstances of the day—defining the narrative they wanted to embrace. The stakes were different for him, more palpable than they were for others who participated in acts of protest without such considerations shadowing their actions.
