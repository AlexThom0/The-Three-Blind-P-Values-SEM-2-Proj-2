This notion extends to various artifacts and environments, which can steer human actions in subtle yet profound ways. For instance, consider the role of urban design: the layout of a city can influence social interactions, encouraging gatherings in parks or promoting individualism in sprawling suburbs. Such spatial arrangements act as silent agents that foster specific behaviors, demonstrating how physical environments can shape the social fabric without overt human direction. Moreover, technology exemplifies the intricate interplay between human intention and the agency of non-human actors. A smartphone, for instance, not only serves as a tool for communication but also shapes the manner in which individuals interact with one another and navigate their daily lives. This device has the efficacy to create networks of connection, often replacing face-to-face interactions with virtual dialogues, thus redefining social norms. As these devices become increasingly embedded in our lives, their propensity to guide our choices, from trivial tasks to significant life decisions, reveals how they function as active participants in shaping human experiences. Incorporating perspectives from new materialism further illuminates this relationship, pressing for an appreciation of non-human entities not merely as passive objects but as vital components of emotional and societal landscapes. Materials, objects, and technologies collectively contribute to the production and reproduction of culture, suggesting a more integrated view of agency that transcends traditional human-centric approaches. By acknowledging the roles played by these non-human actors, a more holistic understanding of agency emerges, identifying how they interact dynamically with human intentions. This understanding also extends to natural environments. Ecosystems, rivers, and mountains possess a form of agency, governing behaviors and decisions made by the communities inhabiting them. In rural settings, local agriculture hinges not just on the labor of farmers but also on the characteristics of the land—its soil types, weather patterns, and even water availability transition from background elements to influential agents in agricultural practice. By recognizing the tangible impacts of these natural agents, we see how they shape societal structures and cultural practices, illustrating the interconnectedness of human and non-human forces. This convergence of intentionality highlights the necessity of rethinking our conceptual frameworks around agency. Instead of viewing human action as the sole driver of change, we must analyze how all elements within an environment—living and non-living—coalesce to create a complex web of influence. Such an approach invites a richer discourse on responsibility, as it complicates notions of accountability in a world where agency is diffused across a myriad of contributors. The implications reach beyond theoretical inquiry, resonating in the realms of ethics, environmental justice, and the pursuit of sustainable futures, demanding holistic considerations that encompass every actor, seen and unseen, within the tapestry of existence.
