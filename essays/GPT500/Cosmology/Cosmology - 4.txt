Rather than posit the existence of dark matter, MOND suggests that the laws of gravity must be modified in low-acceleration environments. With this hypothesis, Milgrom aimed to not only account for the rotational behaviors of galaxies but also to provide a more fundamental description of gravitational interactions at large scales. This framework sought to explain why galaxy rotation curves deviated so significantly from the expectations driven by luminous matter alone. By introducing a new form of acceleration scale, previously unrecognized in classical physics, Milgrom's MOND posited that at accelerations below a particular value—around 10^-10 m/s²—the dynamics of bodies would no longer follow the inverse-square law as dictated by Newton. Instead, gravitational forces would behave differently at these low accelerations, effectively altering the relationship between mass and the gravitational force experienced. This hypothesis challenged the very foundation of classical physics and sparked significant debate within the astrophysics community. Some welcomed the opportunity for new insights into gravity, while others were skeptical, arguing that without direct evidence of new forces or mechanisms, such deviations from established principles could undermine decades of scientific progress. However, Milgrom's ideas found significant traction, particularly given their capacity to explain observations that dark matter struggled to clarify. Not only did MOND elegantly address the peculiarities of galaxy rotation curves, but it also suggested a framework where the mass distribution within galaxies could be more closely understood. In such a context, the ordinary matter in galaxies—composed of stars, gas, and dust—could play a much more prominent role than previously appreciated, relegating the dark matter hypothesis to the background in certain environments. The implications were vast, leading to a reevaluation of the role of baryonic matter in galactic structure formation and dynamics. An array of empirical studies began to surface, examining the MOND framework against real observational data. Many researchers conducted tests across various galaxies to gauge how MOND performed in explaining rotation curves compared to the dark matter model. Results were mixed; in some cases, MOND provided a superior fit to the data, while in others it fell short, particularly when faced with gravitational lensing phenomena and the cosmic microwave background radiation measurements that strongly supported the existence of dark matter. Such contradictions highlighted the necessity for a comprehensive synthesis of ideas in order to truly advance the field. Milgrom's approach prompted not only a reassessment of gravity but also renewed interest in alternative theories of gravity more generally. Extensions and adaptations of MOND inspired a plethora of new research focused on how gravity could operate under differing conditions. These discussions fostered collaborative endeavors among theoretical physicists and astronomers, igniting broader inquiries into the fundamental laws governing the universe. The dialogue between proponents of MOND and dark matter theorists led to deeper investigations into the nature of gravitational interactions.
