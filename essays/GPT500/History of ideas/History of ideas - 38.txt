In contrast to mere flattery, which aims to elicit immediate gratification, effective rhetoric must appeal to deeper aspects of the soul — the rational and the spirited elements that drive a person toward the good. For Plato, the soul can be imagined as a charioteer steering two horses, one embodying rationality and the other passion. When the charioteer skillfully guides the steeds, they propel toward a virtuous destination, harmonizing reason and emotion rather than allowing them to conflict. This synergy is essential for any rhetoric intended to elevate rather than simply entertain. Rhetorical skill, then, is predicated on this understanding of the soul, necessitating that the rhetor engage with an audience's higher aspirations and ethical portents, rather than pandering to their base instincts. Such engagement asks the rhetor to be attuned to the moral implications of their discourse. The challenge lies not merely in crafting persuasive arguments but in inspiring an audience to act in accordance with higher truths, leading to both individual and collective flourishing. Rhetoric, as conceived by Plato, becomes a means of guiding souls towards enlightenment and virtue — a far cry from the manipulative techniques that dominate demagoguery. In practical terms, this vision of rhetoric invites deeper interrogation of the values embedded within speech and argumentation. It suggests that great rhetoric demands an ethical commitment from the rhetor; they must be both adept at persuasion and anchored in a genuine concern for the well-being of their audience. Here, rhetoric transforms from a tool for personal advancement into a collaborative endeavor, where speaker and listener work in concert toward elevated ideals. The responsibility of the speaker escalates as they must grapple with the weight of their influence, recognizing that their words can shape the moral landscape of their community. Moreover, Plato’s model demands a critical attitude toward the prevailing norms of rhetoric in his day, which often prioritized style over substance. By challenging this superficiality, he laid the groundwork for a more profound exploration of truth and meaning in discourse. The emphasis shifts from mere effectiveness — the ability to persuade at any cost — to the ethical considerations that undergird successful rhetoric, positioning it as a noble art bound to the pursuit of truth and goodness. Consequently, this raises vital questions about the role of the speaker in fostering a dialogue that prioritizes not only engagement but also enlightenment. If the aim of rhetoric is to foster understanding and promote virtue, then it becomes imperative for speakers to cultivate an awareness of their audience's deeper motivations and aspirations. Rhetoric, thus, emerges not just as a means of persuasion but as a practice that integrates knowledge, ethics, and artistry, all while striving toward the common good.
