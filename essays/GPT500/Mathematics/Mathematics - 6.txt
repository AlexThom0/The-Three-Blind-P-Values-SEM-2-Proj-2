Núñez argues that the notion of a number module is overly simplistic and fails to account for the complexities of how numerical understanding develops within cultural contexts. He suggests that while neural responses to visual stimuli may indicate a foundational cognitive ability, they do not, in themselves, provide a complete picture of numerical cognition. For instance, many cultures employ different strategies for counting and quantification, influencing not just the symbols used but also the fundamental concepts of quantity and relation. This cultural mediation suggests that our interactions with numbers are not merely an extension of biological predispositions but also deeply influenced by language and social practices. Taking into account these sociocultural dynamics highlights the significant role of education and context in shaping our numerical understanding. Further supporting this standpoint, Núñez points to societies that do not employ counting systems beyond small quantities, like one, two, and many. In these cultures, individuals may not possess the same sense of numerosity as those raised in more mathematically inclined environments, suggesting that counting and numerical understanding are, to a considerable extent, learned behaviours rather than purely innate abilities. By examining various cultural approaches to mathematics, it becomes evident that the environment plays a crucial role, potentially reshaping cognitive frameworks even when biological capacity exists. The malleability of numerical cognition underscores the fact that teaching and learning practices significantly augment our inherent cognitive abilities. Moreover, even within mathematically sophisticated cultures, knowledge of numbers can take various forms—additive, multiplicative, or even spatial reasoning—all of which depend on context and experience. The significant diversity in mathematical practices around the world illustrates that our cognitive ability regarding numbers can be nurtured or stifled depending on instruction and the presence of a supportive cultural structure. This perspective shifts the debate from a nature-dominated viewpoint to a more integrative framework that includes nurture as a central pillar in cognitive development. Examining educational systems reveals how children are often guided into mathematical thinking through well-designed curricula that help them navigate the complexities of quantity and representation. Techniques such as leveraging visual aids, engaging in hands-on activities, and incorporating cultural props can enhance or hinder mathematical understanding. The impact of pedagogy on numerical fluency is undeniable; it shapes the cognitive landscape within which innate abilities manifest. Additionally, the advancement of technology and the digital age has introduced new dimensions to numeracy, further emphasizing the need to acknowledge the interplay between biological predisposition and cultural transmission. Computational tools, for instance, can provide access to sophisticated mathematical concepts that extend beyond traditional classroom methods, illustrating that our numerical skills can expand in ways previously unimagined. These advancements highlight the ongoing evolution of our understanding of numbers, further supporting Núñez's assertion that we must reconsider the strict biological determinism often associated with cognitive skills. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of numerical cognition must incorporate both our innate faculties and the rich tapestry of cultural influences that shape how we think about and interact with numbers.
