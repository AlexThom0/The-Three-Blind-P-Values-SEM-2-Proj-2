Her research illuminated the complexities faced by women who sought both professional fulfillment and adherence to traditional family structures. Parwati's findings underscored that while economic participation was essential for empowerment, it did not automatically lead to social or political emancipation. Instead, she noted that many women navigated a landscape where professional ambitions were often stifled by lingering patriarchal norms. In her interviews, several respondents described the tension between their work lives and familial obligations, articulating feelings of guilt and inadequacy as they were pulled in different directions by societal expectations. This duality became a central theme in Parwati's work, prompting her to explore how cultural narratives shaped the self-perception of these women. Through qualitative methods, she examined the stories of women who, despite their professional success, often perceived themselves primarily in relation to their roles as mothers and wives. Many felt a profound sense of duty to uphold familial harmony, revealing how these obligations often overshadowed their professional identities. Parwati also analyzed the impact of education and socio-economic status on these perceptions, finding that while higher education provided women with more opportunities, it also exposed them to greater scrutiny regarding their choices. Additionally, she delved into the historical context of women’s rights in Indonesia, tracing the evolution of societal attitudes from colonial times to the modern era. This historical lens allowed her to frame the narratives she collected within a broader discourse on gender and power. Parwati argued that the struggle for emancipation was not solely a modern phenomenon; rather, it had roots in the intersection of political, economic, and cultural developments over decades. By highlighting the continuities and changes in women’s roles throughout Indonesian history, she provided a nuanced understanding of contemporary challenges. In her dissertation, Parwati also examined the role of community support systems for working mothers. She pointed out the critical importance of familial and social networks that often sustained these women, enabling them to balance work and family duties. While some respondents spoke highly of the support they received from extended family members, others expressed disappointment when such support was lacking, illustrating how community dynamics could significantly impact women’s capacity to pursue both career and personal aspirations. Through her comparative case studies, Parwati urged scholars and policymakers to consider the diverse experiences of women across different regions and socio-economic backgrounds in Indonesia. Her work advocated for more tailored approaches to women's empowerment, recognizing that a one-size-fits-all model would not suffice in addressing the varying needs and aspirations of women in different contexts. By pushing for a deeper understanding of local cultural frameworks and their implications for gender rights, she positioned her research not just as academic inquiry but as a call to action for meaningful change within both academic and societal realms. These insights remain essential for developing strategies that genuinely promote gender equality in Indonesia.
