In his view, the centralized authority of the Saudi state provided a unique framework where difference could be maintained but not encouraged. This arrangement, he argued, prevented conflict that could arise from competing interpretations of Islam while simultaneously reinforcing the broader social order. The vast number of pilgrims participating in the Hajj, regardless of their specific sects, showcases how governance can facilitate a temporary suspension of sectarian identity in favor of a shared religious experience. Here, the Deobandi cleric posited that political authority acts as a stabilizing force, ensuring that the religious landscape remains manageable and unchallenged by disruptive forces. Contrastingly, he viewed Indian democracy as an arena of constant tension, where the expression of diverse beliefs often escalates into quarrels over legitimacy. The absence of a unified state religion in India, rather than fostering a cooperative spirit among sects, gives rise to friction and discord, as various groups vie for recognition and influence. The cleric painted a vivid picture of this struggle, demonstrating how, on numerous occasions, underlying resentments surface to publicly disrupt the social fabric. He expressed concern that such tensions not only undermine public order but also challenge the Islamic imperative of unity among believers. As a result, the Deobandi perspective contends that a state-centric religion would provide a bulwark against the centrifugal forces of pluralism, effectively channeling religious sentiment into a cohesive national identity. To be successful in this endeavor, the state must not only endorse a singular interpretation of Islam but actively suppress dissent to foster a climate conducive to collective worship. Such a model, he suggested, is more beneficial for society than a cacophony of voices competing for prominence, which could easily devolve into chaos. Moreover, the arrogance of democratic freedoms, which promote unrestrained individual expression, often blinds adherents to the potential for societal fragmentation. The Deobandi cleric cautioned that while he valued the principles espoused by liberal thinkers, they risked overlooking the practical realities of governance. Tolerance without structure can breed indifference or worse, fanaticism, when groups feel their identity and legitimacy are under siege. In this light, the politicization of religion becomes a vital mechanism for maintaining peace and preventing conflict. Ultimately, his observations regarding the interplay between governance and belief systems served as a reminder that political realities often dictate the viability of diverse religious sentiments. His analysis urges a reevaluation of how religious diversity can be effectively managed within different sociopolitical systems. With tensions surrounding the expression of faith rising globally, understanding the relationship between political authority and religious practice remains crucial for fostering lasting harmony among communities.
