These examples cultivate a narrative that celebrates the individual, championing resilience as the pathway to accomplishment. However, this narrative can obscure the complexities of success and how achievement often derives from a confluence of factors, including, but not limited to, opportunity, resources, and socio-cultural capital. The romanticization of grit risks reducing struggles and adversity to mere individual failings, while also placing immense pressure on those who are already disadvantaged. Individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds frequently face systemic barriers that cannot simply be overcome with hard work and determination alone. For them, the hurdles to success are multifaceted and deeply entrenched, affecting their access to quality education, networking opportunities, or even basic psychological resources such as support systems and mental well-being. Furthermore, framing success predominantly through the lens of grit encourages a performative understanding of struggle, wherein engaging with hardship becomes a badge of honor that often overlooks the unsung contributions of community support and collective resilience. It inadvertently prioritizes individual success stories that validate Duckworth's hypothesis while neglecting to explore the societal structures that shape those experiences in the first place. This one-dimensional portrayal may lead many to internalize failures as personal shortcomings rather than recognizing them as manifestations of larger systemic inequities. Thus, while grit can play a significant role in personal achievement, it is essential to analyze the broader contexts in which individuals operate. Exploring the interplay between individual effort and systemic factors can deepen our understanding of what truly drives success. For instance, exploring the reasons behind the high attrition rates among students from marginalized communities invites a more nuanced discussion. It reveals that the fight against adversity is often compounded by socioeconomic struggles and a lack of representation in spaces of influence, which can be disheartening, especially for those seeking validation through grit. When the narrative solely emphasizes personal perseverance, it risks ignoring the vital influence of mentorship, access to quality education, and the vital role that community plays in sustaining individuals through challenges. Recognizing the shared responsibility of creating environments that nurture potential rather than solely attributing outcomes to individual efforts can shift the conversation on success. Instead of reducing grit to a standalone quality, we should advocate for broader, structural changes that will elevate collective success and provide equitable opportunities for all. In doing so, we honor not just the notion of trying hard but also the environments that allow people to thrive. This comprehensive approach has the potential to reshape our definitions of success, moving from a singular focus on individual grit to a richer understanding of the collaborative nature required for genuine advancement.
