Many African, Asian, and Indigenous communities had their own forms of writing, oral traditions, and modes of knowledge transmission that predated European colonialism. These systems were not merely primitive or simplistic, but complex and sophisticated in their ability to convey culture, history, and philosophical approaches to existence. Rather than a binary division of literate and non-literate societies, a continuum illustrates the various ways societies have employed language and communication, catering to their distinct socio-cultural landscapes. By acknowledging the influence of orality in these communities, we can recognize how such traditions shaped societal structures and identities, challenging the notion that print was the sole catalyst for intellectual advancement. Furthermore, the assertion that print technology singularly revolutionized thought diminishes the role of community interaction and public discourse in shaping ideas. In many Indigenous cultures, knowledge was maintained through storytelling, rituals, and communal gatherings, fostering a collective memory and shared understanding that was as vital and complex as any printed text. Rather than being a relic of the past, these oral practices provided frameworks for ethics, governance, and ways of knowing that were deeply intertwined with the environments and experiences of those communities. McLuhan’s perspective not only reframed the narrative around technological advancements but also neglected the agency of non-Western peoples in formulating their understandings of the world. His deterministic view of technology suggested that societies evolve in a linear progression towards modernity, as defined by Western parameters. This inevitability of technological evolution discounts the numerous and varied pathways societies have taken based on their specific circumstances and historical contexts. By framing European experience as the pinnacle of a universal narrative, it promotes a homogenized version of history that erases the contributions of countless cultures. The implications of this narrative are particularly pronounced when analyzing the global South’s interactions with modernity and technology. By categorically dismissing the intellectual contributions of these regions, McLuhan entrenches colonial stereotypes and perpetuates a cycle of marginalization. Scholars today emphasize the importance of examining how technology interacts with local practices and belief systems, instead of imposing a one-size-fits-all model of progression. The idea that technological adoption is a straightforward path neglects messy realities, such as resistance, adaptation, and the transformation of local cultural practices in light of new innovations. In recognizing these complexities, we pave the way for a broader understanding of knowledge production that appreciates the myriad voices and experiences shaping global histories. This requires a critical examination of historical narratives and the frameworks we use to interpret them. Embracing a pluralistic understanding of knowledge enables us to appreciate not only the diversity of thought but also the vibrancy of intellectual traditions that have, for too long, remained on the periphery of mainstream discourse. Such an approach does not merely enrich our understanding of the past; it also informs how we engage with contemporary issues surrounding technology, culture, and identity.
