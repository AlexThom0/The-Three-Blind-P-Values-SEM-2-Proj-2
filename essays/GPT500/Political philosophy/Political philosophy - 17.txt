He argued that the transition from feudalism to capitalism was not merely a linear progression but rather a multifaceted process influenced by various political, social, and cultural dynamics specific to each region. This interpretation resonated deeply with younger Marxists in India, who were grappling with their own historical contexts marked by colonial legacy and emerging national identities. As Hobsbawm parsed through historical events, he emphasized the importance of local conditions and the influence of international capitalism, a perspective that invigorated debates surrounding the Indian struggle for independence and its aftermath. Students and intellectuals were captivated by his approach, which encouraged a re-evaluation of indigenous social formations and their relationship to global trends. This intellectual ferment in India, often characterized by a fusion of local Marxist theory and Western Marxist thought, created fertile ground for Hobsbawm's ideas to take root. The youth of the time, disillusioned by the limitations of established political parties, sought new frameworks through which they could interpret their rapidly changing society. This search paved the way for movements that challenged the status quo and sought deeper structural changes. Hobsbawm’s writings not only influenced their theoretical understanding but also inspired a moral imperative to address inequalities, reminding them that ideology should anchor itself in the lived experiences of the people. Moreover, Hobsbawm's critiques of economic determinism also found a receptive audience. His insistence that culture and political agency are crucial in shaping economic outcomes aligned well with Indian Marxist orientations that sought to understand the traditions and practices that sustained societal hierarchies. Figures like Mohan Kumaramangalam saw in Hobsbawm's work a resolution to the growing tensions between traditional ideologies and modern political aspirations. This intellectual synergy was reflective of a larger global discourse among leftist thinkers who were simultaneously striving to redefine Marxism in light of contemporary realities. Thus, as young Marxists navigated their unique socio-political landscapes, Hobsbawm's contributions provided both a theoretical foundation and a narrative framework, allowing them to articulate their frustrations and aspirations more effectively. As they engaged with Hobsbawm’s ideas, an original and localized Marxist discourse began to emerge, diverging from European-centric interpretations. The emphasis on colonialism's legacies, class struggle in the context of post-colonial nation-building, and the contradictions within capitalist development found their way into Marxist scholarship in India. This transformation in Marxist thinking revealed a vibrant engagement with theoretical traditions that were being actively reconstructed in response to local realities. The debate, therefore, transcended academic circles and found a profound echo in the ideological battles of the day, as emergent leaders and thinkers articulated revolutionary visions that drew inspiration from both global Marxist thought and indigenous experiences in India.
