This exploitation of public sentiment was strategic; by framing their self-serving policies as necessary for the greater good, they effectively obscured the truth of their manipulative practices. The merchants' ability to sway public opinion allowed them to cultivate a narrative that justified their monopolistic behaviors and the restrictive regulations that they championed. While they profited, average citizens found themselves trapped in a system that prioritized the accumulation of wealth for the elite, all the while demonizing alternatives that might disrupt their carefully curated dominance. Smith recognized that this portrayal of trade and economics was not just misleading but fundamentally flawed. He argued that true wealth should not be measured simply in precious metals or confined to trade surpluses. Instead, it ought to encompass the broader spectrum of human welfare, including labor productivity and the health of local economies. This insight underpinned his advocacy for a more enlightened approach to economic policy—one that prioritized competition and innovation over the artificial constraints imposed by mercantile doctrine. By allowing markets to operate freely, he believed, nations could break the cycle of conspiracy among elites and enable a more equitable distribution of wealth. In contrast to the merchants' clamorous calls for protectionism and trade barriers, Smith's ideas championed the benefits of free trade and open competition. He posited that a nation’s wealth is more effectively enhanced not through a narrow focus on export surplus but by fostering an environment where goods, services, and ideas could flow freely across borders. Such an approach would stimulate domestic industries and ensure that consumers benefited from both lower prices and higher-quality products. Sweeping away the cobwebs of mercantilism allowed for a more dynamic economy, one capable of innovation and growth that serves the needs of the populace rather than the whims of a privileged few. Despite the allure of the mercantile rhetoric, the durability of its doctrines began to wane as enlightened thinkers like Smith increasingly called into question the sustainability of such a system. They explored the complexities of interdependence among nations and advocated for policies that aimed not only to protect national interests in a narrow sense but also to recognize the interconnectedness of global trade relationships. This shift in thinking suggested that nations could indeed become wealthier not by hoarding resources or engaging in zero-sum competition, but by collaborating and exchanging in ways that bolstered mutual prosperity. Thus, the merchants’ monopoly over economic thought faced mounting challenges as the foundations of classical economics began to take shape. As these revolutionary ideas permeated public discourse, they sparked debates that reshaped societal norms and expectations, laying the groundwork for contemporary understandings of economics that value inclusivity and shared prosperity over parochial self-interest.
