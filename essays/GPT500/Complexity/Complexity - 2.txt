These individuals, accustomed to the high-stakes environment of gambling, often employ a unique set of heuristics that can influence broader market behaviours during crises. When the pandemic unfolded, many traders leaned on their experiences in sports betting to assess the rapidly changing landscape. Their instinctual reliance on patterns and probabilities transformed traditional market reactions into behaviors shaped by emotional responses and rapid, instinctive decision-making, echoing the scramble seen during panic-driven events. As bets turned into trades, the psychological underpinnings of risk-taking became evident; market participants often acted not just on analytical predictions but also on fears and hopes that stemmed from their personal experiences in uncertain environments. Furthermore, this interplay between gambling psychology and market dynamics introduces additional complexity to understanding contagion effects. For instance, the concept of streakiness, prevalent in betting, seizes relevance here. Traders, much like bettors on a winning streak, may become overly confident following an initial market downturn, believing they can predict the next reversal. This mindset can create artificial stability or volatility, as clusters of uncertainty prompt reactions not entirely grounded in statistical reality. Traders might ignore fundamental analysis in favour of following trends or the movements of peers, leading to herd behaviour where the momentum of trading decisions appears to be predicated on collective sentiment rather than individual, informed assessments. As the pandemic progressed, the emergence of social trading platforms further amplified this phenomenon, allowing less experienced investors to mimic the actions of seasoned traders. The instantaneous sharing of information and strategies online leveraged the emotional responses tied to both market fluctuations and social validation. The FOMO (fear of missing out) culture that took root during this period exemplifies how contagion not only pertains to physical health but also spreads through financial decision-making. Investors witnessed the rapid rise of retail trading and, emboldened by tales of quick profits and communal reinforcement, many jumped into the fray, disregarding the risks inherent in such an environment. The accessibility of real-time trading data cultivated a sense of urgency that led to decidedly non-linear patterns in market behaviour, often driven by external narratives rather than economic fundamentals. This disjunction can create feedback loops, where large numbers of trades dictated by emotional impulses inflate asset prices, resulting in unsustainable bubbles. The bursting of such bubbles, whilst often initially viewed as unforeseeable, can often be traced back to the very behaviours fostered by this environment of contagion, leading to second-order events that exacerbate market instability. Thus, the dynamics of human behaviour, influenced by the psychological factors inherent in both gambling and trading, become critical for understanding not just isolated incidents of market disruption, but the broader implications for financial systems. The intersection of these behaviours during a crisis offers insights into managing future tail events and highlights the necessity for a more nuanced approach to risk assessment that considers the unique ways in which human agents interact with stochastic processes.
