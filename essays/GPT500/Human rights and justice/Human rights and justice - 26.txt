By perpetuating a cycle of dependency, Aanan and others like him create an environment where the workers not only feel obliged to conform but also develop an emotional investment in their oppressors. This dependence is nurtured through subtle manipulations; small loans and favors are framed as benign and generous acts, yet they are tools designed to entrap. Aanan's rhetoric echoes this sentiment, weaving tales that humanize himself while dehumanizing the workers. He employs language laden with paternalistic overtones, painting himself as a benevolent guardian of the laborers, who he asserts lack the ability to make sound decisions without his guidance. This paternalism masquerades as a protective measure but serves to entrench his authority further. Instead of empowering the workers to seek independence or better conditions, he reinforces the narrative that they are unfit to strive on their own. Such tactics are not exclusive to individual interactions; they permeate the very structure of labor relations. The psychological burden on workers is compounded by societal narratives that frame them as inherently lesser, incapable of envisioning futures beyond their immediate struggles. This condition, exacerbated by economic vulnerability, fosters a culture where aspirations are stifled, and dreams of escaping oppression fade into a mere flicker. Moreover, Aanan’s assertion about emotional pressure reveals a calculated strategy where empathy becomes both a tool and a weapon. He understands the power of emotional leverage, using it to manipulate sentiments to serve his interests. Workers often find themselves caught in a web of obligation; they feel indebted not just financially but also emotionally. This complex interplay of sympathy and subjugation makes it exceptionally challenging for individuals to challenge their circumstances. Aanan’s construction of ‘us versus them’ perpetuates a tribal mentality that stymies potential solidarity among workers. The appeal to loyalty and gratitude distracts from the stark realities of exploitation and reinforces a belief that collaboration among the oppressed is impossible or unwise. They may regard one another with suspicion rather than camaraderie, further preventing collective action and reinforcing Aanan's position of power. This emotional conditioning is insidious; it cultivates an environment where the line between reality and manipulated perception blurs. As Aanan skillfully manages these dynamics, he creates a narrative that blames the workers for their plight while positioning himself as their necessary savior. In this way, he prevents them from recognizing the true source of their suffering — the systemic inequalities and entrenched power dynamics they navigate daily. As conversations unfold, the complexities of these relationships become increasingly evident, revealing how deeply embedded power structures can shape perceptions and behaviors. It is a paradox where the oppressed remain crucial to the survival of oppressive systems, binding them in cycles that seem inescapable. Understanding this manipulation demands a reckoning with both the economic realities at play and the emotional experiences that form the basis of compliance and control.
