This simplification allowed colonial authorities to impose rigid interpretations of tribal identity that served their economic and political interests. Such impositions frequently alienated individuals from their heritage, subsuming diverse cultures under a unified but artificial legal category that belied the rich tapestry of indigenous traditions. In practice, tribal affiliations became defined less by historical and cultural connections and more by the needs of the colonial state. Elders, who traditionally held wisdom and authority, found their roles diluted as colonial officials imposed new hierarchies that favored a selected few. The resultant 'tribal councils' often operated under the banner of local governance but lacked genuine legitimacy, as they were co-opted by colonial powers to facilitate resource extraction and maintain social order. Indigenous resistance to these imposed structures was met with systematic repression, reinforcing the notion that such arrangements were essential for maintaining peace and progress. Simultaneously, the categorization of individuals into tribal groups had significant implications on their social mobility and access to resources. Those deemed part of the culturally sanctioned, traditional structures often encountered pathways to limited recognition, while others were marginalized or stripped of their rights entirely. This created an environment where the colonial authorities could manipulate tribal identity to destabilize communities, fostering divisions that undermined collective resistance. The legal frameworks established under both civil and customary law were designed to perpetuate settler colonialism by erasing the fluidity and dynamism inherent in indigenous identities. Settlers positioned themselves as actors of civilization, tasked with reforming what they perceived as 'backward' practices. The ethos of this worldview crystallized in various laws and regulations that scrutinized individuals’ conformance to these imposed identities, severely restricting their autonomy. Moreover, the discourse surrounding incorporation into civil or customary structures often ignored the wishes of the indigenous populations, revealing a colonial paternalism intent on defining what constituted progress. Such frameworks not only dehumanized individuals but also transformed them into subjects who had to navigate a labyrinthine legal system that was as alien as it was oppressive. The veneer of civilizational uplift, thus, belied the reality of social control wherein both systems functioned to disenfranchise native populations while presenting themselves as just and beneficial. Importantly, the intersection of these regimes meant that many indigenous people did not perceive their struggles as purely racial or tribal; rather, their experiences reflected a complex interplay of both systems enforcing dominance. This careful distinction is crucial for understanding the depth of colonial architecture, wherein both civil and customary legal systems were tools of oppression crafted to maintain the hegemony of settlers over the land and its original inhabitants. The legacy of this dual oppression continues to influence contemporary discussions surrounding identity and governance in these societies.
