Such practices increase the likelihood of zoonotic spillover events—where pathogens leap from animals to humans—by creating ideal conditions for viruses to thrive. The concentration of livestock in close quarters, coupled with the global supply chain’s rapid movement of animals and animal products, can facilitate the transfer of pathogens across vast distances. Furthermore, the expansion of industrial agriculture into previously untouched habitats disrupts biodiversity, which can destabilize local ecosystems, making them more susceptible to disease outbreaks. Resources are often redirected away from disease prevention and surveillance, leaving communities vulnerable to outbreaks that could have been mitigated through better practices. Moreover, the economic frameworks within which these agricultural practices operate often undermine traditional ecological knowledge that indigenous communities possess. These communities have historically managed resources in a way that promotes biodiversity and overall ecosystem health. However, the encroachment of neoliberal policies often marginalizes these perspectives, favoring large agribusinesses that may not value ecological sustainability. As a result, the loss of biodiversity due to habitat destruction and corporate farming practices not only creates environments ripe for disease emergence but also reduces the resilience of ecosystems to withstand such threats. The correlation between agricultural practices encouraged by neoliberal policies and the rise of zoonotic diseases prompts an urgent reevaluation of how we approach food systems globally. Additionally, the global market pushes for uniformity in agricultural products, which further reduces genetic diversity among crops and livestock. This genetic uniformity can make species more susceptible to diseases since there are fewer variations that might confer resistance. For instance, large-scale poultry and pig farming operations often rely on a limited number of breeds selected for their marketability, resulting in weakened populations that can rapidly succumb to infectious agents. Such vulnerabilities are not isolated to one location or species; they have implications for food security and public health worldwide, creating a cascade effect that can endanger entire communities. This intertwining of capitalism and ecology raises fundamental questions about the sustainability of our current trajectories. The framing of disease names, as discussed in the WHO guidelines, becomes an echo of these broader socio-political dynamics, reflecting how human activity shapes our understanding of biological realities. The portrayal of diseases is not merely an academic exercise; it is directly influenced by the socio-economic context within which we operate. Changing the narratives around diseases may help mitigate stigma, but it does not address the root causes of their emergence tied to human interactions with the environment. Addressing these root causes therefore requires a comprehensive approach that not only involves policymakers and health organizations but also the communities most affected by these zoonotic diseases, ensuring a more equitable and holistic response to public health in an increasingly interconnected world.
