During the Renaissance and into the Baroque period, the act of smiling—particularly when showing teeth—was laden with connotations of status, refinement, and even danger. To smile openly was to risk suggesting a lack of self-control, reflecting a certain je ne sais quoi of the time that prioritized restraint over exuberance. This aesthetic assumption permeated various artistic movements, communicating ideals of virtue and moral fortitude. Artists, therefore, favored representations that implied an inner world full of emotions subdued, where the outer expression remained elegantly composed and closed. As societies evolved, so too did the significance attributed to smiles and their relation to identity. By the late 18th and early 19th centuries, broader shifts in social and political contexts began to alter the function of the smile in art and culture. The Enlightenment, with its emphasis on reason and individualism, influenced artists to grapple with new understandings of personal and social identity. The transition toward romanticism brought forth an era where emotions were often elevated, and expressions began to reflect personal sentimentive experiences. This newly found favor for the open and expressive smile in painting signified a departure from elitist control over emotional displays. Moreover, the burgeoning fascination with the sublime and the sentimental in the realm of artistic expression encouraged a turn toward the raw, unfiltered aspects of humanity. As artists began to depict scenes from everyday life, candid interactions, and emotional intimacy, teeth bared in genuine smiles emerged as symbols of authenticity and warmth. The smile transformed into a versatile vessel for expressing joy, camaraderie, and the complexities of human connection. Painters like Thomas Gainsborough and later Romantic artists would use the smile not only to evoke emotion but as a way to invite the viewer into a shared experience, forming a bond that transcended mere visual representation. The moral and ethical implications of such an expression are also noteworthy. As the social stratifications began to dissolve and middle classes grew more prominent, the portrayal of smiling faces became increasingly democratized. The emancipation of expressions from strictly aristocratic confines allowed broader segments of society to relate to and appreciate the embellishment of happiness that showcased their own lived experiences. Thus, the emergence of a more open smile in art didn’t just redefine aesthetics; it reshaped societal norms that had dictated how emotions were publicly expressed. Thus, what was once considered genteel restraint morphed into a canvass for emotional and social discourse, whereby the smile evolved from a symbol of potential danger to one indicative of human joy and connection. In this light, it becomes evident that the evolution of the smile in art speaks volumes about the changing nature of cultural identity and the ever-deepening reflections of human emotion throughout history.
