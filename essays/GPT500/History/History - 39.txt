The evolution of masculine violence and the social acceptability of such expressions cannot be understood without considering the shifting power dynamics and societal norms that influenced male behaviour. In different historical contexts, violence served various roles, often as a means of enforcing social hierarchies or addressing grievances. The medieval knight who upheld his honour on the battlefield represented not just individual bravery but also his status and the expectations of chivalric culture, which glorified martial prowess as a virtue. In this sense, violence was a performance intended to validate one’s position within a rigid social order. As we moved into the Renaissance and into the Age of Enlightenment, the nuances of honour began to intermingle with emerging notions of civility. This period saw the rise of the gentleman ideal, which sought to redefine masculinity in terms of education, refinement, and emotional restraint. A gentleman, unlike the brutish warrior, was expected to resolve conflicts through negotiation rather than bloodshed. However, this did not negate the underlying belief in the importance of honour; rather, it complicated expressions of masculinity, creating a duality that persisted across social strata. As class distinctions became more pronounced, interpretations of honour and the appropriateness of violence morphed, reflecting the contexts in which different groups found themselves. The higher classes could afford to invoke gentlemanly conduct while the lower classes were often caricatured as brutish or uncivilized, subject to harsher scrutiny for their violent behaviours. This societal judgement resulted in a type of cultural policing where the working class was disproportionately penalized for acts of violence, reflecting the controlling narrative of the time. Consequently, the average labourer fighting for his honour was often portrayed as a threat, while the gentry were afforded leeway to engage in duelling or brawling as a means of addressing slights. The legal and social ramifications further entrenched this disparity; thus, the act of fighting transformed not only in its nature but also in its societal implications based on class. The shift to industrialisation exacerbated these tensions as economic struggles intensified, leading to increased frustrations and conflicts among the working classes. Herein lay a paradox: while the vocabulary of honour remained a powerful tool, the way it manifested in violent encounters became distinctly classed. The very act of asserting masculine identity through violence thus became weaponized by socio-economic factors, further entrenching divisions and perpetuating cycles of violence that reflected wider societal issues. As the lens through which masculine honour was viewed evolved, so too did the societal expectations placed upon men, culminating in a complex interplay of identity, class, and violence that shaped the historical narrative. Such a framework enables a deeper understanding of how different expressions of masculinity have been constructed, performed, and critiqued, leading to various repercussions for both individual men and broader societal structures.
