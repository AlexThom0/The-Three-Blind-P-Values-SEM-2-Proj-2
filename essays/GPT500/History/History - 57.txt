The prevailing belief was that the act of reading could evoke emotional responses so intense that they might lead individuals, especially women, to lose their grip on reality. Consequently, this discourse framed the act of reading as a double-edged sword — a source of enlightenment and knowledge on one side, yet a potential gateway to madness on the other. Critics likened the experience of reading to intoxication, insinuating that literature could intoxicate the mind, leading to irrational behavior similarly to how alcohol might impair judgment. This portrayal fed into a broader societal anxiety about women's emerging literacy and their increased engagement with literature, which some men viewed as a threat to traditional social hierarchies and gender roles. Women readers, in particular, were seen as vulnerable to the dangers of unmoderated consumption of novels, where emotional experiences could seep from the pages into their own lives, potentially destabilizing the rigid confines of domesticity expected of them. Consequently, authors of the time often reinforced the notion that reading could lead to moral and emotional deterioration, particularly in young women, who were frequently depicted in literature as frail and overly sentimental characters. Works addressing female audiences often contained didactic elements aimed at cautioning against the seductions of fiction, urging women to temper their enthusiasm for novels with the prudence befitting their gender. This emerging anxiety was further amplified by specific literary cases in which narratives illustrated dire consequences related to excessive reading. The focus on individual responsibility in such narratives also reflected the larger social tensions of the period, as society grappled with rapidly changing notions of identity and agency. The perceived danger of novels transformed into a broader cultural critique, with some advocating for literary censorship, believing that certain texts could dangerously awaken repressed emotions within their readers, leading them toward illicit desires or depressive states. The psychological implications of this discourse were profound, as they suggested that literature held a unique power over the mind's workings. The metaphor of contagion, often employed by critics, implied that emotional responses could spread from reader to reader, creating a collective experience of turmoil. Consequently, authors faced scrutiny not just for their storytelling skills, but for the potential moral failings inherent in their plots and character developments, which might endanger their readership. Thus, the act of reading became entwined with the larger debate on morality, mental health, and the role of women in society, marking a complex intersection of literature and societal values.
