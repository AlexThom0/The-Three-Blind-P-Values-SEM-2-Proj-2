This skepticism was compounded by the inherently interdisciplinary nature of peace studies, drawing from sociology, political science, psychology, and international relations, among other fields. Yet, these varied perspectives enriched the discourse, facilitating a more holistic understanding of conflicts and the pathways to resolution. In particular, the contributions of feminist theorists began to emerge, challenging traditional notions of security that prioritized militaristic approaches. They posited that genuine peace cannot merely be the absence of war but must also encompass social justice and human rights, integrating the voices and experiences of marginalized groups. Moreover, the rise of grassroots movements illustrated a different dimension of peacebuilding. Local communities, often directly affected by conflict, demonstrated their capabilities in fostering dialogue and reconciliation. Their initiatives highlighted the necessity of bottom-up approaches, which contrasted sharply with top-down peace processes that frequently ignored the lived realities of those on the ground. Analyzing case studies from various conflicts, scholars began to recognize that sustainable peace required not just political agreements but also social cohesion, a shift in cultural narratives, and the dismantling of systemic inequalities. In tandem with these developments, the discourse around peace began to address the complex interplay between peace and justice. Scholars and practitioners wrestled with the term "transitional justice," advocating for mechanisms that could appropriately address past atrocities while laying the groundwork for future harmony. The insight that peace without justice could lead to resentment and re-emergent violence propelled further inquiry into restorative practices. It became increasingly clear that healing societal wounds necessitated not only negotiation but also acknowledgment of past wrongs and active efforts toward reparations. In this milieu, empirical research played a pivotal role. By evaluating the outcomes of various peace initiatives and conflict transformation programs, advocates sought to empirically substantiate the effectiveness of nonviolent approaches. This growing body of evidence countered long-established beliefs in the inevitability of violence, establishing a more nuanced understanding of conflict dynamics. Methods such as participatory action research empowered affected communities, allowing their voices to inform theoretical developments. Furthermore, the globalization of communication technologies facilitated the rapid spread of information about successful peace efforts worldwide. Local movements were no longer isolated; rather, they formed part of a global tapestry of resistance against violence and injustice. Online platforms became important spaces for advocacy, fostering international solidarity and providing a platform for sharing strategies that worked. Thus, through the confluence of theory and practice, peace studies evolved into a vibrant field, marked by a commitment to understanding conflict in all its complexity and promoting pathways to enduring peace. The academic landscape transformed, moving beyond critiques to propose actionable solutions rooted in the realities of diverse communities worldwide, emphasizing that peace is an ongoing process requiring collective effort and commitment.
