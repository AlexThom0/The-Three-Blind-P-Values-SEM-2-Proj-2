Higiro believed that the principles he witnessed at Auschwitz could be adapted to the Rwandan context, where the scars of genocide remain deeply embedded in the social fabric. Inspired by the cathartic nature of dialogue and the emphasis on shared humanity, he began to envision a modernized form of council that would prioritize healing over punishment. His idea revolved around fostering an environment where victims and perpetrators could converge, allowing their stories to be heard without the fear of judgment. This reflective dialogue was perceived as essential not merely for reconciliation, but for breaking the pervasive cycles of silence and resentment that afflicted families and communities torn apart by violence. The initiative aimed to leverage the transformative power of storytelling as a means of confronting the past and forging new relationships. As he returned to Rwanda, he faced the challenge of cultural adaptation. Traditional methods of dispute resolution, such as gacaca courts, had lined the path for addressing the aftermath of the genocide but often fell short of fostering deeper understanding or addressing the emotional wounds of those affected. Higiro recognized that a more nuanced approach was necessary: one that would integrate lessons from both Rwandan history and global practices of restorative justice. His model would employ storytelling not just to recount individual traumas, but also to highlight shared experiences—emphasizing the interconnectedness of the Rwandan narrative, regardless of how one viewed their position on the moral spectrum of the genocide. To validity this approach, Higiro sought partnerships with local community leaders, educators, and mental health professionals who could help him tailor the council system to the values and norms intrinsic to Rwandan society. He understood that any initiative that sought to bridge the deep divides created by the genocide must resonate with the population. This meant incorporating cultural traditions that honored elders and established a sense of kinship among participants. By framing the council as an integral part of communal healing, rather than an outsider imposition, Higiro hoped to build a foundation of trust and acceptance. Moreover, recognizing the psychological needs of both victims and perpetrators was vital. The concept of Ubuntu, which articulates the idea that individual well-being is intrinsically tied to the well-being of the community, provided a philosophical undercurrent to Higiro’s vision. In this light, the platform for dialogue would serve as a crucible for restoring dignity to those affected, allowing them to reclaim their narratives in a way that underscored shared humanity rather than deepening divisions. This perspective made it possible for participants to engage in frank discussions about guilt, pain, and forgiveness, embodying a rare opportunity for collective catharsis. As this initiative took shape, Higiro remained committed to demonstrating that healing is a multifaceted process, one that requires not only the acknowledgment of past wrongs but also the pursuit of a compassionate understanding among all involved. Each council meeting aimed to cultivate an atmosphere of respect and openness, inviting participants to assume not only the role of speaker but also that of listener, thereby reshaping the discourse surrounding regret and resentment into one characterized by empathy and hope.
