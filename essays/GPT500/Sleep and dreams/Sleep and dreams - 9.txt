Research emphasizes metrics, quantifying sleep cycles and patterns, often reducing the richness of the experience to mere data points. The individual’s narrative is lost within these clinical frameworks, where the qualitative aspects of sleep—its mysteries, its dreams—are overshadowed by an obsessive focus on duration and efficiency. What was once a deeply personal and often spiritual aspect of human life is now regulated by sleep studies and prescriptions, relegating natural rhythms to the background. In this world, we become consumers of sleep, purchasing products that promise unblemished rest, filtering our consciousness through devices designed to optimize every moment spent unconscious. This commodification speaks to a broader societal issue: the loss of intuitive, embodied knowledge in favor of artificial constructs. We create gadgets that track heart rates and cycles, yet how often do we stop to tune into our own bodies and their needs? The importance of rituals surrounding sleep is increasingly neglected in our rush towards efficiency; the bedtime stories, calming teas, and sacred spaces of earlier times give way to screens and unyielding timetables. The irony is palpable: in an age of unprecedented connectivity and access to information, our understanding of one of the most fundamental human experiences is diminished. Moreover, the relentless pursuit for productivity fuels a culture that shuns any sign of vulnerability associated with fatigue, dismissing exhaustion as a weakness rather than recognising it as a universal human need. In this context, sleep takes on a dual identity—both essential for well-being and a barrier to relentless productivity. The dissonance grows; we grapple with a societal message that glorifies perpetual motion while undermining the very need for restorative rest. A regression occurs, not as a return to the wisdom of ancient cultures, but as a stark movement away from the symbiotic relationship with our natural surroundings. Sleep becomes merely another arena in which performance is evaluated, further entrenching the cycles of anxiety that keep us awake. Those who dare prioritize rest face judgement for not contributing enough to the dominant narratives of achievement. Thus, the very act of claiming time for sleep becomes an act of resistance. Understanding sleep as a holistic experience rather than a mere physiological function may be our antidote to this malaise. Reclaiming sleep means reestablishing our relationship with it, recognizing it as a fundamental element of our humanity deserving of respect and reverence. It requires a cultural shift, one that invites us to embrace the twilight between wakefulness and slumber, to see it not as wasteful but as nourishing, vital, and essential to the fabric of our lives.
