The connection Widgery draws between local religious practices and public health issues reflects a broader colonial narrative, where the perceived backwardness of Indian customs was often highlighted as justification for British intervention. This framing painted the indigenous population as incapable of managing their societal challenges, further entrenching colonial ideologies of superiority. The dog’s plight, caught between cultural reverence and the practical need for control, underscores the tensions inherent in colonial encounters. Such perspectives not only influenced public opinion but also affected policy decisions, fostering a climate of disdain that contributed to the marginalization of specific communities, including the Jains.
