Berger's reflections highlight a deeper contradiction at play: while these institutions were designed to elevate public understanding and appreciation of wildlife, they often failed to provide a genuine connection to the animals themselves. The carefully curated exhibits, with their easy accessibility and aesthetic considerations, masked the reality of confinement and isolation that the animals experienced. Spectators, drawn by the promise of enlightenment, often found little more than a display of their own expectations projected onto the silent creatures. This disconnect only grew as the spectacle of nature was filtered through the lens of human amusement, raising questions about the ethics of such portrayals.
