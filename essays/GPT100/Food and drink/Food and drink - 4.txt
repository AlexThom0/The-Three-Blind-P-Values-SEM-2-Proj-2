This aversion to the communal environment of taverns highlights a critical tension in colonial society: the division between the elite and the masses. For the upper class, these establishments represented not just a place to rest, but also a space where social order could be challenged by the very people they sought to separate themselves from. This discomfort played a crucial role in shaping early American attitudes toward public spaces, as the elite grappled with their ambivalence towards a culture that demanded interaction with the "lower" classes. Thus, the tavern emerged as both a necessary convenience and a site of social anxiety for the privileged.
