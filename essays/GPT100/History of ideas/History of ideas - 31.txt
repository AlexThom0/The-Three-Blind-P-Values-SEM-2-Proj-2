As literacy rates increased and scholarly work circulated more widely, there emerged a distinct sense of shared identity among Muslims across vast geographical divides. This new consciousness was fueled by journals, pamphlets, and speeches that not only articulated the injustices faced by Muslims but also emphasized their rich historical legacy and cultural unity. Despite these developments, the actual mobilization of Pan-Islamism into a cohesive movement proved elusive, reflecting the complexities of regional politics and varying national interests.
