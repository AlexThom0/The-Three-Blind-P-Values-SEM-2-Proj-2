Colonial powers quickly recognized that satisfying Indian demands for weaponry was not merely a matter of offering munitions; it involved negotiating complex social relationships that shaped military alliances. The ability of Native groups to dictate terms stemmed from their strategic importance in regional conflicts, where their choices influenced the balance of power. This intersection of commerce and diplomacy allowed Indian tribes not only to procure arms but also to assert agency over colonial strategies. Consequently, the colonial desire for military expansion became entwined with the understanding that reliance on Indian warriors necessitated a reciprocal relationship.
