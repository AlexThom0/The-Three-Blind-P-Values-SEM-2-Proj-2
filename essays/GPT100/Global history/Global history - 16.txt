In these economically charged environments, stereotypes around Jewish financial practices were further fueled by pervasive cultural narratives. As Conversos assimilated, their dual identities often blurred lines, leading some to scrutinize their interactions with established Christian merchants. This intermingling gave rise to anxieties about fidelity to economic ethics, as accusations of usury became a convenient means for discrediting rivals. Such tensions were compounded by the fundamental tensions in capitalist expansion, where moral and ethical considerations frequently clashed with profit motives. Thus, the portrayal of Jews—in addition to serving as scapegoats—reflected deeper societal fears and the complexities of economic relationships during this tumultuous period.
