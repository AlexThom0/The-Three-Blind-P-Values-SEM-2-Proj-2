This approach, rich in personal experience, is often dismissed within traditional educational frameworks, which prioritize analytical and abstract modes of expression deemed more 'academic.' As a result, children from working-class backgrounds may struggle to engage with curricula that appear alien to their lived realities. This mismatch not only undermines their confidence but also perpetuates the cycle of inequality, suggesting that the very structure of education must be evaluated to better serve diverse linguistic and cultural backgrounds. Effective educational reform necessitates a broader understanding of language's role in learning.
