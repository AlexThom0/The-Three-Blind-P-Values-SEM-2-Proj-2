For Friedman, the emphasis on individual responsibility in investing in one's own skills and education highlighted personal agency and freedom, aligning closely with capitalist principles. He believed that viewing education as a means to enhance one’s productivity not only justified private investment but also celebrated the notion of self-improvement through personal effort. This perspective inherently positioned individuals as economic agents capable of shaping their destinies, in stark contrast to any interpretation that leaned toward communal obligations or state intervention, which he feared could undermine the foundational tenets of a free-market economy.
