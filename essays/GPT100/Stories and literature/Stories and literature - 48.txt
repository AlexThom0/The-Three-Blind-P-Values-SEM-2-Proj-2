The translation of non-Western texts was a double-edged sword, reflecting both an exchange of ideas and a form of cultural appropriation. European colonial officials often operated under the guise of enlightenment, presenting themselves as civilizers while simultaneously diminishing the very cultures they sought to engage. This dynamic fostered a selective appreciation of literature, where only those works deemed palatable to Western sensibilities were propagated. Consequently, the literature that emerged from this context was not merely a reflection of diverse voices but rather a filtered narrative, primarily serving the ideological interests of colonial powers. Thus, the landscape of world literature became intricately linked with economic and political motivations.
