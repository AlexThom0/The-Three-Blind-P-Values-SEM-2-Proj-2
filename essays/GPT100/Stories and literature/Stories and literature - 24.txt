He believed that through self-examination and introspection found within such texts, Beckett might uncover deeper insights into his own suffering. This approach reflected a broader trend among writers of the time, who increasingly sought solace in spiritual introspection rather than conventional therapeutic practices. MacGreevy’s suggestion was not merely a recommendation but an attempt to initiate a journey towards reconciliation with the self, an embrace of life's complexities that might transcend the limitations of both psychology and outright despair.
