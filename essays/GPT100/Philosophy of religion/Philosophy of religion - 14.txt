While Buddhism embraced a cyclical view of existence where individuals could learn and grow from their past actions, Augustine's perspective imposed a linear, definitive judgment that rendered any chance for redemption moot after death. This emphasis on a singular, unchangeable outcome intensified the moral weight of one's earthly choices, creating a fear-based framework that diverged sharply from the more forgiving nature of karmic rebirth. Such contrasting ideologies shaped not only spiritual beliefs but also influenced cultural attitudes toward morality and ethics across civilizations.
