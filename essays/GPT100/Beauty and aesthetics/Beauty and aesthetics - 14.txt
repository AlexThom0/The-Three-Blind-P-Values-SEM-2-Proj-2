By presenting a mass-produced object as art, Duchamp questioned the very values that dictated what could be considered worthy of aesthetic appreciation. This act disrupted traditional hierarchies, shifting the focus from artistic skill to the conceptual framework surrounding art. In doing so, Duchamp not only contested the labor-intensive processes glorified by previous movements but also prompted a critical re-evaluation of authenticity and originality. This shift laid the groundwork for subsequent avant-garde movements, opening new avenues for artists to explore identity, meaning, and the relationship between art and society, which would reverberate through future generations.
