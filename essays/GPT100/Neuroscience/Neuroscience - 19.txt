This concept of kindling suggests that each subsequent episode is not merely a reaction to a preceding life event, but rather part of an escalating cycle fueled by neural changes. As the brain adapts to repeated stressors, its baseline state evolves, making it more susceptible to depressive episodes. This phenomenon might also explain why some individuals experience severe, chronic forms of depression after only a few significant life stresses, highlighting the interplay between environmental triggers and underlying biological vulnerabilities. Understanding this process raises critical questions about intervention strategies and the potential for resilience in those prone to recurrent mood disorders.
