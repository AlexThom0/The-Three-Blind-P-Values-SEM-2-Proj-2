This focus on export-oriented agriculture created a vulnerability to climatic shifts, as local needs were subordinated to global markets. In the face of such policies, communities struggled to adapt, their age-old methods of managing water and soil increasingly inadequate against external pressures. Consequently, when the rains failed, starvation was not merely an act of nature but a predictable outcome of decisions made far from the fields where the suffering unfolded. The link between imperial intent and local despair became painfully evident.
