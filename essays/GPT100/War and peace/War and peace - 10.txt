The legacy of colonial narratives continues to shape perceptions of Iraq’s viability as a cohesive state. Such discourses often overlook the resilience and agency of local communities, which have adapted to shifting political landscapes while maintaining their cultural identities. The complex interplay of religion, ethnicity, and political allegiance in these communities cannot be reduced to mere sectarian divides; instead, it reflects a history of negotiation and survival amidst external pressures. As Iraq navigates its post-colonial reality, understanding these dynamics is crucial in fostering inclusive governance that truly represents its diverse populace.
