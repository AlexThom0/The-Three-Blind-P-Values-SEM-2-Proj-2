By analyzing testimonies and behavioral patterns, we reveal that individuals often act not merely out of coercion or financial incentives but rather from deeply rooted beliefs and identities. This profound connection to their cause, framed by historical and societal narratives, underscores the complexity of human motivation in conflict. It challenges traditional views that reduce combatants to mere rational actors, suggesting instead that emotional and symbolic factors play critical roles in shaping decisions. Understanding these dimensions is crucial for developing effective policy responses.
