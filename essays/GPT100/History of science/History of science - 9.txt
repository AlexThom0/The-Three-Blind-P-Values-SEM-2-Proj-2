Patients were often subjected to ECT as a last resort, with clinicians desperate to alleviate suffering framed within a paradigm of clinical necessity. Despite the potential horrors associated with the treatment, especially its physical and psychological impacts, it was seen as a breakthrough, a way to offer hope where none seemed possible. The enthusiasm for ECT among some in the psychiatric community stemmed not only from the dire circumstances many patients faced but also from a lack of alternative therapies. In this context, ECT was perceived as a beacon of possibility, leading practitioners to overlook its ethical implications in the pursuit of effective intervention.
