Critics argued that these methods further marginalized individuals, stripping them of identity and autonomy under the guise of treatment. In this climate of dissent, figures like Thomas Szasz and R.D. Laing emerged, advocating for a more humane understanding of mental illness that emphasized the social and political dimensions of psychological distress, rather than merely biomedical explanations. Their criticisms catalyzed a reevaluation of therapeutic practices, leading to a search for alternatives that would prioritize patient dignity over compliance with societal norms.
