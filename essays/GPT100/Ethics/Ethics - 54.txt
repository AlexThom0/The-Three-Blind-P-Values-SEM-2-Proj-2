Historical evidence reveals that societies with rigid in-group versus out-group distinctions have often outcompeted more inclusive groups, fostering an evolutionary advantage rooted in loyalty and mutual aid among members. Thus, moral frameworks have likely been shaped not only by the necessity of cooperation within groups but also by the competitive dynamics that place external groups in a position of threat. This duality reinforces the notion that moral systems prioritize alliances and kinship over universal altruism, steering behavioral tendencies toward the defense of group interests.
