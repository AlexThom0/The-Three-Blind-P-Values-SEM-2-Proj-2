Morality, as a construct, must be grounded in something more consistent than divine whim; it requires a framework that asserts the inherent value of human life. This framework could arise from rational considerations, empathy, or social contracts, all of which provide compelling reasons to regard certain actions as universally unacceptable. By distinguishing between actions that are wrong in themselves and those that are simply prohibited by an authority, we create a more robust moral landscape. Ultimately, authentic moral imperatives withstand the test of divine endorsement, affirming their validity independent of external sanction.
