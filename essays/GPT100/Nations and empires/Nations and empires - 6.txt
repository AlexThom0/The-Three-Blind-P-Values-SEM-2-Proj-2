Quakers leveraged their networks to advocate for abolition, while Evangelical Protestants framed the moral imperative to end slavery as part of a broader mission to spread Christianity. Intellectuals began questioning not only the economic justifications for the institution but also its ethical implications, prompting a societal reassessment of colonial practices. This convergence of economic critiques and moral outrage gradually shifted public opinion, laying the groundwork for a growing abolitionist movement that would challenge the foundations of Empire itself.
