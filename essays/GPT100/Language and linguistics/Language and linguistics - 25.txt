Advocates saw the recognition of Black Urban Vernacular as a step towards validating the cultural backgrounds of students, asserting that language shapes self-perception and communal belonging. By integrating Ebonics into the curriculum, they posited that schools could empower students to embrace their identities while providing a bridge to academic success. However, this recognition met fierce resistance, underpinned by the belief that privileging Ebonics would somehow undermine the value of conventional English, reinforcing a narrative that equated linguistic diversity with inferiority.
