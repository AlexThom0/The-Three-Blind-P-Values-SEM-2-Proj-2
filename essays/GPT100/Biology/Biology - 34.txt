This concept revolves around the idea of emergent properties, where the interactions of simpler elements lead to complex systems. Instead of relying solely on genetic information, these systems exhibit behaviors shaped by their environments and the relationships among individual components. For example, in a habitat where resources fluctuate unpredictably, cells and organisms that can adjust their structure and function in response to immediate conditions are more likely to thrive. This adaptability highlights a fundamental principle in biology: survival often hinges on the capacity to forge new connections and configurations, rather than merely the inherited genetic blueprint.
