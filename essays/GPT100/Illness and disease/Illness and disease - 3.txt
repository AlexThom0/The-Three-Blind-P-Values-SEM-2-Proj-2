This paradox suggested a complex interplay of psychological and physiological factors, with many patients reporting significant distress related to their symptoms. The absence of objective markers did not deter the growing community, as anecdotal evidence and patient testimonials fueled theories about environmental triggers. Some practitioners posited that societal changes, such as increased urbanization and industrialization, contributed to a heightened awareness and sensitivity to previously tolerated substances. This environment created fertile ground for both the clinical and popular recognition of multiple chemical sensitivities as a legitimate concern.
