While psychoanalysis emphasized subjective experience and personal narrative, intelligence-testing moved towards a more rational and quantifiable understanding, often sidelining individual stories in favor of group dynamics. This tension influenced perceptions of childhood disorders, leading many to view symptoms through the prism of established theories rather than the lived realities of children and their families. As traditional family structures faced pressures from modernity, the challenge of integrating these differing perspectives on child development became increasingly relevant, paving the way for new frameworks and discussions surrounding mental health in children.
