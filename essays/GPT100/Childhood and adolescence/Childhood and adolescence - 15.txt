As children, our brains are wired to process experiences differently, relying on a more basic level of understanding and emotional resonance. By the time we reach adulthood, the neural pathways that once facilitated these early memories may have weakened or been restructured entirely. Furthermore, the narratives we later construct about our childhood are influenced by societal expectations and shared familial stories, creating a dissonance between what we once experienced and what we now believe to be true.
