This shift not only transformed the role of authors but also redefined the boundaries of creative expression. The authority once held by oral traditions began to wane, giving way to the singular voice of the writer. Consequently, with the advent of the printing press, texts could be reproduced with increasing fidelity, allowing for consistency in storytelling that oral recitals lacked. This emergence of the "author" as a distinct entity altered how society viewed intellectual property, paving the way for the notion of originality. Thus, the act of writing became not merely a craft but a means of asserting one's identity and influence in a rapidly evolving cultural landscape.
