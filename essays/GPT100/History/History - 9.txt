A closed smile was often associated with dignity and self-restraint, reflecting societal norms that equated openness with vulnerability. This reticence mirrored broader, deeply ingrained values regarding personal presentation in public life, where revealing too much emotion or physical imperfection was disdained. Art served not just as representation but as a blueprint for behavior, reinforcing ideals of refinement. The prevalence of the closed smile thus illustrates how art and societal expectations intertwine, shaping not only aesthetics but also the underlying psychology of how people wished to be perceived.
