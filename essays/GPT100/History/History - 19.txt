This quest for detectable trends was not limited to economic indicators but extended to social and cultural phenomena as well. Scholars began analyzing shifts in ideology, technology, and communication as indicative of larger societal changes. For example, cultural historians investigated how art movements paralleled economic developments, suggesting that societal conditions influenced cultural expressions. The interconnectedness of these disciplines led to a multidisciplinary approach, where economics, sociology, and history informed each other. This fluid exchange fostered a deeper understanding of civilization's trajectory, challenging the previously held notion of linear progress and instead proposing a more cyclical view of historical development.
