Szasz's discontent with the prevailing attitudes in psychoanalysis fueled his critiques of the very system he had once joined. He observed that the field’s insistence on categorizing individuals as ill undermined personal responsibility and autonomy. This contradiction left him feeling increasingly alienated from his peers, whom he viewed as complicit in a coercive paradigm that stifled genuine human agency. By prioritizing mental illness as a biological condition, Szasz believed they diluted the moral dimensions of human behavior and disregarded the complexities of choice and volition. This tension between his libertarian ideals and the mainstream psychoanalytic doctrine drove his subsequent work.
