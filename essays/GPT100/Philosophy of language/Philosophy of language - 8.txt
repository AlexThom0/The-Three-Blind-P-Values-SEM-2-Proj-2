Chomsky's insistence on recursion as a universal feature overlooks languages that utilize alternative mechanisms for structuring meaning. These alternatives can serve similar communicative functions, suggesting that the human capacity for language is not limited to a singular framework. Additionally, linguistic research has increasingly highlighted the role of social interaction and environmental context, reinforcing the idea that language evolves in response to cultural dynamics. This broader perspective not only enriches our understanding of linguistic variation but also challenges the rigidity of Chomsky's model, inviting a re-examination of what constitutes the essence of language itself.
