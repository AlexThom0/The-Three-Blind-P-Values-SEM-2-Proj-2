When subjects recognized the authority behind the correction, they were less likely to revert to their misconceptions. Conversely, if the original misleading claims had been popularized by trusted figures, even well-articulated corrections risked being overshadowed. This dynamic illustrates not just the potency of familiarity in shaping belief, but also the critical role of trust in information dissemination. Consequently, understanding how source credibility interacts with memory’s malleability becomes essential in framing effective corrective messages.
