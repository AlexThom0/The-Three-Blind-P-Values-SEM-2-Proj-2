Christians’ refusal to participate in public rites alienated them further, positioning them as outsiders in a society where communal ritual was the bedrock of identity. This rejection signified not only a disavowal of established traditions but also a challenge to societal norms that dictated participation in civic life through religious observance. The secrecy surrounding their practices fueled misconceptions and bred suspicion. Public curiosity and fear took shape around their gatherings, often leading to slanderous accusations and a deepening divide. Thus, Christians were not just misunderstood; they became targets of social isolation, leading to an environment ripe for conflict.
