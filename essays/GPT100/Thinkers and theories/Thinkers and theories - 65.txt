This divergence highlights a fundamental question in philosophy: is the integrity of one’s ideas worth dying for, or does the true testament of an idea lie in its endurance through life? Hume, much like Aristotle, understood that participating in the discourse of life—exchanging ideas, engaging with others—often necessitated compromise. Thus, he embraced a flexible approach, recognizing that surviving intellectually could be more impactful than adhering rigidly to principle in the face of ignorance.
