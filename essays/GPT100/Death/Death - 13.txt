During the Renaissance, the merging of classical ideals and Christian doctrine influenced not only the perception of death but also the rituals surrounding it. This era saw a shift toward a more personal and introspective approach to dying, where the focus lay on individual preparation and moral reckoning. The concept of a ‘good death’ began to incorporate the idea of repentance and spiritual readiness, reflecting a society increasingly engaged in questions of the soul's fate. As people faced mortality, the guidance provided by these texts became vital in navigating the complexities of both worldly and spiritual legacies, bridging ancient philosophies with contemporary beliefs.
