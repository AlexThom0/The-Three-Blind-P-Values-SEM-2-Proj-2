In many primate species, the challenges of reproduction extend beyond those faced by other mammals. They experience prolonged gestation periods that can lead to increased maternal stress, and their young are often born helpless, requiring significant investment in care. This investment is not merely biological but involves complex social structures where maternal ranking can influence survival rates. For instance, a higher-status female may access more resources, which could enhance her offspring's chances. These dynamics contribute to a more competitive and perilous landscape for primate mothers, underscoring how social hierarchies intertwine with reproductive health and outcomes.
