The perception of usury as a moral failing was, therefore, often inconsistent with practice. Wealthy merchants and financiers developed complex financial instruments that allowed them to circumvent prohibitions while still profiting from loans. This distinction between theory and reality reflected a broader societal tension: the desire for equitable economic relationships clashed with the inevitable pursuit of profit. As a result, the rules governing interest were applied selectively, creating a dichotomy that favored those who could manipulate the system. Such practices not only perpetuated inequality but also set the stage for the evolution of financial systems that prioritized capital accumulation over community support.
