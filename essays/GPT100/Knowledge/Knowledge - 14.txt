Participants' reactions reveal a deep-seated bias in moral reasoning. When faced with harm, individuals are quick to attribute intent, suggesting a tendency to view negative outcomes through a moral lens that presupposes agency. Conversely, the perception of positive outcomes shifts this perspective, leading many to attribute those results to chance rather than intention. This discrepancy highlights how framing and context can influence moral judgments, challenging the notion of objective causal relationships. Such findings point to an inherent complexity in the interplay between intent and consequence, revealing that our moral intuitions are profoundly shaped by the narratives we accept.
