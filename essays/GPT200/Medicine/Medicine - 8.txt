In addition, antibiotics could be produced in large quantities through chemical synthesis, offering a consistency in treatment that phage therapy could not match. The ease of administration and widespread applicability of antibiotics further fueled their dominance in clinical practice. This led to an exponential increase in their use, creating a feedback loop where the more they were used, the more resistant bacterial strains became. Resistance started to emerge due to the selective pressure antibiotics exerted on microbial populations, giving rise to superbugs that defied conventional treatments. Researchers began to recognize the shortcomings of relying solely on antibiotics, prompting a renewed interest in alternative therapies, including phage therapy. This shift in perspective highlighted the importance of exploring various treatment modalities and understanding the ecological dynamics of bacteria and their phages. As the battle against resistant infections became increasingly urgent, the interplay between these two approaches—the robust arsenal of antibiotics and the more tailored interventions offered by phage therapy—became central to developing comprehensive treatment strategies for bacterial infections. These developments underscored the need for innovation in tackling emerging health crises.
