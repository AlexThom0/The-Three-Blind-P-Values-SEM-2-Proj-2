This perception of northern peoples was shaped not only by firsthand accounts but also by a blend of hearsay and literary embellishment. Roman writers often romanticized the wildness of these regions, painting them as both exotic and perilous. For instance, the stories of fierce warriors and uncivilized practices contributed to a narrative that justified Roman domination. Strabo and Pliny’s accounts often echoed with dramatic descriptions that hinted at a land untouched by civilization. This fascination with the unknown also fueled the desire for exploration, as the Romans sought to extend their empire into these shadowy territories. Concurrently, the cultural exchange between Rome and the northern tribes began to surface, as trade and diplomacy introduced new ideas and goods. However, despite the growing interaction, a palpable tension remained; the invocations of Thule served as reminders of the limits of Roman power and the allure of the untamed world. Thus, the interaction of myth and reality created a complex lens through which the Romans viewed the northern tribes. The duality of admiration and fear deeply informed their narratives as they sought to understand the far reaches of their expanding empire.
