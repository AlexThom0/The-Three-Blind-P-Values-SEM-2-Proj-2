This narrative not only served to erase the historical agency of the Indigenous peoples but also positioned them as relics of a past civilization, a notion that allowed settlers to perceive their own expansion as a form of civilizational progress. By denying continuity and connection to these monumental achievements, settler colonial discourse constructed a fabricated history that justified land dispossession and cultural erasure. Such interpretations ignored the rich oral traditions and histories of the Native populations, which persistently articulate their deep ties to the land and its monumental heritage. As archaeological discoveries continue to challenge conventional timelines of migration and settlement, scholars increasingly advocate for recognizing Indigenous narratives as foundational to understanding these ancient sites. Emphasizing the ongoing relationship between Native peoples and their ancestors' legacies disrupts the Eurocentric accounts that have dominated historical scholarship. In doing so, we acknowledge that these cultural histories are not artifacts of a lost civilization but, rather, are integral to the contemporary identity and resilience of Indigenous communities today. This reevaluation compels a broader recognition of the complexities surrounding our understanding of who inhabited the Americas and when.
