Despite the intentions behind welfare regulations, their framework often overlooks the inherent needs and rights of animals, reducing them to mere objects for human benefit. These regulations assume a hierarchical relationship, where humans dictate the terms of acceptable treatment based on utilitarian calculations. Individual animals, with their unique emotional and psychological complexities, become abstractions within legal and ethical discussions, stripped of their agency. This dehumanizing perspective reinforces the notion that animals exist primarily to serve human interests, regardless of their suffering. For instance, species such as elephants, dolphins, and primates display profound social bonds and emotional depth, yet under welfare statutes, they may still be confined to environments that fail to respect their natural behaviors and instincts. In contrast, a rights-based framework acknowledges that animals possess intrinsic value beyond their utility, prompting a shift in perspective that recognizes their autonomy and need for freedom. This paradigm invites a radical rethinking of our interactions with nonhuman beings and challenges the societal norms that permit their exploitation. Acknowledging animals as rights-bearing entities thus not only humanizes them but also revolutionizes the ethical landscape in which we engage with all sentient beings.
