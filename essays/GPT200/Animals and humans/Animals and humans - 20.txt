Berger's critique reflects a broader societal ambivalence toward the representation of animals in captivity, particularly as zoos evolved from elite curiosities to public institutions. Despite the noble aspirations behind these establishments, including fostering a greater understanding of biodiversity and conservation, the reality often fell short. Visitors, in their quest for enlightenment, frequently encountered a stark disconnection between the creatures on display and their natural behaviors. The limitations of the environment and the inherent stress of captivity stripped these animals of their autonomy, reducing vibrant beings to mere objects of fascination. This paradox not only invites questions about our role as observers but also challenges the ethical frameworks that justify such institutions. While proponents hailed zoos as educational resources, critics argued that they presented a skewed narrative, focusing on superficial experiences of interaction rather than genuine comprehension of wildlife. Over time, this tension has fueled debates among animal rights activists, conservationists, and the public regarding the true purpose of zoos—should they serve primarily as entertainment, or should they prioritize the well-being and preservation of the species they display? As these conversations continue, they underscore the complexities surrounding our engagement with the animal world and our responsibilities toward it.
