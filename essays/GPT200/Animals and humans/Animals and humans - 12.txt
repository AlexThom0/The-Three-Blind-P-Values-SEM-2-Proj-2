This assumption of an inevitable anthropoid evolution reveals a profound anthropocentrism that continues to influence our understanding of intelligence across the cosmos. The underlying belief is that evolution, driven by the competitive force of natural selection, must yield something recognizable to us. However, this perspective dismisses the complexity and diversity of evolutionary pathways. Repeatedly, we see that intelligence can manifest in forms completely alien to our understanding; it might function based on ecological niches that bear little resemblance to terrestrial models. This raises critical questions about what it means to be intelligent, as well as the criteria we use to recognize intelligence in non-human entities. The historical lens through which thinkers like Huygens and Saint-Simon viewed the universe reflects a broader trend of projecting human traits onto the cosmos rather than considering the possibility of entirely different frameworks of existence. As our knowledge of life on Earth expands—encompassing intelligence in cephalopods, the intricate communication of cetaceans, and the social structures of insects—the scope of evolution's creativity becomes apparent, suggesting that we are but one variation in a vast spectrum of potential intelligence. Such revelations challenge the myth of humanity's unique preeminence.
