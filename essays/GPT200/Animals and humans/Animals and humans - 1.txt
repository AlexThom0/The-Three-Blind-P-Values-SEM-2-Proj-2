Widgery's critique reveals a broader colonial mindset that not only vilified certain communities but also projected Western moral standards onto Indian society. The juxtaposition of local practices with European ideals often led to a pathologization of non-European cultures. This imperial gaze sanctioned the view that Jains’ reverence for life was an irrational impediment to urban order and hygiene. Such framing effectively stripped communities of agency, recasting them as subjects of civilizing missions justified by perceived moral superiority. The disparagement of dogs as symbols of disorder further reflects this colonial dichotomy, where native practices were depicted as primitive and repugnant. In this light, stray dogs became not just a nuisance, but a tangible representation of cultural decay that colonial powers believed needed remedying. This dynamic also elucidates the shifting relationships between humans and animals under colonial governance, as dogs were seen through a utilitarian lens that bolstered colonial authority. Consequently, the manipulation of language and imagery in these narratives served to reinforce racial hierarchies, fostering a sense of superiority that fed into broader colonial hierarchies. Thus, in seeking to critique what was deemed irrational, Widgery and others inadvertently revealed the deeper anxieties that characterized colonial encounters.
