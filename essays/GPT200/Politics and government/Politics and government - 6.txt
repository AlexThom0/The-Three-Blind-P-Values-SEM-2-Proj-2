This competitive dynamic can lead to a race to the bottom, where countries sacrifice social welfare for the sake of attracting foreign investment. As a result, the very architecture of the liberal order, which ostensibly promotes equality and shared prosperity, tends to entrench inequality by prioritizing capital over labor. Workers find themselves marginalized, with limited bargaining power and shrinking safety nets. This precarious environment breeds discontent, fueling populist movements that challenge traditional parties unable to address these grievances. In turn, political discourse becomes polarized, with parties resorting to rhetoric that often exacerbates societal divides. The implications are profound: while some segments of the population benefit from deregulation and tax breaks, the majority face economic insecurity. Ultimately, this disparity undermines the foundations of democracy, as disaffected citizens may withdraw from political engagement, believing their voices are unheard and their needs unaddressed. This disenchantment poses a significant risk, as the stability of democratic institutions increasingly hinges on the public's perception of government efficacy and fairness.
