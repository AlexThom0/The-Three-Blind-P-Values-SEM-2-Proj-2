This dual existence fostered a unique form of relation to authority, as the lives of these households were directly influenced by imperial edicts and local governance. For instance, military households often bore heavier taxation burdens, which led to significant resentment among them. The intricate web of obligations and benefits inherent in being part of a junhu resulted in complex social dynamics. While some families benefited from military service through land allocations and tax exemptions, others viewed their status as a perpetual threat, especially during times of famines or unrest. Moreover, the presence of military households in local communities often meant that the relationship between civilians and soldiers was not purely adversarial; rather, it was characterized by a mix of dependency, rivalry, and negotiation. This blurred line between military and civilian life sheds light on how individuals navigated their roles within the broader socio-political framework of the Ming dynasty, ultimately influencing the strategies employed by the state to maintain control. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for analyzing the interplay between authority and everyday agency in historical contexts.
