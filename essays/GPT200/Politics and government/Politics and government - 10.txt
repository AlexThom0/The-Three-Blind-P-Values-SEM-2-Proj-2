Economic stagnation has not only led to widespread disenfranchisement but has also fueled a resurgence of identity politics, where individuals increasingly align themselves with tribal loyalties rather than shared democratic values. This shift has changed the landscape of political allegiance, creating rifts between different segments of society. As discontent grows, previously stable institutions are now under scrutiny, and faith in traditional liberal democratic frameworks has diminished. The increasing polarization is reflected in vehement public discourse and the rise of populist movements that advocate for radical change, often at the expense of established norms. This tumult underscores the necessity for a reassessment of not merely liberalism's utility but also its adaptability in a rapidly evolving political climate. The tension between maintaining fundamental democratic principles and accommodating new socio-political realities is palpable, challenging scholars and practitioners alike to reconsider the frameworks they employ in understanding contemporary governance. If liberalism is to survive, it must evolve in ways that resonate with the changing aspirations and concerns of the populace, while still upholding its core tenets of individual rights and democratic governance.
