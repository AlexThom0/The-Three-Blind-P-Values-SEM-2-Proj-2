The very infrastructures that enabled prosperity in the West—transportation networks, industrial advancements, and even recreational leisure—were largely financed by the resources and labor of colonised populations. Ghosh’s critique forces a reevaluation of narratives that celebrate progress without acknowledging the sacrifice and suffering that have fueled it. It reveals a paradox: as societies profess ideals of equality and shared prosperity, these ideals remain unrealizable for the majority, tethered to an unjust history. The question, then, becomes not just how many can attain the benefits of modernity, but at what cost to both the environment and the oppressed. A critical examination of this interconnectedness unveils a need for a more equitable and sustainable future, one that understands freedom not merely as access to consumer goods but as a collective responsibility to rectify past injustices. It is imperative to confront the legacy of colonialism to forge genuinely inclusive pathways that respect both human dignity and the planet’s health.
