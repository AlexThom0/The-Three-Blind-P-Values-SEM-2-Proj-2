As the industrial revolution ushered in new standards of beauty, thinness became increasingly idealized, particularly for women. This societal shift influenced not only personal self-image but also the diagnosis and treatment of anorexia. Clinicians began to recognize that patients’ perceptions of their bodies were heavily shaped by the cultural landscape that valorized slenderness. Moreover, as the medical community grappled with the complexities of anorexia, some practitioners leaned toward a purely medical model, while others called for a holistic approach that considered emotional and social factors. This divergence in perspective created a rich dialogue about the importance of understanding the individual within their environment. Influences such as peer dynamics and media representations further complicated treatments, emphasizing the need for a multi-faceted strategy to restore the patient’s health. As the condition continues to evolve in the contemporary context, researchers strive to understand the ways in which these factors interact, underscoring the importance of viewing anorexia as not merely a medical issue but a profound societal phenomenon impacting individuals' lives.
