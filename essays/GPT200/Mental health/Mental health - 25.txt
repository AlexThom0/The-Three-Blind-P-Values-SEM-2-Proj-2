This evidence caught Layard’s attention, as it aligned perfectly with his vision of happiness as a measurable entity. Clark emphasized that CBT was grounded in empirical research, and its principles could be operationalized in a way that directly impacted public health. The framework also suggested that by implementing CBT widely, governments could significantly enhance the mental well-being of their citizens. Moreover, the success of CBT in diverse settings indicated that such approaches could be adapted to various populations, making it a versatile tool in mental healthcare. Layard began to see the potential for a new partnership, one that bridged economics and psychology. The possibility of integrating rigorous psychological therapies into public policy could transform how societies address mental health issues, promoting not just treatment but prevention. Both men recognized that if happiness data could shape economic strategies, it stood to reason that effective therapeutic practices could reshape mental health paradigms at the national level. This collaboration was the beginning of a movement that could potentially change how happiness and mental health were prioritized in governmental agendas.
