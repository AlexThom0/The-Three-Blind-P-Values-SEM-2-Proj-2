While rationalist accounts emphasize detachment and calculation, sentimentalism highlights the significance of our affective connections. This perspective offers explanations for why individuals often act contrary to their self-interest when faced with the suffering of others. Empathy, therefore, acts as both a moral compass and a social glue that binds individuals together, often motivating altruistic behavior. Additionally, research has shown that prosocial actions are frequently driven not by cold logic but by emotional resonance. In contexts where empathy is encouraged, even those with psychopathic traits exhibit a greater ability to relate to others on a moral plane. This complex interplay between emotional response and moral reasoning challenges purely rational accounts of ethical behavior, suggesting that our feelings towards others—rooted in shared humanity—are critical for a comprehensive understanding of morality. By recognizing the foundational role of empathy, we can begin to address the ethical implications of actions taken by individuals with diminished emotional responses, prompting deeper inquiries into the nature of human morality itself.
