He argues that religion serves as a psychological defense mechanism, helping individuals cope with existential anxieties by projecting meaning onto an external source. This projection is evident in the fervent devotion to Adolf Hitler, who became an embodiment of national identity and moral salvation in the minds of many Germans. Freud's analysis sheds light on the broader phenomenon where political figures acquire a quasi-religious status, manipulating deeply rooted psychological needs for belonging and security. The merging of state and spiritual leadership results in a potent mix of admiration and subservience, which can lead societies to overlook moral and ethical transgressions in favor of loyalty to a perceived savior. Hitler’s ability to construct a narrative that resonated with the anxieties of post-WWI Germany illustrates how political ideologies can exploit psychological vulnerabilities. This is particularly relevant when considering the environment in which Freud found himself; he was acutely aware of how socio-political shifts could reshape cultural identities and individual psyches. Even the most rational minds are not immune to the seductive power of such illusions.
