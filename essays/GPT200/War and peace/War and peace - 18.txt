Higiro's experience at Auschwitz served as a profound catalyst for his belief in the transformative power of council. The harrowing backdrop of such historical trauma highlighted the deep need for healing, not only for survivors but also for those bearing the weight of their ancestors' sins. Inspired by the framework of restorative justice that council embodies, Higiro envisaged a model where dialogue replaces confrontation, enabling a communal confrontation with the past. In Rwanda, this approach could address the entrenched divisions that persist long after the genocide, allowing communities to engage in frank discussions about forgiveness and accountability. Through facilitated conversations, participants could confront their history, articulate their pain, and, importantly, explore pathways toward reconciliation. Such gatherings might also empower surviving victims to reclaim their narratives and participate actively in the healing process. Recognizing the urgent need for societal healing, Higiro's vision sought to create safe, inclusive spaces where even the most marginalized voices could contribute to the overarching narrative of recovery. By embracing a shared humanity and fostering empathy, the potential for individual and collective redemption emerges, illuminating a way through the darkness of Rwanda’s past.
