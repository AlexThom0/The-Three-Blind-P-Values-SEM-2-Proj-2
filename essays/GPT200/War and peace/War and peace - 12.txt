North Korean regimes, for example, have engaged in conflicts and provocations that could escalate under the shadow of nuclear arsenals. This suggests a fundamental flaw in the premise of deterrence: instead of promoting stability, the existence of nuclear weapons can incentivize risky behaviors and miscalculations. Fears of retaliation may lead states to act aggressively, believing they can exploit the perceived restraint of their opponent. Furthermore, as geopolitical dynamics shift, the idea that nuclear capabilities guarantee security is increasingly challenged by emerging cyber threats and asymmetric warfare tactics. As countries prioritize modernization of their military structures, the role of nuclear weapons becomes diluted amid multi-faceted security challenges. The assumptions underlying nuclear deterrence must be re-evaluated in light of contemporary conflicts, demonstrating that reliance on these weapons may foster insecurity rather than peace. Engaging in disarmament discussions can lead not only to a safer world but also to a productive international dialogue focused on comprehensive security strategies that emphasize cooperation over confrontation. In this context, the path to a nuclear-free future becomes more conceivable, prioritizing diplomacy and trust-building measures over an arms race that ultimately endangers global stability.
