This interplay of power and cruelty can be seen in the era's legal frameworks, which often sanctioned brutal treatment as a deterrent. The justification for torture was couched in the rhetoric of maintaining order and upholding the divine right of kings, thereby intertwining the act with theological and royal validation. Moreover, such rituals of agony were public spectacles, intended to galvanize support for the state and demoralize potential dissenters. The audience, compelled by both spectacle and superstition, became complicit, transforming the act of torture into a grotesque form of entertainment rather than a mere punitive measure. This cultural acceptance of violence reflected broader societal values where suffering was framed as a necessary evil in service to the greater good of the community and the state. Over time, these practices codified a brutal tradition that transcended individual tragic narratives, embedding a cycle of violence within the fabric of social order. Thus, the depiction of torture in art and literature served not only as commentary but as a reinforcement of the ideologies that justified its existence.
