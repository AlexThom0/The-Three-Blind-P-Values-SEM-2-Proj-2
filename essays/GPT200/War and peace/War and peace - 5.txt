Critics argued that the field lacked the methodological rigor found in disciplines like economics or sociology, which could measure outcomes with precision. However, proponents contended that peace studies necessitated a different epistemological approach, one that acknowledged human psychology and sociocultural contexts as vital components of conflict resolution. This holistic understanding encompasses more than mere metrics; it requires an exploration of values, identities, and narratives shaping societies. For instance, peace processes are frequently influenced by the collective memories of conflict, which can perpetuate cycles of animosity or, conversely, cultivate reconciliation. Thus, to study peace effectively, scholars must integrate qualitative methods that explore these complex relationships. This melding of disciplines not only broadens the scope of inquiry but also enriches our comprehension of how lasting peace can be achieved. As the academic landscape evolved, these innovative perspectives began to garner recognition, shifting the narrative surrounding peace studies from marginal to essential within the broader discourse on human coexistence.
