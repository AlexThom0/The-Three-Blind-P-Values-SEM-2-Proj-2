The interplay between biology and culture emphasizes that our neural architecture is not prescriptive but rather adaptive and responsive to contextual factors. This dynamism suggests that the same neurological underpinnings can manifest in vastly different behaviours depending on social influences, environmental triggers, and learned experiences. For instance, cultural narratives and societal norms shape our moral frameworks, which can amplify or mitigate innate tendencies toward aggression or empathy. Neuroscience increasingly points to the importance of epigenetics, indicating that experiences can alter gene expression, further embedding cultural context into our biological make-up. Such interactions illustrate that while certain predispositions may exist, the actualization of these traits is profoundly influenced by the surrounding world. Consequently, understanding violent behaviour or the capacity for empathy involves an integrated approach that considers not only the brain's structure but also socio-cultural dynamics. This multidimensional perspective underlines the necessity for interdisciplinary collaboration, merging insights from psychology, sociology, and anthropology to form a holistic view of human behaviour. By fostering such integration, we can better appreciate the nuanced ways in which our evolutionary heritage interacts with the contemporary societal landscape, ultimately shaping who we are as individuals and members of a collective.
