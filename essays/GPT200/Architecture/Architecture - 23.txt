Collaborative conservation efforts that incorporate traditional ecological knowledge can lead to innovative solutions, ensuring that both biodiversity and local livelihoods thrive. For instance, agroforestry practices can enhance connectivity between patches while providing economic benefits to surrounding populations. Moreover, the impact of climate change necessitates adaptive strategies that allow corridors to shift with changing conditions, thus supporting the survival of species that might otherwise become isolated. Without the involvement of local stakeholders, protected areas risk becoming exclusive zones, where people are forced into conflict with conservation goals. The perception of these areas as impositions can foster animosity, further undermining conservation efforts. This highlights the need for a fundamentally different approach—one that sees people not as threats but as integral participants in sustaining ecosystems. Establishing genuine partnerships bolsters resilience, facilitates the sharing of responsibilities, and ultimately creates a more favorable climate for biodiversity to flourish alongside human development. Only through mutual understanding and cooperation can these protected spaces achieve their intended purpose.
