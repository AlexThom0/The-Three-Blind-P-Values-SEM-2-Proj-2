The gardens and landscapes of such estates were meticulously crafted to evoke a sense of timelessness and tranquility, mirroring the pastoral themes in contemporary poetry. This cultivation of beauty was not merely for aesthetic pleasure; it was also an assertion of identity and status in a society increasingly defined by class distinctions. The idealized suburban retreat became a sanctuary where intellectual, artistic, and social pursuits could flourish, embodying a lifestyle that privileged leisure and appreciation of the arts. The lush gardens served as stages for the display of knowledge and creativity, inviting poets and thinkers to engage with nature’s bounty. Artists found inspiration in the colors and forms, and the very act of gardening became a metaphor for control over nature and a reflection of one's values. Thus, the gardens of this era were not only places for relaxation but also critical spaces for cultural engagement and expression, shaping the ideals of beauty and harmony that echoed through the literature of the period.
