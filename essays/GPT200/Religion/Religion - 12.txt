The rapid enlightenment suggested by early readings does not align with the complexities of human experience, especially in the transformative journey related to spiritual awakening. It is worth considering that the initial response to the Buddha’s teachings may have been more reflective of curiosity and skepticism than total acceptance. The psychology of these early followers likely influenced their understanding and interpretation of his messages, with doubt nestled alongside inspiration. This dynamic is common in movements that challenge prevailing societal norms. As Gotama navigated his new role, he likely encountered resistance both from within his circle and from the broader context of pre-imperial India, where conventional beliefs held sway. The gradual development of the Sangha, or community, may speak to a more protracted process of assimilation, requiring not just intellectual acceptance of the teachings, but also a deeper, more experiential understanding that took time to foster. This understanding may resonate with those familiar with other philosophical movements, where immediate transformation can be more myth than reality, obscuring the often slow and nuanced process of inner change and communal growth.
