Their refusal to participate in public rituals and sacrifices was seen as an affront to civic duty, an act of rebellion that threatened the traditional social order. This tension escalated as Christians insisted that their new faith eclipsed the old gods, claiming a universal truth that undermined the validity of ancestral customs. Consequently, suspicion grew, with rumors circulating about their practices fueling paranoia. The conflation of their gatherings with notions of subversion and secretive malevolence turned public sentiment against them. Greeks and Romans, accustomed to a pantheon that embraced diverse cults, found it unsettling that a burgeoning faith could so starkly reject the norm. In this climate of fear and misunderstanding, it became increasingly difficult to differentiate between genuine critiques of a new belief system and baseless accusations arising from ignorance. Rather than being seen as a legitimate movement, Christianity was often interpreted through the distorted lens of societal anxiety. Thus, the foundations for future conflict were laid, with cultural misunderstandings exacerbating the Christians' otherness.
