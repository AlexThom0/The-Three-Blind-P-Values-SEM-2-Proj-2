By positioning themselves as protectors of the Orthodox faith, they sought to foster a loyalty among the local populations that would align more closely with Soviet interests rather than nationalism. This complex maneuvering revealed a dual strategy: reinforcing Soviet ideology while manipulating religious sentiments to create a buffer against potential uprisings. The approach was not merely a response to pressures from their allies or the re-emergence of local religious practices, but rather a calculated measure to co-opt spiritual authority and integrate it within the broader framework of state control. Such actions illustrated a significant shift in the Kremlin's policy, evolving from outright hostility towards religion to a pragmatic embrace of it as a tool for consolidation of power. This instrumentalization of faith was both a testament to the adaptability of the Soviet regime and a reflection of the changing dynamics within the region, where religion could become an asset in the quest for geopolitical dominance.
