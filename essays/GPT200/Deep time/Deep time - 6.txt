Brown's exploits fueled public interest in paleontology, turning dinosaur hunting into a sensational spectacle rather than merely a scientific pursuit. His larger-than-life persona resonated with the era’s fascination for adventure, and the media popularized his findings with dramatic flair. As collectors and researchers raced to uncover the next monumental find, the competition heightened, leading to more discoveries but also sparking ethical concerns about the preservation of these ancient specimens. Rapid excavation often prioritized profit and prestige over scientific integrity, a trend that became increasingly problematic as the popularity of dinosaurs surged. Fossils began to be sold at exorbitant prices, commodifying paleontological treasures and sometimes resulting in incomplete or poorly documented discoveries.
