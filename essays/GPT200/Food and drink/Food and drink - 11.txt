This shift goes beyond mere convenience; it encapsulates the broader economic and cultural transformations of the 20th century. As labor-saving devices proliferated in households, women found themselves in a dual role—managing both domestic responsibilities and pursuing careers. The economic necessity for dual incomes became increasingly apparent, reflecting changing attitudes toward women's work. This evolution was not merely a byproduct of consumer choice; rather, it was a deliberate recalibration of societal structures where women sought not just to fulfill familial duties but also to carve out their own identities in the workforce. The emergence of convenience foods thus mirrored a socio-economic shift that allowed women to negotiate their roles both at home and in professional settings. Rather than viewing packaged meals as a diminishment of culinary tradition, it is more accurate to consider them as instruments of empowerment, facilitating women's entry into various domains previously dominated by men. The underlying message was clear: women could engage in varied pursuits, importantly redefining their narratives in a rapidly modernizing society.
