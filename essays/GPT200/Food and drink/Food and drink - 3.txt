Additionally, the dominant medical theories of the time often hindered the acceptance of these simple solutions. The humoral theory, which suggested that health depended on the balance of bodily fluids, overshadowed the idea of a direct nutritional deficiency. Thus, when fresh produce and citrus fruits were recommended, they were often met with skepticism or dismissed as unscientific. This clash between traditional beliefs and emerging evidence continued to create barriers to widespread adoption of effective prevention methods. Despite repeated instances of successful treatments across different cultures and historical contexts, the knowledge was not persistently integrated into mainstream practices. Each rediscovery of the beneficial properties of certain foods faced the same cycle of neglect, pointing to a broader issue within medical practice: the difficulty in overcoming established doctrines and beliefs. Such setbacks not only delayed advancements in scurvy prevention but also reflected a broader pattern of resistance often encountered in the history of medical scientific progress.
