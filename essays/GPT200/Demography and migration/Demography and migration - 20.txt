The challenge of quantifying peace lies not just in its elusive definition but also in the contrast it presents against the visceral impacts of violence. While violence manifests in concrete actions, it gives rise to a narrative filled with urgency and chaos, easily measurable and analyzable. This narrative can be politically mobilized, guiding responses from institutions and governments alike. In stark contrast, peace feels almost passive, as if it requires a profound stillness that is inherently difficult to capture. It exists not as a moment but as a continuum, fluctuating with societal sentiments and conditions. Some theorists argue that peace can be assessed through proxy indicators—such as stability, economic prosperity, or social cohesion—but these metrics often fall short of encapsulating the true essence of what it means to live without conflict. Thus, when attempting to engage with the subject of peace, scholars might be left grappling with its imprecision, overshadowed by the stark realities of war and discord. This ambiguity invites deeper exploration of how peaceful experiences can be nurtured and sustained, revealing layers of human interaction and shared aspirations that often go unnoticed amid the cacophony of turmoil.
