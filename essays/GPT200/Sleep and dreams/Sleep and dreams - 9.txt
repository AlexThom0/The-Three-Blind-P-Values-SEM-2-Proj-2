The focus has shifted from understanding sleep as an essential aspect of human life to viewing it merely through the lens of efficiency and productivity. Consequently, many individuals increasingly rely on pharmaceuticals to mitigate sleep disorders, further alienating themselves from their innate rhythms. This transformation neglects the rich tapestry of cultural practices around sleep that honored its restorative and transformative qualities. Ancients understood the value of sleep rituals, embracing it as a necessary element of existence rather than an obstacle to be conquered. Today, sleep is often viewed through the prism of performance metrics, such as sleep cycles or hours logged, reducing a fundamentally human experience to mere numbers. The metrics-centric narrative not only commodifies sleep but also cultivates anxiety, as people grapple with the fear of insufficient rest in a society that demands constant vigilance. The allure of a tangible solution in the form of sleep aids only deepens this divide, as it obscures the natural, restorative functions that our bodies inherently understand. Thus, our relationship with sleep continues to fracture, reducing it to a mere resource instead of a vital source of rejuvenation and creativity.
