The shift from oral storytelling to written texts did not merely alter the medium; it also transformed the perception of authorship itself. Authors began to be seen as individuals with unique voices rather than just vessels of collective tradition. This newfound emphasis on individual authorship led to the emergence of copyright and intellectual property rights, as the concept of ownership over written works became more pronounced. As audiences grew accustomed to printed texts, they started to identify not just with the content but also with the people behind it. The intimacy of authorship fostered a sense of personal connection between reader and writer, reshaping the cultural landscape in ways that favored distinctiveness and originality. Consequently, a cultural hierarchy developed around authorship, giving rise to a marketplace for ideas where established voices could dominate. While this innovation democratized access to knowledge by making it available to the masses, it also introduced complexities in how stories were told and shared, often sidelining the contributions of oral traditions and communal narratives. This dual impact of the printing press signalled a profound shift in the literary world, paving the way for contemporary forms of literature and the diverse voices we encounter today.
