By granting NGOs voting rights in this iterative process, we empower entities steeped in expertise rather than transient political motivations. These organisations can leverage data and research to inform decisions that benefit both present and future citizens, aligning policy choices with evidence-based forecasts rather than mere electoral expediency. This approach also mitigates the risk of partisan bias, as NGOs typically focus on broader societal outcomes rather than short-term gains. Enhancing their role as voters introduces an additional layer of accountability, as these groups would not only advocate for future generations but would also be answerable to their stakeholders, including civil society. These entities, equipped with a mandate to represent the long-term public interest, can play a transformative role in shaping policies that transcend the limitations of a merely electoral timeframe. Consequently, by integrating such comprehensive and knowledgeable voters into our democratic frameworks, we enable a shift toward a more equitable, sustainable, and future-oriented governance model. This vision lays the groundwork for a more robust dialogue between present and future responsibilities.
