Pope’s assertion that "whatever is, is right" echoes Leibniz’s vision, suggesting that human suffering and adversity not only serve a purpose but also reflect a deeper, underlying morality woven into the fabric of existence. This notion challenges the immediacy of human experiences, casting them instead as threads in a tapestry crafted by divine intention. As the Enlightenment unfolded, thinkers began to grapple with the implications of this worldview, questioning how free will intersects with divine predestination. The acceptance of a rational, benevolent cosmos nurtured faith in progress and improvement; however, this same belief led to controversies when confronted with the realities of injustice and suffering in human society. Responding to these contradictions, philosophical discourse evolved, leading to a spectrum of interpretations about the nature of reality and the moral responsibilities of individuals within it. Through this lens, literature and philosophy reveal a persistent struggle to reconcile individual agency with a universe governed by an incomprehensible yet benevolent design, reflecting humanity’s deep yearning for meaning amid chaos.
