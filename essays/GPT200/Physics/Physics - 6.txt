While personal experience may lend itself to the sensation of time flowing, the scientific framework paints a vastly different picture. The notion of a flowing time is inherently at odds with the geometric structure of spacetime, which positions all moments on equal footing. This creates a tension between our subjective experience and the objective reality suggested by physics. To reconcile these views, some propose that our perception of time’s passage may arise from cognitive processes rather than an intrinsic temporal flow. This perspective invites a reexamination of how we construct memories and anticipate future events, emphasizing the role of consciousness in shaping our understanding of time. Even within theories that support the passage of time, one must concede that the underlying physical laws treat temporal events as coordinates in a four-dimensional continuum, ultimately leading to the conclusion that time’s passage is an emergent phenomenon rather than a fundamental aspect of reality. Thus, what remains is the question of how we can meaningfully navigate a universe where time, as it is traditionally conceived, may not hold the same significance as we intuitively believe.
