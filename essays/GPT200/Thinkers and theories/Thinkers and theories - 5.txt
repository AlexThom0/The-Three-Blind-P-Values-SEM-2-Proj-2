The interactions with his pupils forced him to confront the inadequacies of his previous theories, prompting a reevaluation of how meaning is constructed in everyday life. In those chaotic classrooms, filled with laughter and tears, Wittgenstein began to see language not merely as a logical structure but as a living, fluid entity shaped by context and use. He recognized that the exchanges he had with his students revealed more about human communication than any formal philosophical discourse could. This newfound perspective shifted his focus from abstraction to the practicalities of language in action, leading him toward the exploration of "language games" and their role in forming meaning. These observations underscored his belief that understanding is an experiential process, deeply embedded in social interactions rather than isolated intellectual exercises. As he engaged with children, he began to understand that the essence of teaching lies in the relationships between people, language, and the world, a realization that would enrich his later work and profoundly influence his approach to philosophy. The complexities of these early experiences would resonate throughout his later writings, weaving together themes of learning, communication, and the intricacies of human connection.
