This illusion, he argues, stems from our psychological makeup; we experience events in a linear fashion, facing the future while recalling the past. However, if we dissect our experience, we find that these temporal distinctions are mere constructs. Events do not transition from one state to another, but rather exist in a timeless framework where our labels of past, present, and future serve only to organize our thoughts. This perspective challenges not only our understanding of time but also the nature of reality itself. If our perceptions do not reflect an underlying temporal structure, then the fundamental aspects of existence we take for granted—such as cause and effect—may also be called into question. The dependency of our actions on a temporal sequence becomes problematic. If every moment is equally real and fixed, the causality we ascribe to our experiences begins to unravel. This invites further exploration into whether our lived experience can provide any genuine insight into the nature of reality, or if it is merely the byproduct of a constructed framework we insist upon in our search for understanding.
