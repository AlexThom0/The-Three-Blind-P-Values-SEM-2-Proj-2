This shift in perspective marked a pivotal transformation in the relationship between myth and reason. No longer seen merely as artistic expressions or reflections of societal values, these myths were scrutinized for their moral implications and philosophical shortcomings. Critics like Fontenelle emphasized the dissonance between the fantastical narratives of ancient lore and the emergent scientific rationalism. They posited that these legends not only failed to provide moral guidance but also undermined the very principles of reason and empirical inquiry that were becoming the bedrock of modern thought. As natural philosophers sought explanations grounded in observation and evidence, the whimsical tales of gods and heroes began to seem increasingly anachronistic. The burgeoning field of critical analysis propelled these myths into the realm of skepticism, inviting readers to dissect and reinterpret them through a contemporary lens. This newfound critical engagement laid the groundwork for later explorations into how narratives shape cultural identity, suggesting that understanding myths requires not only a dismissal of their traditional exaltation but also an active reexamination of their roles in society's evolution.
