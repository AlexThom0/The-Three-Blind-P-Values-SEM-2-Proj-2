Husserl proposed that instead of seeking to prove or disprove the existence of the external world, philosophers should focus on how we experience these objects within our consciousness. By employing a method of epoché, or suspension of judgment on the existence of the external world, he sought to examine the structures of experience itself. In this light, numbers, as abstract entities, became a vital case study; it was not their existence outside of the mind that interested him, but how they manifest in conscious thought. This shift reflects a broader pivot in philosophy, moving away from metaphysical concerns toward a more rigorous examination of subjective experience. Underpinning this exploration is a recognition that the act of meaning-making is inherently tied to human consciousness. Each mathematical operation or philosophical concept is imbued with the subjective perspectives and lived experiences of individuals, suggesting a foundational interconnectedness between thought and reality. Understanding this relationship opens the door to deeper inquiries into the essence of knowledge itself.
