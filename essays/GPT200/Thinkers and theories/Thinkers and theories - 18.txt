Workers became alienated from the products of their labor, and in doing so, they relinquished both agency and identity. The relentless pursuit of efficiency stripped away the richness of human experience, leading to a pervasive numbness permeating everyday life. Anders argued that this condition is exacerbated by a culture that glorifies technological advancement while neglecting the moral implications of its trajectory. As society becomes increasingly dependent on digitized systems, the opportunity for critical reflection diminishes, making it all the more imperative to interrogate the ethical frameworks underpinning these advancements. Consequently, the human spirit is placed at risk, as superficial engagement replaces genuine connection, both with one another and with the world around us. The consequences are dire: a populace that is disconnected from the very essence of what it means to be human, trapped in a cycle where every action becomes transactional, reflecting not personal choice but the demands of an impersonal market. In this context, the question of moral responsibility emerges as a central theme demanding urgent engagement.
