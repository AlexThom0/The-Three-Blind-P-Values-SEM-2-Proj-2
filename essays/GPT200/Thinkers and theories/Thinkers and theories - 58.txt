The idea of a flat Earth not only aligned with the observational experiences of the time but also supported a more materialistic interpretation of nature that Epicurus championed. By adhering to a flat cosmology, Lucretius could dismiss the seemingly arbitrary complexities introduced by spherical models, which often invoked divine influence or celestial hierarchies that he sought to rationalize away. Furthermore, a flat Earth allowed for a simpler, more accessible understanding of natural phenomena, one that reinforced the tenets of atomism and the absence of divine intervention. This adherence to earlier ideas also highlights how philosophical beliefs can sometimes hinder scientific progress. Rather than adapting to newfound knowledge, Lucretius clings to a familiar framework that resonates with the materialistic and chaotic principles he promotes. In this way, his rejection of the spherical model serves as both a reflection of the intellectual climate of his time and a testament to how philosophical commitments can shape and sometimes distort our comprehension of the natural world. The implications of this embrace of flatness are profound, echoing through the ages in various forms of scientific skepticism and resistance.
