The transformative acts—renaming, the imposition of new customs, and sanctions against old loyalties—served as tools for captors to assert dominance over their captives. This dual process of assimilation and erasure not only prioritized the captor's cultural narratives but also placed captives in perpetual liminality, trapped between worlds yet belonging to neither. An especially poignant aspect of this experience lies in the tension between coercion and agency; while many captives experienced profound loss, some began to navigate their new lives creatively. They forged new identities within the confines of their imposed roles, sometimes fostering bonds with captors that blurred the lines of power. As negotiations of identity unfolded, social structures shifted, leading to a reimagining of kinship and status within these mixed communities. This complicated interplay challenges us to reconsider the simplistic binary of captor and captive, urging a deeper exploration of how agency can manifest in even the most oppressive circumstances.
