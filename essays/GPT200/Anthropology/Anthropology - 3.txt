These stories often map not only the geographical transformations but also the profound impacts on cultural identity and social structures. For many Aboriginal groups, these submerged territories remain a part of their cosmological beliefs, underscoring a continuity between past and present. As sea levels rose, certain cultural practices and sacred sites became unreachable, challenging communities to adapt while also preserving the essence of their heritage. This adaptation is reflected in their oral traditions, which serve as vital instruments for remembering and transmitting knowledge about landscapes that may no longer exist. Such narratives are not mere echoes of a forgotten past; rather, they are living testimonies that convey the complexities of human resilience in the face of environmental change. These stories often highlight the relationships between people, the land, and the ever-encroaching sea, illuminating how ancient wisdom can inform contemporary challenges, thereby bridging historical experiences with modern understandings of climate adaptation and preservation. The multifaceted interplay of memory, environment, and identity showcases the richness of Indigenous narratives in the context of global sea-level rise.
