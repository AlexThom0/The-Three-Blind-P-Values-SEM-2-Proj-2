The Niha tales, passed down through generations, serve not merely as entertainment but as a vital means of agency and resistance. In these stories, the heart symbolizes the collective memory of struggle and resilience, echoing the historical context of colonial subjugation and the lingering effects of foreign domination. Each retelling reinforces community bonds and reasserts their wisdom amidst changing political landscapes. This is not mere nostalgia; rather, it is a strategy of survival, enabling them to reclaim narrative authority over their lives and history. As they weave these narratives into public dialogue, the Niha exercise a form of resistance that transcends mere words, transforming past grievances into a living testament of identity. Thus, the power exerted by the storytellers reveals a continuity of conscience, tethering individuals to their cultural roots while confronting contemporary challenges. As the heart continues its rhythmic beat, it prompts reflection, urging listeners to acknowledge both the pains and triumphs woven into the fabric of their existence. The Niha, through their oratory, facilitate a profound conversation on autonomy and voice.
