Within this context, laughter can be seen as a bridge between aggression and playfulness, tapping into a primal instinct that fosters tight social cohesion. Primates often engage in vocal behaviors that serve dual purposes: signaling distress while simultaneously reinforcing group bonds. During moments of social tension, laughter may emerge not only as an expression of relief but also as a subtle reminder of social hierarchy and collective identity. In this framework, laughter acts as a social lubricant, easing conflicts or reinforcing group bonds, just as mobbing behaviors escalate tensions within a community. The contagious nature of laughter suggests that it can diffuse potential aggression, letting individuals reconnect through shared emotional experiences. This interactive dynamic highlights laughter’s role in establishing social norms and reassuring group members of their place within a social structure. By understanding laughter as a sophisticated communication strategy, we can recognize its evolution as a multifaceted expression that embodies both threat and camaraderie. As social animals, both humans and their primate relatives utilize laughter not merely for amusement but as an essential tool for navigating the complexities of group living, reflecting our shared ancestral roots and the ongoing importance of emotional connection.
