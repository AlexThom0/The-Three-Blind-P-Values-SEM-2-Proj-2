This disjunction between medium and meaning can be observed in the prevalence of digital platforms where art is commodified, reshuffling historic narratives into consumable content. The loss of a singular experience is palpable; the instantaneity of sharing reduces the contemplative engagement traditionally associated with viewing art. Moreover, the flood of images that fill our screens can obscure the nuances of intent behind the creation. Artists today grapple with this paradox – they both embrace and critique the facile accessibility of their work, navigating a landscape where personal expression coexists with mass consumption. This relationship challenges not only the integrity of the artwork itself but also the viewer’s interaction with it. Anonymity seeps into the discourse as more creators participate in the digital dialogue, blurring the lines between authorship and audience. These dynamics raise critical questions about authenticity and interpretation in an era defined by rapid dissemination, echoing the anxieties articulated in Berger’s analysis of visual culture, and reminding us that the act of seeing is far from passive.
