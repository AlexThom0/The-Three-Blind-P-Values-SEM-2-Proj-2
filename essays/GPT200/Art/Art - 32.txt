This perspective encourages individuals to view damage not as a flaw to be hidden but as an opportunity for innovation and personal storytelling. The act of mending thereby becomes a dialogue between the repaired item and its owner, where the history of wear is acknowledged, and new narratives are woven into the fabric. This playful divergence from conventional aesthetics aligns with the principles of wabi-sabi, celebrating imperfection and transience. Just as Furuta Oribe’s philosophy enriches our understanding of Japanese ceramics, the contemporary movement around mending invites a reevaluation of our relationship with possessions. Each stitch and patch becomes a conscious decision, an assertion of individuality that contrasts with mass production's anonymity. This subversive act transforms not only the object itself but also the mindset of the beholder. Objects that once appeared broken now hold a richer significance, embedded with memories and creativity, showcasing a vibrant dialogue between past and present. By embracing this approach, we reimagine our consumption patterns and redefine what it means to care for the things we own.
