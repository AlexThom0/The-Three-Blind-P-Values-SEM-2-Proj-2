The artists who participated in events like The Wrong rely on diverse modes of outreach and engagement, yet their works, often vibrant and provocative, can become mere background noise amid an ocean of content. Social media algorithms, designed to cater to our preferences, play a significant role in filtering out anything that lies outside our established interests. This creates a paradox: while there is more art than ever before, the mechanisms meant to enhance our exposure often limit our appreciation of it. Consequently, pivotal exhibitions or thought-provoking pieces may slip through the cracks, unobserved and unengaged. Creative dialogue suffers as a result, leading to a homogenization of artistic discourse that restricts innovation. Moreover, the very act of filtering, whether intentional or not, embodies a reluctance to confront diverse perspectives. A dynamic exchange of ideas requires stepping outside one’s comfort zone, risking dissonance for the sake of growth. The challenge lies in actively seeking out the unfamiliar and allowing it to challenge our established narratives. Only then can we transcend the limits imposed by our self-selected bubbles.
