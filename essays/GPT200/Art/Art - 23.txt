Travelers sought to absorb the artistic and historical riches of cities like Rome, Florence, and Venice, considering themselves part of a broader European tradition. This engagement was not merely passive; it involved active participation in local customs and social circles. The experience of encountering Renaissance masterpieces or engaging in philosophical discourse with continental intellectuals profoundly influenced the travelers' worldview, shaping their identities upon return. The allure of Italy was bolstered by its association with beauty, enlightenment, and sophistication, which contrasted sharply with the strictures of post-Cromwellian England. Such encounters often led to the creation of diaries and letters, serving as both personal reflections and broader commentaries on society. The reflections of these English travelers reveal an intimate connection between personal experience and scholarly pursuits, suggesting that their journeys were as much about self-exploration as about exploring the illustrious past of the civilizations they encountered. Hence, these travels did not merely serve aesthetic or recreational purposes; they fundamentally transformed the social and intellectual landscape of England in the late 17th century.
