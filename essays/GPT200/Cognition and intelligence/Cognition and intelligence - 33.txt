Conversations about privilege were often brushed off as theoretical abstractions, and he engaged with the world through the lens of youthful optimism. I often found myself torn between encouraging his spirited debates and preparing him for the harsher realities that lay outside our liberal bubble. The Westboro protests were not just a backdrop for his passionate beliefs; they represented a wider societal conflict that would enable him to confront the stereotypes and fears that may arise in less accepting spaces. He had fervent ideas about fairness and justice, yet he hadn’t fully grasped how those principles intersected with the complex dynamics of race in America. As he prepared to join the fray of public opinion, I felt a weight of responsibility to inject a dose of reality into our discussions. It was crucial that he understood not just the importance of speaking out, but also the potential consequences of being a young Black man in situations fraught with misunderstanding and prejudice.
