Even those unfamiliar with cinematic conventions draw upon their inherent understanding of narrative flow and spatial relationships. This suggests that the cognitive processes involved in responding to images aren’t merely a product of learned behaviors associated with film; rather, they tap into deeper, evolutionary mechanisms that govern how we perceive and construct meaning from dynamic scenes. This innate ability might explain why viewers, despite lacking exposure to film or specific editing techniques, can engage with edited sequences without significant confusion. Their familiarity with real-life experiences—observing actions unfold in a fragmented manner and making sense of them—serves as a foundational guide. As these viewers process the visual information presented to them, they rely on context and coherence discerned from their own life experiences, allowing them to bridge gaps in understanding where cuts might otherwise mislead. Thus, while certain editing strategies pose challenges, the ability to parse visual narratives largely remains intact, underscoring a robustness in our cognitive faculties that persists even amidst unfamiliar mediums.
