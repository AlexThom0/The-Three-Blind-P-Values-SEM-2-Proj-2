This distinction is crucial for understanding how we attribute ‘mind’ to entities that occupy various roles in society. The observation that organizations can be seen as making decisions, yet lack the capacity for emotional experiences, reveals a broader pattern in our perception. This duality suggests that we may also project certain characteristics onto individuals and systems based on their functional roles, further complicating our interpretations of personhood and agency. For instance, while we may ascribe intentions to a company, we hesitate to attribute the vulnerability associated with suffering or empathy. Such categorizations might stem from underlying social or cultural norms that influence our judgements about what constitutes a ‘full’ mind, shaping how we interact with both humans and non-human actors. By dissecting these perceptions, researchers can better understand the factors that drive our ethical considerations and responsibilities toward artificial intelligences and other non-human entities. This nuanced understanding informs ongoing debates about the moral status of various beings, as our inherent bias towards competency and emotionality continues to shape societal constructs of value and dignity.
