This framing of success shifts the narrative from external circumstances to individual effort, implying that those who falter may simply lack the necessary resilience or motivation. Critics, however, argue that such a view oversimplifies the complexities surrounding success, particularly for individuals facing systemic barriers tied to socioeconomic status, race, and access to resources. They contend that grit alone cannot dismantle the entrenched inequities that shape individuals' opportunities. This debate raises questions about the role of personal responsibility and the extent to which societal structures influence outcomes. If success can largely be attributed to grit, it risks ignoring broader conversations about privilege and inequality, obscuring the harsh realities that many must navigate. Duckworth's model, while inspiring, may inadvertently reinforce a narrative that de-emphasizes the significance of supportive environments, mentorship, and education. Therefore, a more nuanced understanding of success may require integrating the concepts of both personal effort and systemic change, recognizing that resilience is often cultivated within a framework of opportunity and support.
