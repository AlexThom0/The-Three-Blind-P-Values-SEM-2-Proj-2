This perspective not only perpetuates the patriarchal narrative that dismisses women’s subjective experiences but also risks invalidating those whose pain stems from complex psychosomatic origins. The insistence on a purely physical explanation creates a hierarchy of pain where only certain experiences are deemed worthy of empathy and intervention. This binary approach fails to recognize that pain is a multifaceted experience shaped by biological, psychological, and social factors. By framing women’s pain within the traditional medical model, we further stigmatize those who face pain without a clear diagnosis or who struggle against the cultural scripts that dictate how pain should be expressed and understood. Thus, external validation through medical frameworks becomes a necessary, albeit often insufficient, pathway for women to gain agency over their experiences. As conversations around pain evolve, it’s critical to embrace a more inclusive understanding that acknowledges the intersection of physical and psychological components in women's health, steering away from reductive categorizations that undermine their lived realities.
