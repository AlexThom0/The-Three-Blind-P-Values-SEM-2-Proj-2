Fridlund's departure from the established dogma was not merely an academic shift but a profound reassessment of emotional functionality itself. He began to consider that emotional expressions might not be as rigidly wired as once thought. Instead, they could arise in specific contexts, shaped by cultural and social factors. This perspective opened new avenues for understanding how different societies interpret and express emotions, suggesting that contexts significantly influence emotional signals. For instance, tension in social relationships might evoke expressions that diverge fundamentally from those predicted by the Basic Emotion framework. Moreover, Fridlund's exploration into the nuances of interspecies communication introduced an additional layer of complexity. By observing how animals signal distress or joy, he recognized parallels with human emotional displays. Such insights indicated that rather than universal expressions, what we perceive as basic emotions may indeed be contextually anchored behaviors, honed by individual experiences and environmental interactions. This paradigm shift challenged not only the universality concept but also the assumptions underpinning emotional interactions within human societies. In essence, Fridlund’s work catalyzed a more nuanced understanding of emotions, revealing a tapestry woven from diverse threads of experience and cultural significance.
