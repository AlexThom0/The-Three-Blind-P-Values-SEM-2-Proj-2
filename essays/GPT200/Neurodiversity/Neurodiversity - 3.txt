Different cultural contexts may influence perceptions and recognition of autism, affecting both diagnosis and treatment. In many places, symptoms that qualify a child for an autism diagnosis in high-income countries might not be recognized or understood in low-income settings. Furthermore, socioeconomic factors play a crucial role in access to healthcare, with many children potentially going undiagnosed due to insufficient resources or a lack of trained professionals. This gap complicates efforts to provide equitable support and interventions. Additionally, the stigmatization of developmental disorders in various societies may deter families from seeking help, further impacting prevalence statistics. As research continues to evolve, the need for international collaboration becomes clear, enabling a richer, more nuanced understanding of autism across different cultural and economic landscapes. Understanding these discrepancies is critical not only for accurate prevalence estimation but also for developing effective public health strategies tailored to diverse populations.
