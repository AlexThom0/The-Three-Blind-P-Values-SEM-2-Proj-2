The alignment of pharmaceutical interests with the evolving definitions of mental health conditions has led to an environment where the threshold for treatment eligibility is continually lowered. This phenomenon is compounded by the increasing public awareness of mental health issues, which often results in a heightened sensitivity to symptoms that may have gone unnoticed in the past. Social media, along with other platforms, has accelerated this trend, as individuals share personal experiences of mental health struggles, potentially influencing public perception and understanding. The more mental health conditions are normalized and made a topic of widespread discussion, the more individuals may identify with these labels, prompting self-diagnosis or increased medical consultations. This dynamic creates a feedback loop; as awareness grows, so too does the demand for treatments. The complexities of human behaviour do not lend themselves neatly to the categories provided in the DSM, making it imperative to critically assess how these classifications evolve amid the pressures from both the medical community and society at large. Balancing the benefits of treatment access with the risk of overdiagnosis remains a critical challenge in modern psychiatry.
