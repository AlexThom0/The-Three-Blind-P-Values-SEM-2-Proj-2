They sought to redefine the Turkish self-image, promoting a narrative of progress and enlightenment that would resonate both domestically and internationally. This involved not only the adoption of Western cultural practices but also an emphasis on the idea of racial modernity—positioning themselves as a bridge between East and West. Educational reforms were instituted, aimed at transforming the social fabric of Turkish society by fostering a sense of nationalism grounded in an idealized version of Turkish history. This cultural shift was crucial as it aimed to reclaim the narrative that had been shaped by Western powers, turning the "Terrible Turk" into a symbol of resilience and transformation. The modernization campaign encompassed language reform as well, with the Latin alphabet replacing the Arabic script in an effort to simplify literacy and promote cultural assimilation. By reshaping their identity through these myriad reforms, Turkish leaders endeavored to challenge the persistent stereotypes and carve out a space for a modern nation-state that was self-determined and forward-looking. Such efforts were not only about shedding the old image; they were fundamentally tied to the aspiration of redefining belonging within the global order.
