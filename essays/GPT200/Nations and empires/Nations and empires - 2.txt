This blending of climates fostered the emergence of a diverse agricultural economy ripe for experimentation. Local farmers and imperial officials alike engaged in the cultivation of European, Asian, and Middle Eastern crops, all in an effort to maximize productivity and export potential. The introduction of new irrigation techniques, alongside the use of imported seeds, transformed the agricultural landscape, paving the way for the cultivation of exotic fruits such as citrus and pomegranate, marking the region as an agricultural frontier within the empire. Additionally, the geopolitical ambitions of tsarist Russia prompted a narrative of manifest destiny, revitalizing interest in transforming the South Caucasus into a model of imperial success. This vision was intertwined with the desire to showcase the empire’s agricultural capabilities while asserting Russia's claim over territories that were increasingly viewed as essential to national pride and economic viability. Such narratives served to legitimize both the expansionist policies and the underlying missions of the empire, cementing the South Caucasus’s identity as a vital agricultural hub at the crossroads of differing cultural influences.
