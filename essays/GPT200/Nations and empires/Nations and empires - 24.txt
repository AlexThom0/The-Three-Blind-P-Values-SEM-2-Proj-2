For many, accepting an honour was not just a personal accolade but also an endorsement of colonial authority. Figures like Tagore and Gandhi exemplified the idea that dignity and self-respect were far more valuable than the hollow prestige associated with titles bestowed by a foreign power. As these movements gained momentum, a cultural shift occurred that celebrated national identity over colonial recognition. Education and awareness played crucial roles, fostering a climate in which honouring native achievements became a fundamental part of the independence struggle. Writers, artists, and thinkers increasingly sought recognition based on their contributions to society rather than allegiance to colonial accolades. The emergent discourse around honour and dignity catalyzed a transformation, where titles were increasingly seen as instruments of control rather than marks of distinction. Thus, the relationship between honour and national identity evolved, prompting Indian leaders to advocate for a system that celebrated indigenous contributions, reflecting a deeper, more authentic connection to their history and aspirations. This shift was not merely tactical; it was a fundamental reimagining of what it meant to be honoured in an era of rising nationalism.
