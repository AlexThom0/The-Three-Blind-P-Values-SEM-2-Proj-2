This gruesome practice linked anthropological study directly to evolving notions of racial hierarchy and the justification of imperial expansion. Native Americans, viewed through this scientific lens as primitive and inferior, were seen as obstacles to the progress of civilization. Those advocating for their removal often cloaked their intentions in a rhetoric that praised the cultural superiority of the European settler population while depicting Indigenous ways of life as relics to be eradicated. The duality of fascination and disdain towards Native Americans echoed sentiments surrounding other marginalized groups, reinforcing a collective national identity that prioritized certain races over others. Scholars sought to categorize and delineate differences, creating a sort of intellectual schema that rationalized dispossession and violence. Thus, this emerging lexicon of racial classification not only justified the physical removal of Native populations but also cemented the ideological foundations for viewing the ‘Other’ as inherently unequal and undeserving of the same societal privileges as their European counterparts. The consequences of such scientific racism had profound implications for policy formation and public perception.
