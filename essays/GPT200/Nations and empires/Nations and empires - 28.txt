As France grappled with its position vis-à-vis Britain and its evolving empire, the concept of the Anglo-Saxon became a pivotal point of reference. This shift was not merely linguistic; it echoed deeper anxieties about cultural superiority and the perceived threats posed by Anglo-American expansionism. In this milieu, French intellectuals sought to define and solidify a distinct Latin identity as a counter-narrative to Anglo-Saxon hegemony. The portrayal of Anglo-Saxons in the literature of the time often reflected not just admiration but also a critical perspective that highlighted differences in values, governance, and societal structures. This ambivalence fueled a fervent debate over the qualities attributed to those considered Anglo-Saxon versus their Latin counterparts. The racial implications of these discussions laid the groundwork for future ideologies, ultimately influencing how both regions perceived and interacted with one another in the ensuing decades. Through this evolving lexicon, the term became more than a descriptor; it embodied a cultural and political battleground that would resonate throughout subsequent colonial and post-colonial discourses.
