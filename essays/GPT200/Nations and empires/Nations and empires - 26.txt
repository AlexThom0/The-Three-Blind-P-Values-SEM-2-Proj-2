As the British capitalised on their military technology, local leaders navigated a landscape increasingly defined by shifting loyalties mediated through weaponry. The proliferation of arms transformed regional conflicts, enabling smaller states to resist or collaborate with larger powers. Consequently, these arms became symbols of both autonomy and subservience, as regional monarchs embraced or rejected British advances based on their military needs. The significance of indigenous craftsmanship in firearms production illustrated a duality where local expertise drove both resistance and assimilation. The arms trade thus linked disparate regions within the subcontinent, fostering networks of diplomacy and commerce that transcended simple colonial narratives. The intertwined fates of British interests and local entities crystallised in both conflict and cooperation, showing that weaponry was more than mere tools of war; they were instruments of strategic negotiation and identity formation. This backdrop laid the groundwork for the intricate engagements that would define the era, with each transaction echoing the evolving interplay of power and prestige among competing factions.
