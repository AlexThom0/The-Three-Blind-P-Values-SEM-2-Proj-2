The assertion that Western ways could be wholly adopted was fundamentally flawed, not only because of the cultural disparities but also due to the unique trajectory of Japan’s own historical development. Fukuzawa recognized that Japan had a rich tapestry of traditions and practices that could not simply be overshadowed or replaced by Western norms. He advocated for a selective approach, emphasizing the importance of adapting Western knowledge and technologies while preserving the core values inherent to Japanese society. This perspective offers a nuanced understanding of modernization, where the integration of foreign ideas does not necessitate the abandonment of indigenous identity. In fact, Fukuzawa believed that true progress lie in the synthesis of Western learning with Japanese culture, allowing for an evolution rather than a capitulation to foreign influences. This delicate balance would enable Japan to navigate its modernization without losing its essential character, thus fostering a unique Japanese path that remained firmly rooted in its own traditions. Such a strategy not only reaffirmed Japan’s sovereignty but also positioned it as a formidable player in the global arena, capable of engaging with the West on its own terms while still honoring its indigenous civilization. Maintaining this identity was crucial for Japan’s self-definition during a period of rapid change.
