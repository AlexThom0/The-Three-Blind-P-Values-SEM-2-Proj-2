These practices not only disrupt local ecosystems but also facilitate the spread of pathogens by creating dense populations of animals that are vulnerable to infections. As livestock and wildlife are pushed into closer proximity, the chances for cross-species transmission increase dramatically. This dynamic is particularly evident in the rapid rise of certain diseases that have jumped from animals to humans, with the trade-offs of increased agricultural productivity often overshadowing the hidden costs to public health. Environmental degradation driven by urban expansion further compounds these risks, as habitats are destroyed and animal populations are disrupted. The proliferation of travel and trade means that pathogens can be transmitted across borders with alarming speed. Therefore, not only does the renaming of diseases play a role in public perception, but it also reflects a broader struggle against the systemic factors that allow zoonotic diseases to thrive. Addressing these foundational issues may indeed be more critical than simply altering nomenclature, as it is the interaction between humans, animals, and environments that ultimately shapes the landscape of infectious diseases.
