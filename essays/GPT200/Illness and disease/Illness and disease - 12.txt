The belief was that the very essence of these creatures could transfer their vitality to the patient, attacking the malignancy that thrived within. This practice not only reflected a deep-seated connection between nature and medicine, but also showcased the struggle to comprehend cancer's insidious nature. The metaphor of the wolf, particularly, illustrated the predatory aspect of the disease—an invader lying in wait, much like the terror of a ravaging beast ready to feast on the suffering host. As practitioners navigated this murky terrain of folklore and empirical observation, their remedies became entwined with narratives of both beasts and physiology, blurring the lines between myth and medical science. By elevating animals as central players in treatment strategies, they attempted to assert control over a fearsome, seemingly uncontrollable condition. In seeking to alleviate the horror of cancer, these early healers employed not just surgical techniques but also a symbiotic relationship with the animal kingdom, drawing parallels between the organic and the pathological.
