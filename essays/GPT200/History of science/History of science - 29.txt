The normalization of such unethical practices is not merely an individual issue but reflects a broader systemic challenge that challenges the integrity of research institutions. When the competitive landscape rewards quantity over quality, researchers are incentivized to cut corners, leading to questionable methodologies and even fraudulent results. This trend is exacerbated by funding bodies and publication outlets that prioritize prestigious metrics, such as citation counts and journal impact factors, which further entrench these perverse incentives. Consequently, junior researchers, often acutely aware of their precarious positions within academia, may feel compelled to conform to these damaging norms, fearing that their careers hinge on meeting artificially high expectations. Social networks play a critical role in this dynamic, as scholars share strategies for maximizing output and navigating the demands of their institutions, even at the expense of ethical considerations. This shift in priorities not only impacts research integrity but also diminishes the value and credibility of scientific inquiry itself, creating an environment where robust peer review becomes an arduous task, overshadowed by political maneuvering and institutional interests. If left unaddressed, this cycle may lead to a further erosion of public trust in scientific research.
