The reliance on historical data to determine the height of seawalls overlooked the evolving nature of tsunamis, which can vary significantly in intensity and impact. While the 5.7-metre figure was rooted in past events, it did not account for the increasing severity of natural disasters attributed to climate change and geological shifts. In the years leading up to the construction, oceanographic studies suggested that ocean temperatures were rising, potentially leading to more powerful storms and more erratic seismic activity. Despite these warnings, decision-makers displayed a troubling level of confidence in historical benchmarks, thus downplaying the likelihood of unprecedented waves. Furthermore, many coastal communities, reconstructed after previous disasters, were unaware of the limitations of the adopted safety measures. Interviews with local residents revealed a prevailing belief that modern technology had sufficiently mitigated risks. This hubris not only obscured the lessons from prior calamities but also fostered vulnerabilities that put lives and infrastructure at further risk. The fixation on a singular historical event, rather than a comprehensive assessment of risks, marked a critical flaw in the strategy for coastal defense, leaving communities exposed to the unpredictable power of nature.
