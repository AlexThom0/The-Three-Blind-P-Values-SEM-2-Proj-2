This move would not merely have marked a personal triumph for Le Verrier but also signified a shift in the underlying narrative of celestial nomenclature. As astronomy evolved, the naming of celestial bodies became entangled with national pride and scientific legacy. The urge to claim ownership over discoveries compelled astronomers to adopt names that signified their cultural or political identities. While naming celestial bodies after mythological figures had offered a universal framework, the inclination to honor individual contributors blurred the lines of impartiality in science. It suggested a more overt association between celestial entities and nationalistic sentiments. Observers from different nations began to assert their claims with increasing fervor, leading to heated debates that transcended scientific discourse and delved into issues of sovereignty and recognition. This was not merely about naming a planet; it became a battleground for intellectual ownership and prestige, revealing how the cosmos could be embroiled in earthly rivalries, and challenging the idea of an ever-neutral cosmos. The implications of Le Verrier’s proposition would ripple through the astronomical community, forcing a reconsideration of how discoveries were celebrated or appropriated on the global stage.
