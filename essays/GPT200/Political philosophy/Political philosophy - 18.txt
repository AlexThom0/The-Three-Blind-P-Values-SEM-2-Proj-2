Instead of bridging divides, the Reformation intensified the existing schisms within Christianity, creating a framework where coexistence became synonymous with rivalry. With the newfound authority of Protestant and Catholic factions, theological differences were weaponized, leading to a heightened dedication to confessional purity. This period saw the emergence of state-sanctioned persecution, elevating the role of the church in civic matters and intertwining it with nationalism. Rather than a melting pot of ideas, Europe became a battleground for competing claims of truth, where dissenters faced dire consequences. In such an environment, the historical examples of tolerance from other traditions stood in stark contrast to the relentless pursuit of dominance within Christendom. These contrasting experiences highlight not only varying depths of religious tolerance across cultures but also how political power can shift the temporal advancements of societal coexistence toward fractious division. The legal frameworks that had once promised coexistence now faltered under the weight of sectarian zeal, reshaping the continent's spiritual landscape for generations to come.
