This concentration of power fostered an environment of exploitation, where workers faced not only the threat of job loss but also the pervasive fear of reprisals for organizing or advocating for their rights. Strikes often ended in violence, as company-hired private security forces or local law enforcement were deployed to suppress any attempts at solidarity among workers. Such brutal tactics exposed the fragility of workers' rights in an economic system primarily designed to benefit the capitalist class. Many sought refuge in existing unions, hoping to find collective strength through organized labor movements, yet even these institutions often struggled against systemic injustices, weakening their resolve. The tumultuous climate of the era defined a critical context for Debs and his comrades, illustrating how deeply intertwined the fight for political freedom was with the struggle for economic justice. As workers recognized their plight, their ability to mobilize for change became a powerful symbol of resistance, pushing against both judicial and corporate oppression in pursuit of a more equitable society.
