SENS Research aims not only to extend lifespan but also to enhance the quality of life in later years. This goal raises ethical questions about the implications of such advancements. If humans can easily reach extreme old age, we must confront societal shifts, including possible overpopulation and resource depletion. Moreover, the psychological impact on individuals who witness long-term changes in their loved ones and the world around them cannot be overstated. As Tithonus illustrates, merely elongating life does not guarantee happiness or fulfillment; in fact, it could lead to a deeper existential crisis. The potential for people to live extended lives without the vitality or vigor associated with youth poses a paradoxically grim alternative to the concept of death. In this context, bioethicists advocate for a balanced approach to gerontology, emphasizing that the wisdom gained through age should not become a burden marked by perpetual decline. The human experience inherently encapsulates loss, thus raising the question of whether engineered longevity truly serves human flourishing. Ultimately, it is imperative to consider how our pursuit of eternal youth interacts with the universal truths of life—suffering, love, loss, and the inevitability of death.
