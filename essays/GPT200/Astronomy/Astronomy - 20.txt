search for dark matter has led to numerous hypotheses, with candidates ranging from weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) to axions and sterile neutrinos. Each proposed particle brings with it unique properties and implications for both particle physics and cosmology. For instance, WIMPs, predicted by many extensions of the Standard Model, could potentially be detected through their rare interactions with ordinary matter. Theoretical frameworks suggest that if dark matter particles exist, they might be available in a specific mass range, making them targets for next-generation experiments. Moreover, the implications of discovering such particles would extend beyond confirming the existence of dark matter; it could unveil new physics that challenges our current understanding of the universe. Similarly, axions, with their very light mass and potential for exhibiting a peculiar behavior in strong magnetic fields, offer another tantalizing alternative for solving the dark matter mystery. Each avenue of inquiry not only aims to detect these elusive particles but also to piece together a broader understanding of how matter and energy have coalesced over cosmic time. The interconnectedness of these efforts highlights the collaborative nature of modern astrophysical research. Scientists continue to forge ahead, driven by the promise of unlocking these cosmic enigmas.
