Not only did light serve functional purposes—illuminating paths and safeguarding communities from nocturnal threats—but it also shaped social dynamics. The interplay of wealth and access to light created divisions among the populace; the affluent basked in the glow of superior candles, while the poorer classes relied on flickering, insufficient light sources. This disparity extended beyond mere luxuries, influencing social interactions and communal gatherings. Illuminated spaces became sites of social stratification, where the elite flaunted their resources, subtly reinforcing their status while the majority navigated the shadows. Furthermore, the growing prevalence of street lighting began to change the very nature of nightlife, prompting new cultural practices around evening leisure. Coffee houses buzzed with conversation, and streets once considered dangerous became more accessible and inviting. In this way, the emergence of artificial light not only transformed physical spaces but also the human experience of night, leading to evolving norms and expectations. Social lives adapted to the rhythms of light, intertwining the safety and joy derived from illumination with the complex tapestry of urban existence. Light, thus, became a powerful social currency.
