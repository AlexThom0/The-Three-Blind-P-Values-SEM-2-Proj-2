Lacking a parent star, their atmospheres may evolve independently, cooling and thickening as they lose heat slowly over time. Each rogue planet tells a story of cataclysmic events, of violent collisions and gravitational dances disrupting their once-stable formations. Recent studies suggest that free-floating planets might even capture traces of stellar material, remnants of their birthplaces, creating unique chemical signatures that can be detected through spectroscopy. This variability makes them intriguing subjects for understanding planetary formation and evolution beyond the traditional framework of stellar systems. Given their isolation, they may also represent a form of planetary evolution distinct from their star-bound counterparts. Such planets challenge our perceptions, offering a glimpse into the unexpected pathways of formation and the resilience of planetary bodies. Understanding these wanderers widens our scope of planetary science, emphasizing that the cosmos holds many surprises yet to be uncovered. Exploring the origins and characteristics of free-floating planets not only enriches our knowledge but also poses questions about the potential for life beyond conventional constraints of habitability.
