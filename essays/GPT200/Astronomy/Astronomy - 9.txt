This phenomenon, known as gravitational lensing, provides a powerful tool for mapping the elusive substance. By observing how light bends around massive structures, researchers can infer the distribution of mass, both visible and dark, within galaxies and clusters. These investigations have transformed the understanding of the universe's composition. The realization that ordinary matter constitutes merely a fraction of the cosmos has pushed scientists to reevaluate existing models and seek new paradigms in astrophysics. As dark matter continues to evade direct detection, the theories surrounding its properties and interactions have evolved. Some suggest it may interact only through gravity and possibly other unknown forces, while others propose the existence of various dark matter candidates, such as Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) or axions. The implications of dark matter extend beyond the immediate observational realm; they challenge fundamental assumptions about the universe's formation, structure, and fate. As a result, understanding dark matter could not only unveil the mechanics behind galactic rotation but also shed light on the very nature of reality itself.
