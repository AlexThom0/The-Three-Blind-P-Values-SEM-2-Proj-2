the concept of authenticity often hinges on the notion of origin, where an original work is venerated and its copy undervalued. This stark distinction tends to overshadow the nuanced reality of cultural practices, particularly in sacred contexts like that of Ise. Here, the continuous regeneration of treasures and structures challenges these Western assumptions by presenting a form of identity that embraces both continuity and renewal. Each new creation is not just a replacement but an affirmation of the original’s purpose, underscoring the philosophical depth behind its sacredness. The dual presence of replicas simultaneously enriches and complicates our understanding of what it means to be "original." Rather than a linear progression of value, we observe a circular dynamic where meaning is imbued through repetition and ritual. The treasures, in their ongoing production, reflect the importance of community and collective memory, anchoring these objects in a timeless narrative. As the cultural significance shifts from a singular original to multiple copies, we must reconsider our metrics for value and authenticity. This evolving understanding highlights the interplay between memory, culture, and materiality within religious traditions. Therefore, the temple treasures have become both an act of preservation and a revelation of evolving identities.
