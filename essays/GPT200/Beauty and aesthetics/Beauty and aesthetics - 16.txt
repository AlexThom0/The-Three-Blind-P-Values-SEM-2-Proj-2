The infant, engulfed in a sensory overload, begins to parse and categorize stimuli over time, leading to the ability to make distinctions between the senses. This evolution is critical not only in understanding personal identity but also in exploring how language and social context shape our interpretations of sensory input. Each moment of subsequent clarity brings with it new layers of meaning, influenced by prior experiences, social interactions, and cultural frameworks. Thus, our understanding of the world is not solely a reflection of raw sensory data but a richly textured tapestry woven from the threads of language and socialization. The interplay between innate sensory perception and the external world can be seen as foundational in the formation of consciousness itself, paving the way for complex thought and emotional depth. This foundational perceptual ambiguity and its subsequent clarification may explain the struggles of characters like Shelley’s monster, who, confronted with a world rife with sensations yet devoid of interpretation, grapples with the very essence of existence in a manner that resonates with the human condition.
