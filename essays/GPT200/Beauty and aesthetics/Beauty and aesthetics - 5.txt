In this new paradigm, rhythm and melody became subordinate to quantifiable elements, leading to a hierarchy that favored cerebral comprehension over elemental expression. This transition marked a critical juncture in the evolution of music, as it not only changed how music was created but also how it was perceived and valued within society. Communities once connected through shared musical experiences began to fragment, with formal education systems reinforcing the notion that true music must adhere to established rules. Those who came before, like the women who engaged in drumming and communal song, were gradually marginalized as their contributions were relegated to the background. The essence of music as a universal language, one deeply embedded in human experience and interaction, risked being lost to the complexities of classification and technical mastery. This raises essential questions about the inclusivity of music education today and how we can foster a more holistic approach that honors both numeracy and the raw, emotional power of sound. Embracing diverse methods of musical understanding can bridge this widening gap.
