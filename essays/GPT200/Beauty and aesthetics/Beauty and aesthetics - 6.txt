These enduring stereotypes not only reinforce damaging myths but also serve to restrict the social and political agency of black women. The Jezebel and Mammy archetypes create a binary that defines black femininity in terms that are convenient for a predominantly white society, compelling black women to oscillate between sexual availability and domestic servitude. This duality strips them of their individuality, reducing their experiences to mere caricatures. In popular culture, film, and advertising, these tropes are often recycled, perpetuating an insidious cycle of representation that fails to capture the complexity of their lives. Such narratives neglect the rich histories, contributions, and diverse identities of black women, illustrating how institutional forces work to erase their reality. Consequently, black women not only contend with the societal implications of these stereotypes but also bear the burden of challenging and reshaping perceptions. This struggle becomes evident in the works of contemporary black female artists, writers, and scholars who actively confront and dissect these limited portrayals, putting forth nuanced representations that assert agency and authenticity. By doing so, they carve out spaces for more genuine narratives that reflect their lived experiences and perspectives. These endeavors are essential in dismantling the harmful legacies of colonialism and slavery.
