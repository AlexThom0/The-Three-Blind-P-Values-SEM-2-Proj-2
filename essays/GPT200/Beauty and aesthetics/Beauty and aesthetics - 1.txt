Kant’s intervention shaped not only metaphysics but also the poetic landscape, challenging poets to navigate this new terrain where traditional notions of certainty seemed to evaporate. In rejecting the possibility of an all-encompassing rationality, he inadvertently pushed poets like Wordsworth and Coleridge to delve deeper into subjective experiences and personal interpretation. This shift encouraged emotional resonance to take precedence over empirical clarity, fostering a connection between nature and human emotion that was both profound and complex. Romantic poets increasingly embraced the fragmented nature of truth, celebrating the individual’s journey to meaning amidst uncertainty. The inherent contradictions in their work mirrored Kant’s own philosophical tensions: the struggle between aspiring towards completeness while acknowledging limits. As poetry transformed, it served as a vital space for exploring the interplay of reason and emotion, where beauty was discovered in the very act of grappling with ambiguity. This reflective process became integral to the poetic experience, inviting readers to engage with their own feelings and thoughts, thus enriching the dialogue between aesthetics and philosophy. In doing so, they not only responded to Kant’s critiques but also expanded the boundaries of what poetry could ultimately achieve.
