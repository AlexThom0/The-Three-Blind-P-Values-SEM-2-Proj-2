Traditional narrative structures are increasingly abandoned in favor of fragmented storytelling, where plotlines are interrupted or diverted in a way that reflects the chaotic pace of contemporary life. This approach produces a disjunctive experience, causing audiences to operate on a superficial level of engagement. Rather than immersing ourselves in a complete narrative, we find ourselves piecing together disparate elements, echoing Adorno’s concern about the loss of a holistic aesthetic experience. The emotional resonance that once flourished within cohesive works is diminished, transforming art into a spectacle rather than a profound exploration of the human condition. This commodification of art leads not only to a shallower engagement but also raises ethical questions about the societal implications of such art forms. When art becomes merely a collection of attention-grabbing moments, it risks detaching itself from deeper themes that challenge or inspire audiences, ultimately reinforcing the very societal structures that it could critique. In this context, the role of the audience shifts toward passive consumption, as we become spectators rather than participants in a richer cultural dialogue.
