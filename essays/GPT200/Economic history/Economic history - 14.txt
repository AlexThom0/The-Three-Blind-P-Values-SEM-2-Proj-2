The unification brought promises of progress, yet many felt abandoned as resources were siphoned away to the northern regions, leaving the south languishing in poverty. As agricultural livelihoods faltered, people turned to informal networks for support and protection. In this context, local figures began to emerge, filled with charisma and authority, presenting themselves as champions of the disenfranchised. They offered a semblance of order and security amidst chaos, appealing to a populace desperate for stability. Instead of state institutions, communities often relied on these leaders, who began to operate outside the boundaries of the law, fostering an environment ripe for the mafia to take root. The notion of loyalty and kinship among locals became intertwined with the mafia's operations, transforming their activities into something seen as both necessary and somewhat glamorous, deeply embedded in the local culture. This symbiotic relationship between the mafia and the Sicilian people set the groundwork for an enduring legacy that would influence the social and economic fabric of the island for generations.
