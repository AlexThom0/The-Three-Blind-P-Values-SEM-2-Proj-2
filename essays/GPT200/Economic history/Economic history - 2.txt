The concept of consumerism gained traction, as laypeople began perceiving material wealth not merely as a necessary evil but as an avenue to enrich their spiritual lives. This was evident in how churches and religious institutions became pivotal in the promotion and sale of items believed to enhance one's spiritual experience. The trade of indulgences and relics exemplified this merging of faith and commerce, reflecting society's growing desire to visualize and promote piety through tangible goods. Such practices, however, were contentious, often igniting debates about the moral implications of commodifying the divine. The anxiety among religious leaders about the potential corruption of faith by materialism illustrated the tensions at the heart of early capitalist undertakings. Paradoxically, as the Church sought to preserve spiritual integrity, its very actions fed into the economic machine, further entrenching the link between wealth and spirituality. This set the stage for later critiques of excessive consumption, rooted in deep-seated fears of idolatry and the erosion of authentic faith, foreshadowing the critiques that would later resonate in Enlightenment and Marxist thought.
