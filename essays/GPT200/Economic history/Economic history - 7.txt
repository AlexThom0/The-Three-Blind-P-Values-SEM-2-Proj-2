Countries that had previously been insulated from global market forces began to adopt measures favoring liberalization and openness. This change was driven not only by the necessity to stabilize their own economies but also by ideological shifts favoring market-driven approaches over state-controlled economies. Nations like China embraced reform, introducing market mechanisms that opened them up to foreign investment and trade. Meanwhile, the dismantling of trade barriers facilitated an influx of goods and capital, reshaping consumption patterns worldwide. The interplay between domestic reforms and international pressures created unique opportunities for countries looking to integrate into the global economy, allowing them to attract foreign direct investment. It was during this period that multinational corporations began to expand their operations abroad, seeking new markets to mitigate risks associated with domestic downturns. This transformation set the stage for a renewed wave of globalization, where countries that had once been considered peripheral began to assert their roles in the global market, experiencing both economic growth and the associated challenges of increased interdependence. The landscape of international trade was undeniably shifting.
