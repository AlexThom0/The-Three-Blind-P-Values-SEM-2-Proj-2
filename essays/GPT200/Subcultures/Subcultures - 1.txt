This ideological shift mirrors ancient philosophical debates but is now interwoven with a distinctly modern urgency, as existential risk looms large with climate change, artificial intelligence, and pandemics. In this framework, reviving the perennial quest for transcendence takes on a distinctly synthetic hue; it's not merely about ascension but about reengineering existence to conform to our desires. This desire is palpable in the rhetoric of those at the forefront of technological innovation, who frame their pursuits as benevolent acts designed to uplift humanity. Yet, while this ambition can inspire awe, it also raises profound ethical questions: what happens when the divine aspiration becomes commodified? Who decides what constitutes "betterment," and at whose expense? The audacity of believing we can redefine our essence and purpose echoes the ancient quest for divinity; however, in a world increasingly driven by algorithms and capital, the implications of such a transformation could be far-reaching and deeply unsettling.
