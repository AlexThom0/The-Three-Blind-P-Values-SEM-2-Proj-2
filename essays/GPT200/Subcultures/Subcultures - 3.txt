The underlying message conveyed by these representations was one of aspiration, positioning the models not merely as subjects but as embodiments of an aspirational lifestyle. This selective visibility reinforced prevailing beauty standards, asserting a narrow definition of health and desirability that marginalized those whose bodies differed from the ideal. In this environment, nudism's claims of liberation ironically played into a culture of conformity, where the freedom of the nude body was paradoxically tied to stringent aesthetic norms. In an effort to promote body positivity, the movement inadvertently perpetuated a visual hierarchy, privileging certain types of bodies while simultaneously excluding others. Indeed, the nudist portrayal of the human form aimed to promote a certain ideology of health that often overlooked the diverse realities of individual bodies. This tension between liberation and conformity remains crucial in understanding how nudism evolved, revealing the complexities of societal values surrounding body image and self-acceptance in the context of the early twentieth-century. The quest for an inclusive understanding of nudity was continually challenged by these ingrained biases.
