This term, steeped in the imagery of cave dwellers, reinforced the dichotomy between the ruling elite and the disenfranchised populace. It also reflected a broader cultural fascination with subterranean life that permeated various forms of art and literature. Writers like George Gissing captured the stark realities faced by the working class, often juxtaposing their struggles against the backdrop of the affluent society thriving above. Such representations shed light on the social injustices that persisted in plain sight, demonstrating how invisible networks of oppression intertwined with the physical geography of urban landscapes. As these narratives unfolded, they revealed not only the hardships experienced by the downtrodden but also the resilience and agency of those who dared to challenge their fates. The contrasts between light and dark, visibility and obscurity, were not merely thematic devices; they served to critique and dissect the inadequacies of the societal structures in place, compelling audiences to confront the moral implications of their own complicity in the status quo.
