In conversations with residents, I encountered a profound sense of vulnerability that permeates everyday life. Structured routines were disrupted, with many now placing their social events, work commitments, and even personal milestones around the memory of that fateful day. In this heightened state of alertness, individuals began to redefine their relationships with communal spaces, often avoiding crowded areas that once felt safe and familiar. Moreover, the collective memory of the disaster has spurred discussions about infrastructure and preparedness, leading to a dual focus on personal safety and collective resilience. As a result, memorial gatherings intended to honor the victims transform into places of anxiety, overshadowed by the specter of another possible calamity. This lingering apprehension complicates the hard-won conversations about rebuilding and justice, as remembrance becomes interwoven with the pervasive uncertainty of living in a city where the earth itself is seen as a volatile force. The legacy of that seismic event reshapes not only the urban landscape but also the very fabric of social life in Mexico City.
