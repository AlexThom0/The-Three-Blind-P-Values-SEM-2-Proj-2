This hierarchical framework, however, raises critical philosophical questions. The privileging of unitive experiences over dualistic encounters suggests a narrow interpretation of spirituality that may exclude significant aspects of personal religious experience. If one considers the emotional and relational dimensions of dualist encounters, it becomes clear that these can equally invoke profound transformations in the participant's life. For many practitioners, the fervor and fear surrounding these encounters can catalyze personal growth and a deeper understanding of the divine. Furthermore, the social contexts in which these experiences occur play a vital role in shaping their meanings. Cultures steeped in a rich tapestry of traditions often interpret ecstatic encounters through the lens of communal values and historical narratives, emphasizing the importance of relationality in our conceptualization of the sacred. Consequently, one must question whether it is appropriate to apply a reductive scale to experiences that are inherently complex and variegated. Such an approach risks overlooked narratives that do not conform to a singular, unitive interpretation, thus potentially marginalizing vast swaths of spiritual insight available to humanity. In this light, the landscape of mystical experience becomes not merely a segment of human consciousness to be quantified but a rich territory deserving of a multiplicity of voices and interpretations.
