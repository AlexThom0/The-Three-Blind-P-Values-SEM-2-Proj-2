Milgrom proposed a radical adjustment to our understanding of gravity through a new theory known as Modified Newtonian Dynamics, or MOND. This theory posits that at very low accelerations, the gravitational force behaves differently than the laws laid out by Newton. Instead of maintaining its inverse-square nature, gravity would begin to lose strength in a predictable manner, allowing orbital speeds to remain stable at greater distances from galactic centers. By reshaping the fundamental equations of motion, MOND effectively addresses the peculiar observation of flat rotation curves without necessitating the existence of dark matter. This shift in perspective not only provided a potential resolution to the discrepancies in rotational dynamics but also reignited discussions about the underlying principles of gravitational interactions. Milgrom's work has led a segment of the astrophysics community to reconsider longstanding assumptions about the universe's composition and structure. More importantly, it has inspired further inquiries into the frameworks that govern cosmic phenomena, suggesting that our understanding of gravity and mass may still require considerable refinement. MOND stands as a pivotal challenge to established views in theoretical physics.
