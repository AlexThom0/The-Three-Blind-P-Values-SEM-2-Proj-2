Consider the vast distances between stars and galaxies; light from these celestial bodies takes years, if not millions or billions, to reach us. Thus, what we perceive as current is often a reflection of history captured in light. This temporal disparity emphasizes that our sense of ‘now’ is inherently flawed, intertwined with our perspective and the limits of our observational capabilities. Even as new stars are born and others meet their end, our understanding is tuned to a fraction of these occurrences, filtered through the lens of time. Furthermore, in the fabric of the universe, everything is in constant flux, which leads to a misconception about the simultaneity of events. For example, while a star may explode into a supernova at one moment, we may not witness it until many years later—if at all. This dichotomy reveals the limitations of human perception and the tools we use to interpret the cosmos. Ultimately, recognizing this complexity transforms our understanding of existence, offering richer insights into the nature of time and the events that define our universe.
