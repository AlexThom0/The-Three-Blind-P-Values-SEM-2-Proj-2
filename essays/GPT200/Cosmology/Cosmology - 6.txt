This phenomenon aligns with the predictions made by Friedmann, whose early theoretical contributions set the stage for later empirical validation. Nevertheless, Slipher's findings faced skepticism; the prevailing view was still heavily influenced by Newtonian mechanics, which struggled to accommodate such dynamic changes in the universe. Yet, as astronomers began to compile data, the weight of evidence skewed in favor of an expansionary model. The implications were profound: if galaxies were moving apart, did it mean they were once part of a singular entity? This inquiry opened a torrent of questions about the origins of cosmic structures and the fundamental laws governing them. Researchers sought to refine their measurements, and with advancing technology came a clearer picture of cosmic velocities. Observatories worldwide started to compare their findings, leading to a convergence of data that would ultimately affirm the expansive nature of the cosmos. Gaining momentum, this new understanding began to reshape not just astronomy, but also physics, prompting a reevaluation of theories about gravity, time, and the very fabric of space itself.
