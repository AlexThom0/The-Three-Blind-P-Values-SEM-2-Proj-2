It is noteworthy that both Voltaire and Kircher reflect larger discourses surrounding food ethics and cultural practices of their times, illustrating how perceptions of morality and humanity were often intertwined with dietary choices. Voltaire's humorous critique serves to expose the hypocrisy of an elite society that revels in luxury while turning a blind eye to the brutal realities of food production. Conversely, Kircher’s attack emerges from a rigid framework, clinging to a Western paradigm that equated vegetarianism with moral superiority, yet failed to recognize the complexities of cultural identity and ecological balance inherent in diverse diets. The backlash against Kircher's perspectives signified a gradual evolution in understanding and accepting multiple approaches to food, paving the way for nuanced conversations about ethics in consumption. By exploring these polarities, we gain insight into how cultural values influence the ways societies construct their moral landscapes around food. Today, as we confront the consequences of industrial agriculture, the philosophical debates ignited by these early thinkers remain remarkably relevant. The challenge lies in reconciling the clash between traditional practices and modern ethical standards, which continues to shape contemporary discussions on sustainability and animal welfare. Each choice we make as consumers is laden with historical significance.
