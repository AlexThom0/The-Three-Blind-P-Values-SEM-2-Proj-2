The act of vandalism becomes symbolic, a tangible display of grief and retribution that transcends mere property rights. The father’s actions reflect a collective sentiment: that objects connected to tragic events are imbued with their own kind of weight, a weight that society instinctively acknowledges and often endorses. This brings to light the complex relationship between human beings and the artifacts of their lives. When a tool or vehicle is associated with loss, it acquires a narrative that shapes our perception. We find ourselves grappling with the question of ownership versus accountability. Should the object be held accountable for the actions of its user? In many ways, the father's destruction of the motorbike is less about retaliation and more about reclaiming agency over a painful loss. It is an act that communicates a visceral rejection of the normalization of tragedy—one that seeks to re-establish a sense of justice in a world that often feels indifferent. The emotional landscape of loss encompasses not just the victim but also the tools, vehicles, and artifacts that become unwitting participants in these narratives of sorrow.
