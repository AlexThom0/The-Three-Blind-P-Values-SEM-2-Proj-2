The designations of welfare improvements often serve to placate consumer concerns rather than enact substantive change. Measures like free-range farming or organic certification can carry a veneer of compassion; however, they operate within the same exploitative system that commodifies sentient beings. The fundamental issue lies not only in how animals are treated but also in the premise that their lives are expendable commodities, valued solely for their output. While some improvements create less overt suffering, they do not dismantle the underlying hierarchy that places animal lives in a subservient role to human needs. This results in a paradox where even advancements in care are framed within a business model that fails to challenge the ethical implications of ownership and exploitation. Thus, discussions around animal welfare often overlook this critical dimension, inadvertently reinforcing the status quo. Only by addressing the systemic roots of animal commodification and demanding a reevaluation of ownership can we aspire to genuinely humane treatment. The transition from property to beings with inherent rights is essential for fostering an ethical relationship with the animal world.
