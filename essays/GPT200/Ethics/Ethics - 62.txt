If moral truths are contingent solely on divine approval, then ethical behavior becomes a mere transactional arrangement. This viewpoint reduces morality to a fluctuating concept, dictated by the whims of a deity rather than deriving from intrinsic human values. In this light, the notion of moral absolutism fades away, leaving us to question the integrity of actions independent of divine command. While some argue an omnipotent being provides the ultimate moral compass, this framework risks justifying atrocities previously deemed reprehensible with the simple phrase, "God permits it." Hence, to engage in ethical discourse necessitates acknowledging that moral philosophies must rest on universal principles accessible to all, regardless of belief in a higher power. Ethics grounded in empathy, justice, and mutual respect promote a robust understanding of right and wrong, free from the erratic nature of divine mandates. Such an approach honors the notion that human beings possess the capacity for compassion and reasoning, thereby fostering an environment where morality can flourish uninhibited by theological constructs. In this way, the conversation shifts from divine endorsement to the celebration of shared human experiences and values.
