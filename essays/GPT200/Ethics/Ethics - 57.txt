The new government encouraged citizens to adopt a narrative of victimhood, focusing on the hardships of displacement while downplaying or neglecting the complicity of many in supporting the Nazi regime. This selective memory allowed a significant portion of the population to avoid grappling with their own roles in the atrocities. The dichotomy of victim and perpetrator created a psychological barrier, enabling individuals to maintain a comfortable self-image in the face of collective guilt. Conversely, East Germany emphasized a different narrative that framed the socialist state as a bulwark against fascism, further complicating the discourse surrounding accountability. Here, the struggle was not merely against external enemies but also an internal challenge to reconcile the regime's failures with its ideologies. Thus, while both halves of the country engaged in their own forms of historical reckoning, the disparities in their approaches highlighted the complexities of memory and accountability in post-war German society. Understanding these diverging paths is crucial to grasp the broader implications of how history is remembered and utilized in the political landscape.
