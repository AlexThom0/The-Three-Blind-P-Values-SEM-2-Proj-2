For my family, this ritual is not merely a form of respect; it is a connection to our lineage, a bridge spanning generations. The keun jeol becomes a sacred act, embodying the weight of history within each bow. In those stilled moments, I can almost hear the echoes of voices long gone, feel their stories weaving through the air, intertwining with our own. My mother’s insistence on propriety makes more sense now, each gesture a lesson in humility and veneration, a reminder that we are but fleeting presences in a lineage that stretches back, tethered firmly to the earth beneath us. This act of bowing feels strangely liberating; as I release all tension, my thoughts shift from the weight of expectation to the reverence of gratitude. Together, we are not just honoring our ancestors; we’re affirming our existence in the continuum of their sacrifices. As we rise, the sense of shared purpose fills the space, knitting our spirits together amidst the vibrant colors of autumn and the silent company of those who came before us.
