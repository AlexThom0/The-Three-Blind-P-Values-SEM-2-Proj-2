For humans, the toddler stage represents a crucial expansion of this juvenile phase, characterized by increased mobility, exploration, and social interaction. During this time, toddlers exhibit a remarkable capacity for learning, driven by an evolutionary imperative to acquire skills and knowledge necessary for survival in a complex social environment. This extended period of dependency allows for the development of cognitive and social competencies that are foundational to our species' success. As toddlers engage with their environment, they begin to form social bonds and navigate their relationships with caregivers and peers. These interactions facilitate not only emotional growth but also the acquisition of language and culture, which are unique to humans. The evolutionary advantages of such an extended childhood become evident, as they enhance the odds of survival into adulthood and increase the likelihood of passing on cultural knowledge, tools, and social norms that define human societies. This complex interplay of development, social learning, and environmental interaction underscores the unique trajectory of human growth.
