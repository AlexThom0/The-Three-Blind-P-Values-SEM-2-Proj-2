The rise of the child welfare movement introduced a new discourse around childhood, emphasizing the need for protection and nurturing of children. This advocacy highlighted the inherent value of childhood as a period of development rather than a time for economic productivity. Legislative changes began to emerge, implementing minimum age requirements and restricting working hours for children, reflecting a growing societal recognition of their need for education and play. Yet, even as these protections were enacted, a paradox emerged: while childhood was increasingly idealized, notions of childhood innocence were also leveraged to market goods and services. Just as the ‘new mom economy’ commodifies the experiences of motherhood, the idealization of children has resulted in their consumption in various forms—from toys to media features. Parents are often caught in a balancing act, navigating societal expectations while promoting a notion of childhood that fits within the commercial landscape. This ongoing tension underscores the complexities faced by today’s families as they attempt to safeguard their children while grappling with the demands of modern parenthood.
