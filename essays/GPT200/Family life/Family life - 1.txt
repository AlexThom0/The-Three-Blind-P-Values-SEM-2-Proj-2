The Runa community, with its intricate web of shared responsibilities, emphasized communal raising over individual parenting practices. Neighbors had assumed their right to intervene, showcasing a belief that every child belonged to the collective rather than solely to one family unit. It was disorienting at first; the instinct to protect my child surged against the backdrop of these communal values. As I watched familiar faces come and go, confidence in my neighbors' intentions clashed with my deep-seated anxieties about motherhood. Each incident chipped away at my understanding of familial boundaries and revealed a critical lesson in trust within this tightly knit community. I began to reflect on how my background, steeped in Western ideologies of parenting, shaped my perceptions of autonomy and responsibility. While I initially saw the neighbors' actions as intrusive, I slowly recognized them as expressions of care, love, and interconnectedness—hallmarks of Runa culture that formed the foundation of their way of life.
