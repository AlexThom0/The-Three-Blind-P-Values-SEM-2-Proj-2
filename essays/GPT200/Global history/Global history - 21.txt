As individuals faced grueling hours in dimly lit spaces, they mourned a loss of community and connection. This longing for the pre-industrial sense of time and place was evident in their protests. Voices rose against the inhumane conditions, echoing the desire for a restored human-centric pace. They sought not only a reorganization of work hours but also a recognition of their humanity amidst machines and timers. Each change in factory regimes exacerbated existing fractures in family ties and social relations, with many workers not knowing their neighbors, let alone their kin. The perseverance of this discontent was crucial, as it laid the groundwork for a broader movement advocating for labor rights. As people gathered to discuss their grievances, these meetings became a vital space for solidarity, bridging the disparate experiences of workers throughout the city. The demand for a coherent time structure represented a pivotal turning point in labor relations and was a direct challenge to the capitalist structures that dictated their lives.
