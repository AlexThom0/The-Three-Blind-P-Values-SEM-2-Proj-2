Such associations led to a dual narrative where Jews were portrayed as both the cunning financiers and the scapegoats for broader economic anxieties. This polarization served a purpose for the Christian merchants, who, while grappling with their own questionable practices, could deflect scrutiny by invoking the specter of the Jewish usurer. By positioning Jews as the quintessential figures of financial irregularity, these merchants attempted to obscure their own complicity in usury, thereby reinforcing a social order that marginalized Jewish contributions to trade and industry. This dynamic not only intensified religious prejudice but also facilitated a collective amnesia regarding the economic interdependencies between Christians and Jews. The merchants’ rhetoric found resonance in a larger cultural milieu that viewed wealth with suspicion, further entrenching stereotypes that would persist for centuries. Economic trends increasingly obscured the reality that many Jews played vital roles as intermediaries in evolving markets, simultaneously fueling and being exploited by the burgeoning capitalist frameworks. As these narratives took hold, a more complex understanding of economic ethics emerged, necessitating a reevaluation of who bore the brunt of economic censure during turbulent times. The resulting discourse shaped perceptions that would echo through history, creating enduring myths around Jewish financial practices.
