The CCP's characterization of Confucianism as a relic of feudalism contrasts sharply with the nationalistic and cultural currents that resurface in times of turmoil. As the party promotes a narrative that vilifies tradition, it simultaneously adopts selective aspects of Confucian ideals to bolster its legitimacy. This paradox raises questions about the fluid nature of ideology in governance, where ancient wisdom can be repurposed for modern statecraft. Moreover, the resurgence of such cultural references indicates a public yearning for stability and continuity, especially during periods marked by political upheaval and social discontent. The 1989 protests were not merely a repudiation of the regime but also an articulation of a broader desire for moral and ethical grounding within a rapidly changing society. Thus, the disconnection between the state’s ideological stance and the citizens' aspirations underscores the enduring relevance of Confucian thought, challenging the notion that Mao’s era obliterated previous cultural frameworks. This historical interplay of ideology and culture reveals the resilience of traditional values, often bending, but never completely breaking under the weight of political mandate.
