The sheer scale of loss and the senselessness of the violence led many to question not only the legitimacy of the British imperial project but also their own roles within it. The disillusionment was compounded by the prevalent sectarian divisions that colored interactions among soldiers, often leading to a deeply ingrained cynicism about loyalty and national identity. As these Irish servicemen returned home, they found themselves grappling with a new, harsher political landscape, one starkly different from the one they left behind. This transition was not merely a matter of geographical return; it reshaped their worldviews and ignited a fervor for social change. The experiences of O’Sullivan and Dunne serve as compelling examples of how military service could incite a profound shift in consciousness, pushing individuals toward radical political ideologies. Their journeys mirrored a broader trend among Irish veterans, who became increasingly politicized and vocal, catalyzing movements for autonomy and rights that would define the post-war era in Ireland.
