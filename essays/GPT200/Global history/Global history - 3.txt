Trade routes flourished across the continent, linking wealthy states like Mali and Ghana to regions far beyond their borders, demonstrating Africa's active role in global commerce. The connections established were not simply one-sided exchanges; they included the movement of ideas, technologies, and people, contributing to a complex intercontinental dialogue. Historical records indicate that Africa engaged in a form of imperialism well before European contact, shaping social hierarchies and governance structures that aligned with the broader dynamics of the time. These indigenous forms of expansion and influence must be acknowledged to grasp the full scope of African history. Misinterpretations arise when we ignore the diverse experiences of African states that thrived in an interconnected world. A more nuanced understanding of precolonial dynamics reveals not only the agency of African empires but also highlights the vibrancy of their interactions with neighboring regions, challenging oversimplified narratives of colonization as a primarily European phenomenon. By reevaluating these historical trajectories, we can better appreciate the multifaceted legacies that shaped both Africa and the globe.
