In this complex landscape, cultural heritage acts as both a battleground and a bridge. Nationalist narratives frame heritage in terms of specific identities, often leading to the valorization of select histories while marginalizing others. This selective acknowledgment risks creating a fragmented understanding of cultural significance, where only those narratives aligned with the dominant power are preserved and celebrated. Conversely, the emergence of transnational frameworks adds layers of complexity to how heritage is perceived and protected. Agencies like UNESCO attempt to mediate these tensions by promoting a common understanding of cultural significance that transcends borders. Yet, the challenge remains: can truly universal values coexist with local identities without eroding the latter? The push for a shared heritage often uncovers underlying power dynamics, where the voices of less dominant cultures can be overlooked in favor of a homogenized global narrative. This presents a paradox; while striving for inclusivity, the risk of disenfranchising local identities persists. Consequently, a critical examination of these dynamics becomes essential for understanding cultural heritage’s role in both fostering national pride and facilitating global unity. heritage is not merely a collection of artifacts, but a living dialogue of diverse narratives shaping human experience.
