This emphasis limited the understanding of historical events as interconnected phenomena, reducing them to isolated incidents. In contrast, global history sought to transcend these boundaries, encouraging historians to engage with complex narratives that recognize cross-cultural exchanges, migrations, and the global implications of local events. By examining how goods, people, and ideas flowed across borders, scholars aimed to provide richer, more nuanced accounts of the past that reflect the interconnectedness of human experiences. This shift opened avenues for new research methodologies, drawing from disciplines such as anthropology and sociology to deepen the analysis of historical events. Furthermore, it illuminated the influence of global forces on national histories, challenging traditional historiographical practices. The popularity of global history underscored a growing recognition of the importance of context and relationships in understanding the past, suggesting that historical narratives are not merely about the events themselves, but about the complex webs of interaction that shaped those events. This evolution allowed for a more holistic viewpoint that resonated with contemporary issues rooted in globalization, thereby reinforcing history’s relevance in today’s rapidly changing world.
