This infringement impacts not only their survival but also their social structures. In natural settings, coyotes can form complex family units and maintain vital social bonds. However, in cities, these social groups may break down as individuals are isolated by development and urban sprawl. The stress of living in proximity to humans can lead to maladaptive behaviors, such as scavenging from trash or venturing too close to populated areas in search of food. Understanding these dynamics is essential for developing effective management strategies that respect both the wildlife and human populations. As urban areas continue to expand, it is crucial to create coexistence plans that mitigate the risks to coyotes while also addressing the concerns of residents. This requires a rethinking of how we perceive these animals, moving beyond the archetype of the cunning predator to recognize their complexities and needs. Only then can we foster environments where both humans and coyotes can thrive.
