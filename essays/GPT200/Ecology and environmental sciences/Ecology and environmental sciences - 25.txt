As communities migrated or fell into decline, vast tracts of land returned to their primal states. This return of flora and fauna was not merely a recovery but a complex resurgence, one that breached the boundaries of historical perception. With each passing decade, species once thought to be lost began to reappear in the shadows created by derelict farms and abandoned towns. In this transformation, however, lay the duality of rejuvenation and neglect; the burgeoning wildlife flourished while the very human cultures that had once tended to the land faded away. Tomaž recounted how local foragers and herbalists, namely the elders of the community, once possessed a profound relationship with these woods, a link that faded as urban migration swallowed younger generations. The knowledge of seasons, plants, and creatures that formed a symbiotic understanding of the forest's rhythms was dwindling. Thus, as biodiversity thrived, a cultural disconnect echoed through the trees, amplifying a sense of loss interwoven with the new vibrancy of these reclaimed spaces. This tension heightened the enchantment of the forest, revealing layers of history both rich and melancholic.
