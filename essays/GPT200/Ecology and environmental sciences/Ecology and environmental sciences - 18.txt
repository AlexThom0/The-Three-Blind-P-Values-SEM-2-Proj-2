The emphasis on a national standard for poultry was not solely about enhancing product quality; it also reflected the socio-economic dynamics of post-war America. The creation of the ideal broiler was intertwined with rising consumer expectations and the growing demand for poultry as a primary protein source. As tastes evolved, so did the strategies employed by poultry leaders, who recognized that a more muscular bird could not only satisfy appetites but also boost profitability for producers at every level. The involvement of scientific expertise underscored a shift towards industrialized farming, where genetics and breeding practices became pivotal. Additionally, the disparate representation within the committee raised questions about inclusivity in agricultural innovation. Small-scale farmers, often sidelined in favor of larger agricultural interests, had insights that could inform a more comprehensive approach to breeding. Yet, the committee’s approach focused on uniformity, driven by the vision of a ‘perfect’ chicken that catered to evolving market demands while neglecting the diverse culinary traditions that celebrated the bird’s variations. This drive for standardization marked a significant shift in the poultry industry, steering it towards a homogenized product that ultimately altered consumer perceptions and culinary practices surrounding chicken.
