This approach mirrors his philosophical convictions; he views the body as a vessel not merely to be preserved but to be upgraded and improved upon. In his mind, cryonics represents a radical extension of human possibility, a tangible manifestation of humanity's desire to transcend its biological limitations. Each decision he makes in designing these structures reflects this belief—not just to outlast life but to ultimately defy death itself. Critics often overlook this underlying motivation, dismissing cryonics as a mere extension of denial. However, Bauge's work aims to spark a broader conversation about mortality, opportunity, and the ethics of life extension. To him, the buildings are more than cold, hard structures; they symbolize hope, cautionary tales of what society might lose if it fails to challenge the finality of death. The robust materials and designs are not just functional; they are a poignant critique of a world resigned to aging and demise. As he continues to build, he invites others to reconsider their assumptions about life, death, and what it means to exist in a universe governed by time's relentless march.
