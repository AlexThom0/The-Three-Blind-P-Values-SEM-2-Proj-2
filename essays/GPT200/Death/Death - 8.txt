Amid rapid industrialization and scientific advancement, people found solace in the idea that death was not an end, but rather a transition to a different state of existence. Families grieving their losses saw mediums not as frauds but as bridges to the deceased, affirming their emotions and fostering a communal experience of mourning. This need for reassurance allowed spiritualism to flourish across diverse demographics, transcending social classes and educational backgrounds. Even the most astute skeptics, drawn into the mystery of communicated messages, often found themselves entranced by the performances that blended showmanship with spiritual yearning. The struggles between belief and disbelief spawned a complex social dynamic, revealing a society caught in the tension between emerging rationalist perspectives and enduring emotional needs. As the excitement around séance gatherings grew, it also attracted those looking to capitalize on the public's fascination, compromising the authenticity of many who were genuinely seeking answers from their loved ones. Within this milieu of hope and deception, the boundaries between the real and the imagined became increasingly blurred.
