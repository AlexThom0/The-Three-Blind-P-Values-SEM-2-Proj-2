At this stage, they may describe death in terms of physical transitions or transformations, associating it with activities rather than a definitive endpoint. For many, the concept of death is intertwined with their understanding of absence—moments when a person is simply not present, rather than a permanent departure from life. The idea that a deceased person might be 'gone forever' is vague, almost alien. Moreover, children's interpretations of loss are heavily influenced by the narratives they encounter, from bedtime stories to family discussions, which often simplify or sanitize the reality of death. They may hear analogies suggesting that the dead are in heaven or simply asleep, reinforcing a transient view of existence. As they progress toward age six and beyond, their cognitive abilities expand, allowing them to grasp more complex ideas about mortality, such as the finality of death. This development can prompt deeper emotional responses as they begin to connect loss with feelings of grief and sorrow, marking a crucial transition in their understanding of life's impermanence and the nature of relationships.
