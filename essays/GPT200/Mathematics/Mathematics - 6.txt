Núñez argues that the concept of a dedicated number module is too simplistic and overlooks the complexities of human cognition. He posits that while our brains exhibit a response to numerical changes, this does not imply that our understanding of numbers exists independently of cultural or environmental influences. The varied ways in which different cultures conceptualize numbers indicate that numerical ability is largely constructed through social learning and experience rather than a purely biological predisposition. This perspective suggests that human cognitive development is a blend of innate capabilities and experiential learning, shaped by interactions with the environment. Just as mastering a sport requires practice and adaptation of inherited skills, so too does numerical understanding evolve through exposure to counting systems, language, and mathematical concepts. The interplay between our biological endowment and cultural context produces a rich tapestry of numerical competence, challenging the notion of an isolated cognitive module. Thus, our grasp of numbers is not just a reflection of our brain's wiring but also a dynamic construct influenced by our social environments and the educational systems we engage with. This complex interplay makes it evident that cognition transcends simple categorization as purely innate or learned.
