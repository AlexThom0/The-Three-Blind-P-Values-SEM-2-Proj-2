Drawing on his exposure to the works of François Guizot and Adolphe Quetelet, he proposed that instead of merely counting individuals, the census should collect data on various social and demographic indicators, such as age, occupation, and education. This multifaceted approach would not only provide a clearer picture of the population but also facilitate deeper insights into societal trends and challenges. By understanding the population's composition, policymakers could tailor their strategies to address specific needs, whether it be healthcare, education, or infrastructure development. Additionally, this innovative methodology raised questions about the role of statistics in governance, suggesting that data could be an essential tool for social reform. Quételet's vision demonstrated a shift from viewing the census simply as a count to recognizing it as a vital instrument for informing public policy. As discussions progressed, the notion of utilizing statistical methods began to gain traction among government officials and scholars alike, laying the groundwork for more systematic and humane approaches to population management.
