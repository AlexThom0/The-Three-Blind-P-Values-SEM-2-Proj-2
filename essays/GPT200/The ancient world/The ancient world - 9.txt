The reverence for ancient Rome among American elites underscores a troubling allegiance to a narrative that privileges whiteness and imperialism. This admiration was often coupled with a selective interpretation of Roman history, conveniently ignoring the complexities of its imperial conquests and the subjugation of diverse cultures. Figures like Cicero and Cato were revered not solely for their philosophical contributions but as symbols of a supposed moral superiority that resonated with the American ethos of the time. This appropriative reading of history reflected a desire to legitimize contemporary power structures and social hierarchies. Consequently, the idealization of Rome allowed these founding figures to carve out a narrative where liberty and justice were framed predominantly for white men, sidelining the voices and experiences of those who did not fit that mold. The philosophical underpinnings of their arguments served to perpetuate systemic inequalities, casting those outside the dominant group as unworthy of the rights and privileges celebrated in their rhetoric. This cycle of appropriation and justification reveals the deep-seated connections between history, ideology, and the maintenance of racial hierarchies that continue to shape American society.
