By examining the contexts in which big data operates, we reveal the values and biases embedded within the algorithms that govern decision-making. Understanding big data is not merely about analyzing statistics; it is about critically assessing how these systems affect individuals on micro and macro scales. Disparate access to technology can exacerbate existing social inequalities, leading to data exploitation rather than empowerment. As various stakeholders influence and interact with data systems, it becomes essential to interrogate whose voices are amplified and whose are rendered invisible. In doing so, we might explore alternative models that prioritize ethical considerations alongside technical feasibility. This perspective emphasizes not only the consequences of data manipulation but also the potential resilience of communities to reclaim agency over their information. Thus, the ongoing discourse around big data should prioritize inclusivity, ensuring that the human experience is central to the technology we create and the systems we build.
