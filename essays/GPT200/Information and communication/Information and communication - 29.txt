In contrast to this broader understanding of nature, many environmental psychologists advocate for a more restricted definition that emphasizes the importance of untouched ecosystems. This perspective reinforces a notion of nature as a refuge, distinct from the encroachments of urbanization and technology. The romanticized view often hinges on the idea that authentic experiences of nature can only occur in pristine environments, measured by the absence of human activity. Yet, this binary division risks oversimplifying the intricate relationship between humanity and the natural world. Increasingly, research suggests that urban environments, often dismissed as artificial, can also foster strong connections to nature through green spaces, urban biodiversity, and sustainable infrastructure. The interplay between natural elements and human innovations challenges the dichotomy, urging a re-evaluation of what it means to experience nature in an ever-evolving landscape. This shift not only broadens our understanding of natural spaces but also invites a deeper exploration of how our emotional ties to these environments can transform in response to urban development. Ultimately, recognizing the value of these interactions may lead to a more inclusive perspective on nature, encouraging a holistic approach to environmental psychology that embraces both untouched and urbanized landscapes.
