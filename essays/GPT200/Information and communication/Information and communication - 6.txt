This perspective not only distorts the realities of their achievements but also reinforces a dangerous narrative that underpins systems of inequality. By simplistically categorizing cultures as either ‘modern’ or ‘primitive,’ McLuhan and thinkers of his ilk unwittingly support colonial ideologies that devalue non-European experiences. The implications extend beyond academia into broader societal views, where such narratives perpetuate stereotypes and justify colonial dominance. The resulting dichotomy diminishes the complexities of cultural identities, as if the trajectory of technological and intellectual development were a privilege reserved solely for the West. Consequently, this misconstrued framework not only shapes scholarly discourse but also influences policy and perception in contemporary contexts. Emphasizing this disconnection disallows a nuanced understanding of historical trajectories and contemporary realities, culminating in an ever-pressing need to reevaluate these entrenched narratives. Recognizing the multiplicity of voices and experiences is vital for a more inclusive understanding of global history.
