Initial responses indicated a range of aspirations, with many students projecting highly favourable outcomes based on their positive visualisations. However, as grades were released, a noticeable shift occurred; students who had engaged in optimistic thinking often reported feelings of disappointment when faced with less-than-expected results. This led to a question about the nature of these fantasies: were they genuinely uplifting, or did they create unrealistic expectations that could culminate in deeper disillusionment? To address this, we monitored students’ self-reported emotional states alongside their actual academic performances throughout the semester. Our focus on this feedback loop—where optimism meets reality—aimed to clarify whether such fantasies shielded students from depression or inadvertently fueled a cycle of negativity once their expectations were unmet. The findings thus far suggest a nuanced relationship between positive thinking and academic pressures, implying that while optimism serves a purpose in bolstering immediate confidence, it may also set the stage for a more profound psychological toll when reality fails to align with hopeful projections.
