This initiative was not merely about introducing computers into the library setting; it was a pioneering effort aimed at fundamentally transforming how users interacted with information. Atherton and Katzer envisioned a platform that would facilitate easier navigation through vast amounts of data, anticipating the future of information retrieval. They incorporated user feedback into the development process, recognizing early on that usability would be crucial to the project's success. Atherton's experiences as a cross-reference editor provided her with unique insights into the challenges users faced when seeking information, prompting her to prioritize a seamless integration of search functionalities. This foresight laid a crucial groundwork for the increasing reliance on digital tools in libraries. SUPARS would soon become emblematic of the shift towards a more interconnected and technologically advanced landscape in library science, one that mirrored the rapid advancements occurring in the broader digital environment. By embracing both the potential of computers and the needs of library patrons, Atherton and her team positioned themselves as leaders in a transformative era for information access. The project would ultimately influence library practices for years to come.
