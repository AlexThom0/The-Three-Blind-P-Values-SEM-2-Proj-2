This enforcement of customary norms served to create an illusion of autonomy within native communities while simultaneously reinforcing the power dynamics established by colonial rule. The classification of individuals based on tribal identity allowed colonial authorities to manage populations more effectively, creating factions that could be pit against each other, thus diluting collective resistance against oppressive governance. In effect, this method of governance transformed customary law into a mechanism for social stratification, stipulating that adherence to tribal customs was a requisite for participation in a limited sphere of rights. Consequently, while the settlers projected an image of benevolence in preserving local customs, the reality was that these practices were manipulated to disenfranchise native populations further. The overlap where tribal and racial control intersected was particularly pronounced in land ownership, where legal titles often favored settlers, leaving indigenous people relegated to marginal lands or entirely dispossessed. Thus, the institutionalization of both racial and tribal regimes not only continued the legacy of colonial domination but entrenched new forms of inequality that persist long after the formal end of colonial rule.
