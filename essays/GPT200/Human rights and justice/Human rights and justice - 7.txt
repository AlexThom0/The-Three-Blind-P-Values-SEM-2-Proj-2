Every report heightened the communal desire for vengeance, validating the notion that public safety could only be guaranteed through harsh retribution. Politicians, sensing this shift, capitalized on the fear by promising stricter laws and longer sentences, framing it as a necessary sacrifice for the greater good. However, this strategy overlooked the underlying complexities of crime, dismissing the social and economic factors contributing to it. Rehabilitation took a backseat to the allure of immediate justice, creating an environment where restorative practices seemed impractical, almost naïve. Rather than addressing the conditions that foster criminal behavior, society opted for a superficial solution rooted in anger and resentment. Over time, the narrative shifted, embedding deeper cultural beliefs about punishment that remained unquestioned. Detractors found themselves marginalized, their calls for reform categorized as too radical amidst the public's embracing of punitive measures. This relentless cycle, feeding off fear and outrage, not only shaped policies but altered the very fabric of communities, fostering an environment where empathy was overshadowed by the demand for retribution.
