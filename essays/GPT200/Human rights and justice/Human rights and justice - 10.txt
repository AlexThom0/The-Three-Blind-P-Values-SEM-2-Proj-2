The precarious conditions are further exacerbated by the kafala system, a sponsorship framework that binds migrant workers to their employers, limiting their mobility and ability to seek better employment. Under this system, workers often find themselves trapped in exploitative conditions, unable to leave their jobs due to threats of deportation or financial penalties. Documented abuses include withholding of wages, inhumane living conditions, and severe restrictions on workers' freedoms, including their right to organize or voice grievances. Despite periodic reforms and international scrutiny, the reality on the ground remains dire. Many laborers arrive with the hope of improving their situations, only to face a harsh new reality that contrasts sharply with the promises made by recruitment agencies. The absence of effective legal recourse further entrenches their vulnerability. Moreover, the governments of these Gulf states often downplay the scale of these issues, opting instead for narratives that emphasize economic growth and infrastructure development. As the demand for labor continues to rise with mega-projects underway, the cycle of exploitation shows little sign of abating, perpetuating a grim status quo for those who take the brunt of this rapid urban transformation.
