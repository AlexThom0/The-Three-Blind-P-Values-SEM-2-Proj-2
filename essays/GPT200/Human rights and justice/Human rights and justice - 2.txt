The trauma inflicted upon Indigenous communities through policies of assimilation and neglect resonates deeply within the histories of countless families. Each story bears witness to the loss of culture, language, and belonging experienced by those who were subjected to these institutions. The systematic denial of their existence and the erasure of their stories perpetuated a cycle of disconnection that not only affected individual lives but also entire communities. As generations grappled with the aftermath of these experiences, the psychological scars were often compounded by the refusal to acknowledge the historical truths surrounding them. Those who survived the residential schools, often too traumatized to share their experiences, found their voices silenced by a society that chose amnesia over accountability. The ongoing struggle for reconciliation highlights the urgent need to confront these dark chapters of history, to understand the long-lasting implications of colonial violence. As this reckoning unfolds, it becomes imperative to elevate the narratives of survivors and honor the memories of those lost, as a means of fostering healing and restoring dignity to the affected communities. Only through such acts of remembrance and acknowledgment can society begin to dismantle the legacies of oppression that persist in various forms today.
