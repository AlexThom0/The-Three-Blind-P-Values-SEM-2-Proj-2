The emergence of female antislavery societies marked a pivotal moment in the interaction between gender and race within the abolition movement. As white women mobilized to oppose slavery, they often invoked their maternal instincts and moral authority, positioning themselves as protectors of the innocent, which, troublingly, did not extend to Black individuals. Internal rifts arose where white activists prioritized their own social standings over genuine equity, leading to racialized hierarchies within their ranks. Figures like Sojourner Truth emerged, challenging these divisions with powerful rhetoric that highlighted their hypocrisy. Despite these challenges, Black women’s contributions to the abolitionist cause were indispensable and often overlooked. They worked tirelessly both in tandem with and independently from white counterparts, participating in grassroots efforts that were crucial in advocating for freedom while also asserting their right to equality. Yet, the reluctance of white women to fully embrace racial egalitarianism often created barriers, curbing the potential strength of a united front against oppression. This complexity illustrated not only the struggle against slavery but also a broader societal reluctance to confront deep-rooted racial prejudices, illuminating the limitations of the prevailing abolitionist narrative and the essential, albeit complicated, alliances that formed amidst these tensions.
