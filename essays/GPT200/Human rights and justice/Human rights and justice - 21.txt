Used in various ceremonial contexts, obsidian tools and ornaments were often linked to deities and the supernatural, making this choice of material for the altar not merely functional but deeply symbolic. The sheen of polished obsidian might have invoked notions of the divine for the Aztecs, creating an effective bridge between their existing beliefs and the new Catholic practices being introduced. Each glint reflected what they recognized as sacred, thus fostering a sense of connection with the unfamiliar liturgy. The rituals performed atop this altar might have led to a reinterpretation of spirituality, facilitating a dialogue between the two worlds. This altar, while serving practical purposes, also became a catalyst for a complex syncretism, where traditions mingled, allowing the indigenous attendees to find power and meaning in a new framework. In this shared space of belief and practice, the obsidian altar functioned beyond its physical form, helping to negotiate the spiritual realities of both the priests and the congregants.
