Studies began to reveal that many perpetrators were not the stereotypical aggressive monsters but, rather, often included acquaintances or even partners who exploited existing relationships. As researchers shifted their focus, it became clear that motivations for sexual violence were varied and complex, involving a blend of psychological, sociocultural, and situational factors. Notably, understanding the role of entitlement and power dynamics started gaining traction. These factors highlighted how societal norms could foster environments where certain behaviors were minimized or justified. The shift called for a more nuanced approach to studying sexual violence, moving past outdated and overly simplistic notions of inherent aggression. Thus, contemporary research has begun to emphasize the importance of contextualizing acts of sexual violence within broader societal narratives. This evolution in understanding has implications not only for academic inquiry but also for prevention strategies, suggesting that addressing systemic issues might be key in reducing instances of sexual violence. The dialogue around these complexities continues to shape the field, revealing just how interconnected societal values are with individual behavior.
