Scientific reasoning operates on a foundation of empirical evidence, where hypotheses can be tested and retested, evolving with new discoveries. If we find microbial life on Mars, it prompts revision of our theories and beliefs based on tangible results. In contrast, miracles often hinge on anecdotal accounts or faith without the possibility of empirical validation. This stark difference raises crucial questions about the nature of evidence itself. For instance, can an unverified historical event be subjected to the same standards as a scientific proposition? The narratives surrounding miracles are frequently steeped in cultural and emotional contexts, making them resistant to objective analysis. Consequently, while one may hope for or even believe in miracles, such belief does not meet the rigorous criteria necessary for justification. Thus, the crux of my argument rests not on the impossibility of miracles but on the inadequacy of the evidence currently presented. The interplay between faith and rationality becomes more pronounced in this light, prompting us to reevaluate how we assign credence to extraordinary claims in both science and the claims of religious traditions.
