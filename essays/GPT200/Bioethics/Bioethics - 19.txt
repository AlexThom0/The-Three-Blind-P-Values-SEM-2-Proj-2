This perspective highlights a crucial juncture in the ongoing debate about conservation strategies. Rather than viewing human impact as solely detrimental, proponents of mixed-authorship ecosystems advocate for an integrated understanding of how humanity can coexist with and rehabilitate natural systems. For instance, restored landscapes that incorporate both native and non-native species demonstrate resilience and adaptability, offering a counter-narrative to the strict dichotomy between untouched wilderness and disturbed land. As such, successful conservation must embrace a broader definition of nature, one that includes anthropogenic landscapes as valuable and viable components of ecological health. This shift in thinking not only legitimizes the importance of second-growth forests but also opens avenues for innovative land management practices that prioritize both human use and ecological integrity. By recognizing the multifaceted relationships between people and the environment, policymakers can develop strategies that are not only more inclusive but also pragmatically effective in preserving biodiversity while accommodating human needs.
