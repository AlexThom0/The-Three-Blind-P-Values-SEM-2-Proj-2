This line of reasoning began to see traction in the work of John Locke, who, despite his empirical leanings, acknowledged that there was a qualitative difference in the experiences of sentient beings. Locke opened the door to a more nuanced understanding of animal life, suggesting that while animals might not possess reason in the way humans do, their capacity for sensation and emotion could not be entirely dismissed. This shift implied a slow evolution in thought, where the mechanistic view of animals started to fracture under the weight of observational evidence and philosophical inquiry. A burgeoning interest in animal welfare emerged, leading to questions about the ethical implications of how humans treat non-human creatures. As the Enlightenment progressed, a discourse began to develop around the rights of animals, challenging the long-held assertions of their mindlessness. This intellectual climate urged scholars and philosophers alike to reconsider what distinguished humans from the rest of the animal kingdom, planting the seeds for a more compassionate and empathetic stance toward all living beings. This gradual transformation in perspective set the stage for the ethical frameworks that would later redefine humanity’s relationship with animals.
