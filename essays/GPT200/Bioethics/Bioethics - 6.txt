Institutionalization often involved severe dehumanization, stripping away personal agency and reducing unique identities to mere medical diagnoses. The perceived abnormalities, whether physical or cognitive, became justifications for lifelong stigmatization. In schools designed ostensibly for care and support, individuals instead encountered environments of neglect and discrimination, shaped by a societal obsession with normalcy. This pursuit led to systematic interventions that targeted those categorized as 'different,' each marked by the heavy hand of eugenic ideologies. Glenn and Judy's narratives highlight how the legacy of these beliefs infiltrated personal lives, impacting not just immediate circumstances but also shaping broader family histories. The mental scars left by such experiences are compounded by the loss of cultural identity and the denial of rightful self-expression, which were intimately entwined with their identities as Métis and other marginalized groups. These testimonies provoke critical reflection on how society reconciles its past actions with the continued struggle for justice and recognition today. Understanding this context is vital as we strive to dismantle the ideologies that perpetuate harm.
