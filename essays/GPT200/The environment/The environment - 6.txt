The repercussions of this approach are profound, not only damaging ecosystems but also compromising public health. As livestock operations expand, they often encroach upon vital natural resources, leading to over-extraction of water from rivers and aquifers. This unsustainable practice depletes local water reserves and alters the hydrology of entire regions, creating long-term ramifications for both agriculture and biodiversity. Moreover, the heavy reliance on synthetic fertilizers and pesticides has resulted in soil degradation, diminishing its capacity to support crops. Over time, loss of soil health leads to reduced yields, putting additional strain on the food system that is already struggling to keep pace with rising global populations. The reliance on monocultures further exacerbates this problem, as diverse cropping systems are essential to resilience against pests and diseases. Transitioning to more integrated agricultural practices could alleviate some of these issues, promoting soil regeneration and inviting natural pest control mechanisms. Empowering local communities to develop agroecological methods can be a crucial step toward a more sustainable and just food system. Ultimately, realigning our agricultural priorities is critical to ensuring the health of our environments and the people who depend on them.
