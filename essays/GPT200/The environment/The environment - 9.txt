This shift not only dictated the type of crops grown but also made local economies vulnerable to shifting market demands and climatic variations. The imposition of land revenue systems further exacerbated this vulnerability, as farmers were forced to pay taxes regardless of their harvest yields. During drought years, crop failures led to widespread starvation, with the colonial government often resorting to inadequate relief measures that prioritized profit over human life. Critics of this policy noted that the focus on export-oriented agriculture undercut the traditional farming practices that had provided communities with a buffer against environmental fluctuations. As a result, the famines of the 1870s were not mere happenstance but a product of a political and economic framework that enforced dependency and stripped peasants of their autonomy. The interplay between colonial governance and environmental factors highlighted the need for a nuanced understanding of how policy decisions can translate natural phenomena into humanitarian crises. Thus, these historical famines serve as a stark reminder of the consequences of neglect in governance, revealing that disaster is often as much a matter of human choice as it is of nature's whim.
