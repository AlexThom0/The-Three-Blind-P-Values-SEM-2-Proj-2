This dissonance, Kosawa argued, shapes religious beliefs not merely as structures of obedience to an external figure but as attempts to reconcile internal conflicts. Both Kosawa and Bose perceived a more nuanced interplay between culture and psychology, suggesting that religious practices in various societies reflect complex emotional landscapes rather than mere psychological repression. For them, the responses to existential dilemmas within different cultural contexts greatly informed religious expressions. This perspective allowed for a broader understanding of how spirituality can serve as a pathway to navigate guilt, shame, and the search for individual identity. Thus, rather than viewing religion solely through a lens of psychodynamic reductionism, it became essential to engage with its multifaceted meanings as they arise within specific cultural narratives. As Kosawa and Bose continued their dialogues, they sought not only to critique Freud’s limitations but also to expand the psychoanalytic conversation to encompass the rich tapestry of human experience shaped by culture, history, and personal struggle.
