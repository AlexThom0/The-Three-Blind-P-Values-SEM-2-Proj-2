This understanding extends beyond mere physical properties, entering the realm of social constructs and identity. When individuals encounter others, they often rely on perceived essential traits—such as race, nationality, or gender—to categorize and make sense of the world around them. These categorizations can lead to the erroneous belief that individuals embody fixed characteristics, thus facilitating dehumanisation when people are reduced to mere symbols of their group identity. By applying psychological essentialism to societal interactions, we uncover the mechanisms underpinning prejudice and stereotyping. The inclination to view people as representative of their groups, rather than as unique individuals, reflects a cognitive shortcut that allows for easier processing of complex social information. However, this simplification often comes at a profound ethical cost, as it strips away the multifaceted nature of humanity. Acknowledging this tendency encourages deeper reflection on our assumptions and biases, promoting a more nuanced understanding of identity that transcends simplistic, essentialist views. Recognizing the limitations of essentialism is crucial for fostering empathy in an increasingly diverse world.
