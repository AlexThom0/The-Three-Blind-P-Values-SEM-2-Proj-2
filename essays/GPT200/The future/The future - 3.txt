The notion that extinction was merely a temporary setback was deeply entrenched in the intellectual currents of the time, stemming from a broader philosophical inclination towards optimism. The idea of Plenitude supported a vision of nature as inherently regenerative, always moving toward a form of balance that would inevitably lead to the re-emergence of lost species. This belief did not merely reflect an understanding of biological cycles; it resonated with the prevailing sentiments of human progress and destiny. The Enlightenment mindset, characterized by its faith in reason and the potential for human improvement, found a convenient ally in this view of nature. As such, many naturalists of the era, grappling with the fossil record and evidence of past lives, often interpreted their findings through a lens obscured by hopeful reconstructions rather than stark realities. This optimistic misreading was not without consequences, as it fostered a neglect of the true implications of extinction and biodiversity loss. Scholars like Lyell, with notable authority, perpetuated the idea that the Earth’s history was a series of cycles rather than a linear progression with irreversible losses. Such perspectives colored the scientific discourse and delayed a fuller understanding of extinction's finality, influencing how both scholars and the public perceived the natural world around them.
