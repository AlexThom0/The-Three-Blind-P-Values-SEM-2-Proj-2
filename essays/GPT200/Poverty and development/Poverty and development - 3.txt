Despite the significant financial commitments made by the Indian government, the operational approach to disaster relief raised serious questions about its effectiveness and intent. The stringent control over foreign aid and the exclusion of external expertise indicated not only a reluctance to engage globally but also an underlying belief in self-sufficiency that was incompatible with the scale of the disaster. This insularity not only hampered immediate relief efforts but also fostered an environment where critical assessments of local capacities were overshadowed by nationalist sentiments. Such a response pattern underscored a broader issue of governance, where political considerations took precedence over humanitarian needs. The lack of collaboration with experienced international organizations stifled opportunities for learning and adaptation, which could have enhanced the efficacy of relief measures. Instead of fostering an inclusive approach that leveraged diverse capabilities, the government's actions portrayed a misguided sense of sovereignty. The ramifications of these decisions were not merely administrative; they deeply impacted the lives of countless individuals affected by the tsunami. As a result, the need for a more integrated response strategy became ever more evident in the face of natural disasters.
