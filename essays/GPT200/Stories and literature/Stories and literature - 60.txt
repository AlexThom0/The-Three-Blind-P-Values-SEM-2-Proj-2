This merging of ownership and identity reflected not just a personal passion but a shift in how literature was perceived; books became status symbols, markers of intelligence and wealth. The act of collecting transformed from humble pursuit to ostentatious display, suggesting that knowledge was now intertwined with economic power. Scholars and aristocrats began to curate their libraries like private galleries, emphasizing not the intrinsic value of the texts but the prestige of possessing them. As notable collectors emerged, including figures such as de Bury, the value of literature shifted from the wisdom contained within to the allure of accumulation itself. This evolving relationship led to a burgeoning culture of bibliomania, where the frenzy for possession often overshadowed the joy of intellectual engagement. Consequently, the appreciation for reading as a soulful experience was at risk of being eclipsed by the sheer volume of texts one could amass. This tension between genuine literary appreciation and material obsession serves as a critical insight into the changing dynamics of readership in the late medieval period.
