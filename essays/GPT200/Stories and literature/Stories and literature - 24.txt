He believed that Beckett's discontent stemmed from a profound disconnect with the transcendental aspects of existence, which could not be reconciled through mere psychological methods. Encouraging Beckett to delve into the spiritual dimensions of life, MacGreevy proposed that understanding suffering and human isolation through contemplative practice might foster a sense of inner peace. They engaged in philosophical discussions about faith and doubt, grappling with the idea that uncertainty could be a catalyst for creativity rather than a mere affliction. This intellectual journey, steeped in both despair and hope, illuminated the paths their works would later traverse. MacGreevy's insistence on the necessity of spiritual exploration reflected a broader recognition among writers of the era, who often found themselves at the intersection of literature and spirituality. The search for meaning, even amidst existential dread, became a shared quest, binding them in their artistic pursuits and shaping the narrative trajectories they would ultimately adopt. Such exchanges would resonate deeply in Beckett's writing, where silences and pauses often speak louder than words, highlighting the profound complexity of the human experience.
