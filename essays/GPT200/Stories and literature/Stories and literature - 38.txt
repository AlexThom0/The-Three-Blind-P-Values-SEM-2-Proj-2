He examines not just the historical figure of Muhammad, but also how the narrative surrounding him shifts through cultural lenses and personal ideologies. In this light, the novel balances a celebration of faith with a critique of its dogmas. The characters, torn between reverence and skepticism, find themselves navigating a labyrinth of ethical dilemmas where certainties dissolve into uncertainties. This oscillation between belief and doubt becomes a central theme, framing their interactions and conflicts. By portraying these inner struggles, Rushdie underscores the complex relationship individuals have with established religious narratives, particularly in the context of a rapidly changing world. Through the lens of modernity, he raises critical questions about the relevance of ancient texts and the often arbitrary boundaries set by traditional interpretations. The richness of the Islamic tradition thus serves as both a backdrop for personal exploration and a catalyst for larger societal discussions. In addressing the intricate tapestry of faith, Rushdie not only challenges entrenched perspectives but also invites readers to reconsider their own beliefs in the light of historical and contemporary realities.
