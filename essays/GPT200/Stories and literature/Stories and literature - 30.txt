The public’s awe at the telegraph’s instantaneous communication was not unlike the wonderment experienced at earlier spectacles of illusion. It suggested, for some, an almost divine quality to mastery over such inventions. Indeed, to skeptics, these developments risked becoming an arena for trickery where inventors might manipulate perception akin to sorcerers. John Henry Newman, reflecting on the implications of such technologies, questioned whether these marvels reshaped not only the everyday experience but also the very notion of human agency. What could it mean for a society that embraced such power, if in doing so it surrendered its grip on the authentic? The reverence for inventors was tempered by an underlying anxiety about their potential to mislead, thus creating a complex interplay of admiration and critique. As people grappled with the notion of 'magic' in this context, the discourse around technology shifted, intertwining the promise of progress with the persistent specter of deceit and manipulation that had long haunted humanity’s quest for knowledge.
