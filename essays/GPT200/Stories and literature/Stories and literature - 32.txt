Woolf’s rebellion against the bounds of traditional narrative style was not merely an artistic choice; it embodied a broader cultural shift toward individual interpretation and emotional realism. As Bennett meticulously mapped out the social landscapes of his characters, Woolf began to venture into the psychological depths of consciousness, favoring stream of thought over linear progression. This divergence created a framework where writers could explore new themes of identity, gender, and existentialism, challenging the established norms that dictated literary merit. In essence, Woolf's work not only disrupted the boundaries imposed by her predecessors but also scrutinized the societal conventions that underpinned their storytelling. This complex interplay of admiration, irritation, and innovation set the stage for a vigorous dialogue among contemporaries, allowing for a rich exchange of ideas that shaped the modernist movement. The dynamic between Bennett's realism and Woolf's avant-garde techniques laid bare the necessity for writers to experiment with form and content, pushing the limits of what literature could convey in an ever-evolving cultural landscape.
