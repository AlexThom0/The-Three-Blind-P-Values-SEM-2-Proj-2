By elevating these texts to a status of moral and nationalistic archetypes, the curriculum implicitly suggested that the values embedded within medieval narratives resonated with contemporary notions of English identity. This valorization of mythic pasts not only shaped students' understanding of literature but also encouraged a romanticized view of history that glossed over the complexities of race, class, and colonialism. The allure of heroism and moral clarity found in these works often overshadowed the equally important critical perspectives offered by diverse voices engaged in literary analysis. As the curriculum largely neglected these viewpoints, it perpetuated an echo chamber, where only certain heroic narratives were celebrated while more challenging and nuanced interpretations of medieval texts were sidelined or ignored. Such selective nostalgia ultimately shaped not only literary scholarship but also broader societal views within the context of a post-imperial England yearning for a unifying cultural mythology.
