The translations produced in this colonial context highlight the paradox of world literature. While these local adaptations allowed for the dissemination of diverse narratives, they also reinforced the power dynamics inherent in colonial relationships. The process was not merely about transferring texts; it involved reshaping and reframing them to fit the expectations of a European audience. This interaction led to a complex interplay between domination and cultural exchange. The voices of colonized authors were often filtered through the lenses of Western interpretation, which risked diluting their original messages and reinforcing stereotypes. Moreover, the very act of translation became a site of conflict, wherein the struggle for authenticity and representation unfolded alongside economic exploitation. In this way, literature transformed into a commodity, its value tied to its ability to attract a global readership while simultaneously erasing the very cultural contexts from which these works emerged. The implications of such dynamics demand scrutiny as they challenge the notion of an unproblematic celebration of diversity within world literature. This raises essential questions about who gets to tell their stories and under what circumstances, unraveling the intricate threads linking literature, power, and identity.
