As men returned from the front lines, many couples rushed to start families, often leading to a convergence of age and life experience that increased the likelihood of multiple births. Additionally, with changing economic circumstances, families began to embrace a more modern ethos, which sometimes included the acceptance of assisted reproductive measures, albeit limited at that time. This combination of postponed parenthood and heightened familial urgency not only contributed to the twin phenomenon but also reflected a broader reconfiguration of reproductive patterns. Interestingly, these developments were mirrored in various European nations, albeit with local variations influenced by cultural expectations and government policies. The post-war years, thus, can be seen as a crucible of demographic change, where traditional norms were increasingly challenged and reshaped, laying the groundwork for the subsequent evolution of family structures. Pison argues that these factors not only illuminate the dynamics of twin births but also offer crucial insights into the transformative shifts in societal values surrounding family life during this tumultuous period.
