The emphasis on safety and emotional well-being intensified, often at the expense of promoting independence and resilience-building experiences. Parents started to worry that even mild failures or setbacks could be detrimental to their children's development, fostering a culture of overprotection. This transition reflected broader societal anxieties, where economic uncertainties and competitive pressures fueled a desire to safeguard children from any form of hardship. Schools, adapting to these changing parental concerns, implemented policies that prioritized emotional safety over traditional academic rigor. Consequently, children faced fewer opportunities to engage in challenging experiences that previous generations regarded as essential for growth. The increasing insistence on emotional validation, while well-intentioned, risked creating an environment where failure became something to avoid at all costs rather than an inevitable part of learning. As adults grappled with their own fears of producing fragile offspring, the narrative around childhood shifted fundamentally, positioning resilience and vulnerability as competing attributes rather than complementary facets of human development.
