In the years leading up to this pivotal shift, global fish consumption continued to rise, fueled by both increasing populations and dietary preferences shifting toward seafood. This demand exacerbated the strain on overexploited species, prompting nations to expand into previously unregulated waters. The competitive nature of fishing, combined with economic motivations, often led to unsustainable practices, reducing fish populations even further and generating tensions among nations. As coastal communities faced dwindling catches, the social and economic consequences became apparent. Fishermen turned to new techniques and methods, sometimes resorting to illegal fishing practices in desperation. Understandably, these very actions contributed to the destabilization of marine ecosystems, highlighting the urgent need for governance frameworks to regulate fishing practices effectively. Such frameworks are essential to balance ecological preservation with the livelihoods that depend on these resources. In response, international treaties began to emerge, guiding collaborative approaches to shared fish stocks. However, enforcement of these agreements has frequently been inconsistent, often leaving vulnerable regions at risk. The ensuing crises underscored the need for innovative solutions that would support both conservation and sustainable harvesting practices, further setting the stage for aquaculture's rise.
