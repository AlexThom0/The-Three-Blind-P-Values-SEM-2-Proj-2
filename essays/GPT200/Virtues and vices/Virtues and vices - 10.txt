The Loving Saint’s apparent happiness might suggest a kind of ignorance is bliss, while the Rational Saint’s commitment, grounded in ethical deliberation, may lead to a more profound sense of loss. This dichotomy raises intriguing implications for understanding the nature of fulfillment in ethical living. For the Loving Saint, the absence of non-moral goods doesn’t equate to deprivation; instead, they seem to derive meaning and satisfaction solely from their moral endeavors. In contrast, the Rational Saint's acknowledgment of sacrificed pleasures reflects an acute awareness of life's complexities, leading to an existence characterized by a stark sense of duty. However, this commitment to moral rectitude, while admirable, might come at the expense of a richer, more diverse human experience. Wolf’s exploration of happiness, therefore, invites us to consider whether true well-being can be disentangled from a vibrant engagement with non-moral pleasures. Engaging with art, relationships, or personal interests does not merely enrich life but might also enhance one's ethical commitments. Balancing moral pursuits with the appreciation of life’s myriad offerings could yield a more comprehensive understanding of human fulfillment.
