While it can be tempting to cling to retributivist concepts of justice because they resonate with our intuitive sense of moral order, we must consider the implications of a deterministic or luck-based worldview on our understanding of accountability. If individuals are not the ultimate architects of their actions, then attributing moral blame seems misguided. This raises critical questions about the societal functions of punishment. Even if one maintains a belief in desert, the practical application of this concept may need to adapt to recognize the broader context of behavior—acknowledging influences such as socioeconomic status, mental health, and environmental factors. In this light, punishment should be seen more as a tool for creating societal safety and promoting rehabilitation rather than as a means of delivering retribution. Furthermore, the conversation shifts to whether our legal systems can gracefully balance the scales of justice without relying on antiquated notions of moral culpability that may not withstand scrutiny under modern philosophical debates. The focus thereby shifts towards understanding justice in a more progressive framework, emphasizing restoration and community healing over mere retributive satisfaction. Our challenge lies in redefining accountability and ensuring justice serves its intended purposes.
