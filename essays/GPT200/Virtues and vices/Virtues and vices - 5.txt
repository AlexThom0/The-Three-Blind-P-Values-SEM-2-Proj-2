This phenomenon reinforces the idea that genuine virtues are not merely about the actions one takes but also about the sacrifices made to exhibit them. For instance, a person who consistently volunteers their time to help others, foregoing leisure activities and personal gain, showcases a commitment that is difficult to fake. This creates a social contract of sorts—where actions backed by genuine intention foster deeper connections and loyalties. In contrast, superficial displays of virtue, such as posting about charitable work on social media without substantive involvement, often invite scrutiny and skepticism. Consequently, society develops mechanisms for evaluating the authenticity of these signals. Individuals learn to ask questions and observe behaviors over time, reinforcing the need for consistent, long-term patterns of altruism. This complex interplay discourages insincerity, as the perceived costs of maintaining a facade bear the risk of eventual exposure. Therefore, the principles of costly signalling not only illuminate the motives behind altruistic behavior, but also reflect a broader social framework that prizes authenticity over empty gestures of goodwill.
