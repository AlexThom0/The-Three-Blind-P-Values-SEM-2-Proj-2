Intentionalists viewed the actions and policies of the Nazi regime as the result of deliberate decision-making by a central figure, thus embodying a narrative of agency and responsibility. This perspective lent itself to a clearer moral judgement of the era, casting Hitler not only as a pivotal actor but as the singular embodiment of evil. On the other hand, structuralists posited that myriad social, economic, and political forces shaped the regime's trajectory, advocating for a more nuanced interpretation that examined the systemic failures and societal complicity that facilitated atrocities. This distinction was not merely academic; it had real implications for understanding collective guilt and the responsibilities of both individuals and institutions during this dark chapter in history. As historians grappled with these conflicting views, their analyses sought to uncover the intricate webs of influence that defined the regime, recognizing that the truth of the past often lies in the interplay of intention and structure, revealing a more comprehensive and disturbing picture of human behavior in the face of unimaginable moral crises.
