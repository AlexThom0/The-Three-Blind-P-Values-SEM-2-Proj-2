By lauding a system that ostensibly places authority in the hands of the populace, Pericles glosses over the exclusions and inequalities that were inherent to that democracy. The Athenian model celebrated in his oration was not universally inclusive; it systematically marginalized women, slaves, and non-citizens, revealing a stark contradiction between the lofty ideals of equality and the everyday practices of the polis. This selective memory serves a dual purpose: it fuels patriotic fervor while allowing political leaders to invoke Athenian democracy as a mythical benchmark against which modern practices can be compared. In contemporary discourse, this invocation is often stripped of its historical context, leading to a simplified narrative that overlooks the lessons from Athenian experiences. As modern citizens grapple with the implications of democratic engagement, such misinterpretations of foundational texts must be critically examined, yielding insights into the interplay between ideals and realities in both ancient and modern governance. The tension between these two worlds continues to shape our understanding of democracy today.
