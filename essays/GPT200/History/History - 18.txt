Hobsbawm's reliance on Marxist frameworks led him to analyze class struggles and economic systems, framing historical events within the wider context of social forces. In contrast, Koselleck's theoretical lens focused on the structures of temporal experience and the varied meanings of historical time, often engaging with concepts such as "space of experience" and "horizon of expectation." This distinction underscores how both scholars positioned themselves within an evolving dialogue on the interpretation of history, particularly in response to the tumultuous events of the 20th century. While Hobsbawm's narratives frequently reflect an opposition to prevailing power structures, Koselleck's analyses reveal an emphasis on the philosophical underpinnings of political thought, fostering a more nuanced and sometimes controversial engagement with figures like Schmitt. Their divergent paths not only illuminate the complexities of historiography but also emphasize the ongoing debate regarding the role of ideology in shaping historical narratives. This dynamic interplay between personal conviction and scholarly pursuit continues to resonate in contemporary discussions on history's relevance today.
