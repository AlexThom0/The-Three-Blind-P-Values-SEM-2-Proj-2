In examining male honour through time, it becomes evident that the societal context heavily influenced how masculinity was expressed and defended. For instance, in earlier centuries, there existed a collective understanding among men across classes that physical confrontation served not only as personal retribution but also as a communal safeguard of reputation. This communal aspect fostered solidarity among men, binding them together in their shared adherence to a code of honour that permitted, even encouraged, violence under specific circumstances. However, as societal structures evolved, the scope and implications of such violence began to shift. The waning influence of feudal systems and the rise of more structured legal frameworks introduced new expectations regarding masculinity that did not always rely on physical strength or aggression. The individualization of honour began to emerge, where personal reputation could be damaged not merely through physical assault but through social and economic standing, thereby challenging traditional notions of masculinity. This transformation illuminated class distinctions in how honour was perceived and defended, ultimately leading to a divergence in the expression of male honour across different social strata.
