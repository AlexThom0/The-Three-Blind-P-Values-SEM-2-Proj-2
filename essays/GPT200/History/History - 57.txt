Figures such as Samuel Johnson and Edmund Burke argued passionately against the unchecked consumption of novels, asserting that literature must adhere to moral standards to serve society positively. They believed that the allure of sensationalism and emotional extremes in literature detracted from rational thought and civic virtue. This perspective was bolstered by anecdotal evidence of readers experiencing profound emotional upheavals, which critics interpreted as signs of a collective psychological malaise. Moreover, the rise of the novel coincided with greater access to education for women, leading to fears about the societal implications of an independently educated female populace. As these novels often depicted romantic entanglements and moral dilemmas, critics worried that such narratives could subvert traditional values and distort young women's understanding of relationships, further complicating their roles within society. The ensuing backlash underscored a cultural struggle that pitted the liberating potential of literature against its capacity for emotional destabilization, reflecting broader anxieties surrounding burgeoning social changes and the shifting status of women in public life. Such tensions would continue to influence literary criticism and the evolution of the novel.
