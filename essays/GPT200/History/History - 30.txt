The burgeoning economy of the colonies relied heavily on the exploitation of enslaved Africans, intertwining economic interests with legal frameworks that legitimized and perpetuated slavery. Wealth generated from these practices established a new social order, as plantation owners became some of the most powerful figures in colonial society. This economic dependency enshrined the institution of slavery in the legal codes and practices of the time, creating a system where the rights of individuals could be curtailed based on race and status. Documents of the era reflected this growing normalization, anchoring racial hierarchies within colonial law. Such constructs served not merely as economic machinery but also as justifications for the inhumane treatment of countless individuals. The court decisions that categorized enslaved people as property reinforced a culture where ideas of freedom and rights were selectively applied. While proponents of liberty espoused ideals of freedom and civil rights, their practices pointed to a stark contradiction—one where the very foundations of emerging liberal thought were propped up by systems of oppression. This juxtaposition laid a troubling groundwork for future philosophical debates, anchoring capitalism and liberalism deeply within the context of slavery.
