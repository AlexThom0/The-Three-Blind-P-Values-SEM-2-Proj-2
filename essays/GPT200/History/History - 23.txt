For example, a mixture of resin, honey, and various herbs might be administered to cleanse the system, based on the belief that herbs carried potent qualities that could restore balance to the humours. The connection between diet and health amplified these anxieties; what one consumed was perceived as a direct influence on the likelihood of parasitic infestations. Therefore, physicians would often prescribe strict dietary regimens to mitigate risks, emphasizing the importance of good digestion as a defense against these ailments. Social factors also played a critical role, as class distinctions affected access to food and, consequently, health. Wealthier households were more likely to afford fresh, cooked meals, while poorer families often resorted to preserved or spoiled provisions. This discrepancy not only heightened the susceptibility to pests but also underscored the intricate link between socio-economic status and health perceptions in medieval society. Understanding these beliefs offers insight into the complex ways that medical knowledge and cultural practices intersected during this period.
