The societal pressures surrounding breastfeeding were deeply intertwined with notions of morality and duty, underscoring a wider cultural belief that a mother’s value was largely determined by her adherence to these norms. The practice of breastfeeding was often framed as an act of virtue, with women’s dedication to nursing seen as a reflection of their character and maternal instincts. Medical texts frequently reinforced these ideals, depicting breastfeeding not merely as a biological necessity but as a sacred obligation. For many women, the intense scrutiny they faced created an environment ripe for guilt and anxiety. This pervasive stigma around formula feeding and the demonization of women who chose or were unable to breastfeed fostered a narrative that defined motherhood in strict, often unrealistic terms. In this context, the relationship between mother and child was also framed by these external expectations—a bond reinforced through the act of nursing, yet overshadowed by societal judgement. Furthermore, the consequences of failing to conform to these standards extended beyond familial dynamics, as the broader community often weighed in on individual choices, perpetuating cycles of shame that could be felt for generations. Such attitudes not only shaped maternal identity but also influenced the healthcare practices of the time.
