For his audience, dreams often served more as reflections of personal anxieties or desires than as prophetic messages. This trend indicated a cultural divergence in the way dreams were contextualized; instead of weaving them into a framework of spiritual or communal significance, many Britons approached dreams with skepticism, viewing them as mere byproducts of the psyche. Seligman noted that while dreams could be analyzed for hidden meanings, the overarching response of his British subjects revealed a reluctance to ascribe deeper, universal truths to their nighttime visions. This difference in interpretation not only highlighted the diverse influences of colonialism and cultural exchanges but also sparked Seligman’s interest in how societal contexts shape dream narratives. He began to see these variations as indicative of broader psychological landscapes shaped by history, environment, and collective memory. Thus, even in a seemingly shared human experience, the lens through which dreams are perceived can differ significantly based on cultural background and societal expectations. Such observations inevitably enriched Seligman’s exploration of the human mind, prompting further inquiry into the implications of these divergent interpretations.
