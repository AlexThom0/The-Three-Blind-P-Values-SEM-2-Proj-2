Salisbury envisioned a society where individuals, regardless of their geographical location, would harness electricity to create, innovate, and compete on a new playing field. With tools powered by electricity, domestic spaces could be transformed into vibrant hubs of production. This shift would erode the dominance of traditional urban centers that relied heavily on steam power and concentrated labor. The implications for social structures were profound; smaller, self-sufficient communities could emerge, reducing reliance on large factories and prompting a reevaluation of class dynamics. By envisioning a future where each household could encapsulate industry, Salisbury tapped into aspirations of increased autonomy and empowerment for the everyday citizen. The electrical revolution, he posited, would not merely be a technological upgrade but a catalyst for broader social change. In this transformative landscape, the interconnectedness fostered by electrical advancements would redefine personal and professional relationships, urging society to reconsider what was possible for individuals in the rapidly evolving modern world.
