Neurologists began to recognize that rehabilitation required a deeper understanding of individual experiences and adaptive strategies. This meant shifting away from a purely physiological approach toward one that embraced the psychological and contextual dimensions of healing. Each patient’s unique history and personality inherently influenced their response to treatment. Consequently, approaches such as group therapy and experimental tasks were introduced to facilitate social interaction and cognitive engagement. Psychologists and therapists collaborated closely with neurologists, exploring the ways in which emotional support and social structures contributed to cognitive recovery. As these practitioners worked together, they began to acknowledge the fluidity of identity in the aftermath of trauma. Thus, rehabilitative success hinged not solely on restoring lost functions but on reconstructing a sense of agency and purpose, allowing these men to envision futures that transcended their injuries. The very act of imagining possibilities became a crucial component of recovery, intertwining the processes of healing both mind and body.
