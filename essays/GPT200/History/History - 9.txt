In a society that valued decorum, such expressive displays were eschewed in favor of more restrained forms of communication. Portraits from this era frequently emphasized demeanor and poise, reflecting the ideals of nobility and refinement. Instead of conveying raw emotion, the closed smile suggested confidence and control, qualities highly regarded in societal hierarchies. The focus on the subtlety of expression contributed to a distinct cultural identity; it was a way to assert one's position within the rigid frameworks of class and decorum. Moreover, the rarity of toothy smiles in the art led to a visual vocabulary that spoke volumes about societal norms, suggesting that to smile broadly was to question one's adherence to established decorum. This complexity in the portrayal of smiles serves as a fascinating lens through which to understand the values and anxieties of the time, underscoring the power of visual representation in shaping cultural perception.
