The intertwining of sexuality and health was not merely physical; it reflected societal attitudes toward desire, chastity, and the responsibilities of nobility. The stigma surrounding sexual activity was amplified by the belief that indulgence led to moral decay, impacting not only individuals but also families and lineages. Noblemen were expected to exercise control and prudence in their personal lives, especially when their actions could endanger their health or their progeny. Failure to adhere to these standards often resulted in public scandal and a tarnished reputation. Furthermore, the consequences of sexual indiscretions extended beyond death; they influenced political alliances, inheritance disputes, and the broader social fabric. In this context, physicians became arbiters of morality as much as they were healers, offering advice that reflected societal norms as well as medical knowledge. Within this interplay of health, sexuality, and reputation, we observe a complex narrative about gender, power, and the human body—one that reveals how deeply interconnected these aspects were in the medieval understanding of life and death.
