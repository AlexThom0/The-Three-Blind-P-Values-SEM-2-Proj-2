This system prioritized empiricism while discounting philosophical inquiry that did not yield measurable outcomes. Under this model, the traditional humanities faced significant challenges, often being sidelined or dismissed as subjective and irrelevant to the pursuit of objective truth. Faculty members were encouraged to adopt a mechanistic view of knowledge, leading to a culture where inquiry was reduced to mere data gathering and hypothesis testing. Projects that did not fit neatly into the parameters of Allen's formula were often seen as unworthy, contributing to a homogenization of thought across disciplines. The rejection of alternative epistemologies had far-reaching implications, undermining the complexity of human experience and the value of critical discourse. In this new academic landscape, the nuances of ethics, aesthetics, and the human condition risked being overlooked in favor of a simplified model of knowledge that promised clarity but ultimately constrained intellectual exploration. Thus, the impact of the Allen Formula extended beyond a mere evaluative tool, shaping the very foundations of academic culture and defining the parameters of acceptable scholarship.
