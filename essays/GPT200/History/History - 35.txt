This perspective suggested that the pleasures of eating, traditionally tied to cultural and social practices, were secondary to the biochemical processes at play. With the rise of nutrition science, food was stripped of its sensory attributes and transformed into something transactional, where the primary concern was the number of kilocalories consumed rather than the experience of a meal. People began to see food as mere components of a balanced diet. This shift not only impacted individual eating habits but also transformed societal values surrounding food. Meals became less about communal enjoyment and more about personal discipline and adherence to nutritional guidelines. The emphasis on a scientifically driven approach to diet laid the groundwork for contemporary food policies that often overlook cultural contexts and culinary traditions, further marginalizing the sensory dimensions that have historically shaped human interactions with food. Ultimately, this reductive view relegated flavour to an overlooked aspect of our dietary choices, shaping both what people eat and how they perceive food's role in their lives.
