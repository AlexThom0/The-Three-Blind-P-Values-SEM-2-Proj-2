As perceptions of drug use shifted, so too did the cultural framework within which these substances were consumed and understood. The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw a rebellion against the strictures of Victorian morality; cocaine became emblematic of a broader cultural liberation, where the lines between therapeutic and recreational use blurred. This era was marked by a fascination with experimentation and hedonism, as coca leaves were featured in tonics, elixirs, and even early soft drinks. Such products were marketed not merely for their physiological effects, but as gateways to a modern, liberated self, reflecting the zeitgeist of a society grappling with rapid industrialization and urbanization. The rise of jazz culture and the bohemian lifestyle further contributed to this shifting narrative, framing cocaine not just as a substance, but as a symbol of creative rebellion and new social identities. This multifaceted relationship with cocaine invites a deeper exploration of how drugs are contextualized within societal movements, revealing much about the underlying tensions and aspirations of the culture from which they emerge.
