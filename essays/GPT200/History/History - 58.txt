The army's intervention in civilian life had distorted the very fabric of society, intertwining militarism with governance and eroding the possibility of genuine civic engagement. This disconnection bred a deep-seated resentment, as the state apparatus became synonymous with oppression rather than protection. Citizens found themselves trapped in a cycle where every call for reform was met with brutal suppression, further alienating them from the institutions meant to represent their interests. The revolution, thus, was not merely a spontaneous upheaval; it was a culmination of decades of frustration against an oppressive regime that overlooked the basic rights and aspirations of the people. In this context, the revolt represented an awakening, a collective assertion of agency that demanded the dismantling of a corrupt system. The cries for freedom and justice echoed through the streets, revealing an urgent need for transformation, a thirst for a new political landscape that recognized the dignity and voice of every Egyptian citizen.
