It reflected a growing appreciation for nature in its more untamed state, emphasizing harmony between cultivated spaces and the natural environment. As the Industrial Revolution loomed on the horizon, this shift mirrored broader societal changes, with the upper classes seeking refuge from urbanization in these idyllic pastoral retreats. Gardens became spaces for reflection and leisure, encouraging a form of escapism that distanced their owners from the fast-paced life of cities. While the manicured precision of formal gardens symbolized human dominion over nature, the pastoral landscapes designed by Brown highlighted a more intimate relationship with the land. This evolving relationship revealed underlying tensions in social class and environmental stewardship, as new ideals celebrated the beauty of the wild while acknowledging humanity’s role in shaping it. The lush expanses and scenic views crafted by Brown invited visitors to explore and appreciate the subtleties of the English countryside, paving the way for future generations to rethink their interactions with both landscape and nature.
