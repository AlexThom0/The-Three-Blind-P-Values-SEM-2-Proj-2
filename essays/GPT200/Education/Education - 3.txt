In this context, the search for solutions led to a revival of interest in traditional values, particularly those rooted in Shinto and Buddhism. Scholars and policymakers posited that nurturing a sense of spiritual belonging could mitigate the rising tide of delinquency. The narrative surrounding hitozukuri, or 'human formation', emphasized the importance of moral education and character development in shaping a responsible citizenry. This idea resonated particularly strongly in conversations about youth; many believed that instilling ethical frameworks derived from Japan's longstanding cultural heritage could reclaim the youth from the clutches of social disarray. Notably, grassroots movements emerged, promoting local community engagement and initiatives designed to uplift moral standards among young people. These programs often incorporated traditional rites and festivals, seeking to reconnect youth with their cultural identity. As the government sought to counterbalance the perceived threats of modernization and westernization, the push for a religiously informed ethos became a focal point of national discourse. The vision was clear: by reinforcing these age-old principles, society could not only combat delinquency but also foster a more cohesive and resilient community, anchored in shared values that transcended the turbulence of the modern age.
