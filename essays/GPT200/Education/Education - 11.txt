In this context, first-generation college students often face compounding barriers that complicate their integration into elite educational environments. Many struggle with not only the academic challenges of a rigorous curriculum but also the psychological pressures of social alienation. They may experience imposter syndrome, questioning their right to belong in spaces predominantly populated by affluent peers. This disparity extends beyond mere financial challenges; it infiltrates social interactions, mentorship opportunities, and access to resources that can facilitate academic success. For example, continuing-generation students often benefit from familial networks that provide insights into college culture, while first-generation students might lack this crucial support. Institutions frequently underestimate the significance of these networks, failing to recognize the value of social capital that plays a critical role in student persistence and achievement. Additionally, the experiences of first-generation students are influenced by their unique backgrounds, which can affect their comfort with seeking help from faculty or peers. These factors contribute to a cycle where first-generation students are less likely to thrive, further widening the achievement gap. Effective intervention strategies need to address these cultural differences and support mechanisms if institutions are to create a more equitable academic landscape.
