The questionable value of these degrees reflects a larger trend in American higher education during this era: a democratization of learning that prioritized accessibility over academic rigor. Many institutions were established not to cultivate a rigorous intellectual atmosphere but to serve local populations seeking credentials alongside basic education. This proliferation resulted in varying educational standards, where the distinction between genuine learning and mere degree acquisition blurred. With little oversight or standardized curriculum, many colleges struggled to maintain coherent academic missions, often veering into vocational training or religious instruction that aligned more with community expectations than scholarly pursuits. The result was a patchwork of institutions, some striving for scholarly integrity while others merely capitalized on the growing societal demand for educated individuals, regardless of quality. As a result, even among those who did pursue higher education, the experience could feel unfulfilling, with graduates emerging from these institutions lacking the critical thinking and analytical skills necessary for professional success in an increasingly complex economy. This divergence in educational purpose contributed to a landscape where the value of college degrees was continuously scrutinized, leading to inevitable calls for reform as the higher education system evolved to meet new societal needs.
