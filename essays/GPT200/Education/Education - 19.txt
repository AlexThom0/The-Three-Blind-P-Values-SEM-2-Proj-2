They challenge existing narratives and prompt deeper inquiries into the status quo of global aid distribution and policy-making. For many organizations, the findings of such research could force a reevaluation of priorities, potentially placing power in the hands of those most affected by poverty. If the data suggests a need for accountability or a shift towards addressing governance and employment, it could upend established practices that favor comfortable solutions. Furthermore, reliance on conventional metrics often sidelines the lived experiences of individuals, rendering their nuanced perspectives invisible. This disconnect creates a gap between what's proposed in economic models and the realities faced by communities on the ground. As a result, agency is stripped from the very groups these policies aim to support, leaving them at the mercy of abstract decisions made in distant offices. Bridging this gap requires not only a commitment to listening but also a willingness to adapt institutional frameworks to integrate these critical insights, fostering an inclusive dialogue that places the priorities of the marginalized front and center. How can we build systems that incentivize such engagement?
