In these urban narratives, the role of nonhuman entities is often minimized or even completely overlooked. Animals become mere background figures that highlight human innovation rather than active participants in the urban ecosystem. This portrayal risks reinforcing the anthropocentric view that perceives cities primarily as human constructs, designed to serve human needs and ambitions. Yet, an array of studies in urban ecology suggests that nonhuman species play critical roles in shaping their environments, often adapting in surprising ways to urban settings. For instance, pigeons and raccoons have developed unique behaviors that allow them to thrive in human-dominated spaces, demonstrating resilience and agency. Similarly, green infrastructures, such as urban forests and community gardens, reveal a dynamic interplay between human actions and nonhuman life. By fostering biodiversity, these initiatives challenge the misconception that cities are devoid of nature. Recognizing such interactions disrupts the traditional narrative, suggesting that urban environments can be reimagined as cohabited spaces where humans and nonhumans influence each other's existence. Thus, the narrative should embrace a more inclusive understanding of the urban landscape, one that acknowledges the complex tapestry of interactions across species.
