The initial influx of wealth had emboldened the Spanish authorities, who became increasingly indifferent to the suffering of the indigenous labor force. Brutal working conditions in the mines led to a significant loss of life, further hollowing out the local populace. Meanwhile, as the demand for silver grew in Europe and Asia, the relentless extraction took a toll on the environment, rendering the once-thriving landscapes near the mines unrecognizable. This mounting strain created a paradox; the more prosperous Potosí appeared on the surface, the more desperate its conditions grew beneath. The riches that had drawn global attention became symbols of human suffering, reflecting a reality that was increasingly at odds with the city’s celebrated image. As Potosí evolved into a focal point of economic ambition and greed, its artisans’ depictions of splendor began to clash starkly with the lived experiences of its inhabitants, who faced the dual burden of economic exploitation and cultural erasure.
