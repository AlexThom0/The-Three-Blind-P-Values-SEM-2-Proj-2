This right emphasizes not only the entitlement to inhabit and use spaces within the city but also the capacity to influence the processes that shape these environments. The interplay between these rights and the democratic ideals underpinning them reveals a tension between individual freedoms and collective responsibilities. Advocates argue that reclaiming this right necessitates an active participation in policymaking processes and urban governance, highlighting the interdependence of social movements and democratic institutions. It calls for a deeper engagement with the local contexts in which these rights manifest, suggesting that urban spaces should serve as arenas for both expression and collaboration. This vision aligns with emergent practices aimed at fostering inclusive public discourse and decision-making, empowering diverse voices often marginalized in current frameworks. Rather than viewing the city merely as a backdrop for personal rights, recognizing the right to the city urges a broader perspective on identity formation and community resilience, striving for an urban future that reflects collective aspirations and shared values.
