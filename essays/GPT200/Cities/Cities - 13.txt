This shift in perspective allowed scholars to analyze how the increasingly rapid pace of city life contributed to a detachment from emotional connections and a prioritization of rationality over sentiment. The urban environment, with its constant barrage of stimuli, fostered a sense of alienation among its inhabitants, who became disillusioned and indifferent. Individuals were transformed into spectators of their own lives, compelled to navigate through a landscape filled with fleeting interactions and superficial engagements. This phenomenon was not merely a byproduct of the city; rather, it emerged as a direct response to a societal framework that valued productivity and conformity over genuine interpersonal relationships. The rise of new social structures, along with industrialization, further exacerbated this detachment, encouraging individuals to forgo deeper emotional investments in favor of pragmatic survival in a competitive milieu. Understanding these dynamics is essential in grasping the broader implications of urbanization on identity formation and social cohesion, revealing the intricate tapestry of human experience shaped by the environment in which individuals live.
