In cities, the presence of modernity reshapes how death is perceived and handled, often stripping rituals of their communal essence. Funerals became more commercialized, driven by a blend of cultural expectations and the influence of consumerism. While rural funerals typically embrace collective mourning, urban counterparts often prioritize individual expression, marked by personalized ceremonies that reflect the deceased's life rather than shared heritage. This shift highlights a deeper societal transformation; as people migrate to urban areas, there is a gradual erosion of communal ties and collective memory, altering not only how funerals are conducted but also how grief is experienced. Interviews revealed that many city dwellers grapple with the passing of loved ones in isolation, a stark difference from the rural practice of communal support. In this context, the act of mourning evolves, revealing new challenges and adaptations, as individuals seek meaningful connections amidst the anonymity of urban life. Understanding this juxtaposition became central to my research, prompting a closer examination of how cultural practices adapt to contemporary realities.
