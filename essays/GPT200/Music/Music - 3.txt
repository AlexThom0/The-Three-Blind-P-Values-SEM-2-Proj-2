The prejudice against female conductors persisted well into the 20th century, with many questioning their capability to lead large orchestras despite their educational qualifications. Societal norms dictated that conducting was an inherently masculine pursuit, reinforcing stereotypes that women lacked the authoritative presence needed to command an ensemble. This cultural bias meant that even talented women often found themselves relegated to second-class roles, such as being assistants or performers, rather than taking charge of their own orchestras. The victories of women like Boulanger highlight not only individual achievement but also a gradual shift in the landscape of orchestral direction. However, these shifts were often met with skepticism, and women navigated a complex interplay of talent, ambition, and the societal hurdles inherent in pursuing leadership in classical music. To effect change, female conductors had to assert their presence continuously, challenging conventional expectations and redefining what it meant to lead an orchestra. Their journeys were marked by perseverance, advocating not just for their rightful place on the podium but also for a broader acceptance of women in classical music roles.
