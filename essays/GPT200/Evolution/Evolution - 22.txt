A narrow focus on a specific population can lead to skewed interpretations of broader human behavior, which is especially perilous in fields like anthropology and psychology. Chagnon’s work exemplifies this risk, as he framed the Yanomami's violent customs as indicative of inherent human traits, ignoring the vast array of alternative social structures. This tendency to generalize has been echoed in psychological research that relies disproportionately on WEIRD populations, ultimately resulting in conclusions that may not hold true across different cultures. For instance, while some societies engage in organized conflict, others have developed intricate systems of peace and cooperation, demonstrating that both aggression and altruism are products of cultural context. Moreover, societal structures in diverse groups challenge the idea that any singular trait, such as aggression, is universally applicable to all humans. Such oversimplifications not only misrepresent the multifaceted nature of human behavior but also contribute to misconceptions that can influence policy and educational practices. By broadening the lens through which we study human societies, researchers can better capture the richness of cultural variations and the complexities of human nature itself. The need for a more inclusive approach is critical in advancing our understanding of what it truly means to be human.
