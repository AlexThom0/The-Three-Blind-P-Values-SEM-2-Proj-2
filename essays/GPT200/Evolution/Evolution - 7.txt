As these worms break down organic material faster than native species can handle, they create an imbalance that favors certain plant types over others, often promoting the spread of invasive species while diminishing the diversity of native flora. The changes in nutrient cycling and soil chemistry affect the entire ecosystem, making it hard for local species to compete and thrive. The absence of natural checks and balances allows European earthworms to establish themselves extensively, creating a cascade of ecological consequences. Consequently, the persistence of these non-native earthworms exacerbates challenges for conservationists seeking to restore and preserve North American forests, where the delicate interplay between soil health, plant diversity, and animal populations has been irrevocably altered. This complex interaction highlights the intricacies of biological invasions and their far-reaching implications on ecosystems.
