the definition of consciousness should not be limited to human-centric views based solely on complex language or advanced cognitive reasoning. A broader understanding requires examining how different species utilize vision and other sensory modalities to interact with their environments. For instance, studies have shown that many non-human animals display advanced visual processing capabilities, suggesting a form of consciousness predicated upon their unique perceptual systems. These findings challenge the conventional hierarchy often attributed to intelligence, where human abilities are regarded as the pinnacle. Instead, by exploring consciousness across a spectrum of species, we encounter diverse cognitive architectures that operate within varying ecological niches. This perspective enables us to appreciate the complexities of animal minds that may not express thoughts through language yet reveal sophisticated awareness through their behavior and interactions with their surroundings. Additionally, research into visual perception in ‘lower’ mammals can illuminate evolutionary pathways, providing insights into how consciousness may have developed in different lineages. Thus, expanding our framework to include these alternative forms of consciousness not only enriches our understanding but also invites ethical considerations regarding the treatment of non-human animals. It becomes clear that consciousness transcends human-like traits, rooted in broader biological processes.
