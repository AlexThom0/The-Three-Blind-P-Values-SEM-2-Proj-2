If language interacts variably with cognition, it’s possible that those with a strong inner voice experience different perceptual realities compared to those with a less pronounced or absent inner speech. This variability might not just influence tasks such as categorisation or memory recall but could also impact broader cognitive functions like reasoning and problem-solving. The distinction may be especially relevant in cases where individuals engage in tasks that require abstract thinking or creative ideation, suggesting that some might navigate these tasks more effectively through verbalised thought while others rely on visual or non-verbal strategies. Furthermore, exploring this could lead to insights into neurodiversity, offering a richer understanding of how different cognitive styles contribute to unique modalities of learning and interaction. As we dive deeper into this research, we may uncover that the role of language is not merely a unifying mechanism but rather a diverse tool that shapes individual experiences in distinct ways, influencing how people engage with their surroundings and process information.
