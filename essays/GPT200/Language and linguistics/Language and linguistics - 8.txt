This proposition stems from the observation that gestures can convey complex meanings and are often context-dependent, allowing for a degree of creativity that is less apparent in vocal emissions. Studies show that gestures can establish a shared understanding between individuals without the necessity for spoken words. This flexibility can foster social cohesion and collaborative behavior, which are essential for group survival. Furthermore, the development of pointed gestures—specific signals that direct attention—highlights the cognitive demands that underlie early communicative practices. While vocal signals can be blunt and universally understood within an immediate context, gestures offer a nuanced approach that relies on social dynamics and attentiveness. The implication of this is profound: as early hominins navigated their environments and social structures, it is plausible that their communicative repertoire shifted from crude vocalizations to a more sophisticated system of gestures that laid the groundwork for the complexities of verbal language. This transition underscores the importance of non-vocal communication in the evolution of human linguistic capabilities.
