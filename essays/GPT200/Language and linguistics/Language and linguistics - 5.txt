For instance, the language itself, with its roots in a broader Semitic tradition, reveals connections with other ancient languages such as Hebrew and Phoenician, hinting at trade and cultural exchanges that transcended geographical boundaries. Inscriptions often provide glimpses into familial structures, showcasing the importance of lineage and honor in Punic society. Furthermore, the dedication of stelae not only honored the dead but also reflected the collective values and beliefs that shaped communal identity. Through these texts, we learn about the intertwining of daily life with spiritual obligations, as divinities were central to public and private affairs. The context of each inscription varies, with some evoking the somber reality of death while others celebrate achievements or commemorate significant events. This complex interplay between language, culture, and religion illustrates how inscriptions serve not merely as records but also as a medium through which the Carthaginian worldview is articulated and preserved for posterity.
