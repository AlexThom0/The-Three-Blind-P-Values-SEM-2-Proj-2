When we consider complex systems, like ecosystems or the biosphere, the interdependencies can obscure the lines of descent that define units of selection. For instance, while individual species within an ecosystem may evolve and adapt, the ecosystem itself operates as a dynamic interplay of interactions without a singular reproducing entity. This challenges the conventional understanding of natural selection, as it raises questions about how we delineate the boundaries of reproductive units. The modification of these ecosystems, influenced by biotic and abiotic factors, calls into question the coherence of applying selection theory universally. Some argue for a more expansive definition of selection that accounts for these intricate relationships, emphasizing that the interactions among species can lead to emergent properties that may not be attributable to any single lineage. Thus, the conversation around selection must evolve, embracing nuance and complexity to accurately represent the diversity of reproductive strategies and survival mechanisms in nature. Understanding these distinctions can deepen our insights into the processes shaping life, reflecting a richness that transcends simplistic hierarchies.
