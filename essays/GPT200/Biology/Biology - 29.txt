The disconnect between conservation efforts and local livelihoods creates a cycle of resentment and disregard for wildlife. Many families, struggling to survive, view elephants not as majestic symbols of nature but as direct threats to their food security and economic stability. This adverse relationship is further exacerbated by the lack of educational programs that could help local communities understand the ecological roles of these animals, potentially fostering coexistence instead of conflict. Initiatives aimed at promoting alternative income sources, such as community-based tourism or sustainable agriculture, remain scarce, leaving villagers feeling isolated from the economic benefits that conservation projects promise. Efforts to engage communities in wildlife management have often been sidelined in favor of top-down approaches that prioritize revenue generation over local input. Consequently, the narrative of conservation, which emphasizes the importance of wildlife preservation, risks alienating those who directly bear the brunt of living alongside these animals. Effective conservation must incorporate the voices and needs of local populations, ensuring that the benefits of preserving wildlife are shared equitably.
