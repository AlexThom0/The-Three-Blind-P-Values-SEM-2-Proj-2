This fundamental aspect of consciousness suggests that various non-human species possess varying degrees of sentience, which can be understood through their reactions to stimuli. For instance, the instinctual behaviors exhibited by mammals in response to potential threats demonstrate a rudimentary awareness of their surroundings, thereby implying an ability to experience sensations intensely, even if not articulated. In this sense, while higher-order consciousness is clearly tied to language and self-referential thought, the essence of sentience can exist independently and still drive behavioral responses. This perspective can expand our understanding of consciousness across species by highlighting the different levels of complexity in perceiving and interacting with the world. Consequently, even in the absence of sophisticated linguistic abilities, animals, including amphibians and some avians, showcase distinct forms of consciousness grounded in their sensory experiences. Exploring these dimensions reveals the rich tapestry of conscious experience across the animal kingdom and invites a deeper examination of how sentience manifests differently within various biological frameworks.
