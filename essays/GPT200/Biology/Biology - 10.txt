Each organism, even the simplest cells, engages continuously with their environment, making choices that can influence their survival and reproductive success. This interplay underscores the notion that agency is not merely a function of genetic determinants but is also shaped by environmental factors and learned experiences. For example, the way a predator adapts its hunting strategy or a prey animal modifies its escape tactics illustrates a form of intelligence born of necessity. These responses are often the result of a combination of instinctual programming and situational learning, enabling organisms to thrive despite unpredictable challenges. This perspective encourages a broader understanding of behavioral ecology, where agency emerges not just from inherent biological frameworks but also from the interplay between genetics, environment, and the rich tapestry of experiences encountered throughout an organism's life. Such dynamics highlight that agency is a mosaic of influences that combines biology, social interaction, and environmental context into a cohesive behavioral response. As such, we must appreciate the nuances of agency across different life forms to gain deeper insights into the evolution of complex behaviors.
