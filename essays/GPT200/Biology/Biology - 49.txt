This mechanistic view fundamentally shifted the understanding of nature, where explanations sought not only to describe phenomena but to derive them from measurable forces and natural laws. As observation and experimentation gained prominence, phenomena previously attributed to the intents of a deity or a higher purpose began to be understood through empirical evidence. This marked a crucial departure from teleological thinking, which insisted that observable events carried intrinsic meaning and direction. Instead, the focus turned to causality and the interactions of material components, leading to a reductionist perspective that viewed living entities merely as complex arrangements of matter subject to the same physical laws that governed non-living things. In this context, nature transformed into a space of intricate mechanisms, functioning without an overarching divine narrative. The implications of this shift were profound, setting the stage for the advancements of modern science and redefining humanity's relationship with the natural world. The once-cozy rapport between philosophy and science now faced challenges, as the boundaries between metaphysical considerations and empirical inquiries became increasingly delineated.
