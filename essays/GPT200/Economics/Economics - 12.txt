Consumer preferences are not static; they are shaped by culture, history, and context, leading to fluctuations that a simple equilibrium model struggles to account for. Additionally, the notion of utility is deeply subjective, tied not only to material wealth but also to social relations and psychological well-being. As such, mainstream models can inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities by failing to capture the experiences and realities of marginalized groups. This disconnect raises critical questions about the sustainability of current economic paradigms and their ability to adapt to societal needs. When wealth is concentrated in the hands of a few, the overarching narrative of abundance and happiness becomes increasingly hollow. A more nuanced understanding of human motivation and social structures is essential, implying that the conversation surrounding economic happiness must broaden to include diverse perspectives and lived experiences. Only by embracing this complexity can we hope to forge a path toward economic systems that genuinely prioritize well-being for all.
