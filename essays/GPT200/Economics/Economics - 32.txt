This division epitomized a fundamental barrier that extended beyond language, affecting access to opportunities and resources. The curriculum often aligned with middle-class communication styles, which inadvertently marginalized those who spoke from their lived realities. Consequently, working-class children faced a dual challenge: navigating an educational system that prized a language aligned with privilege while simultaneously yearning for recognition of their own experiences. As a result, many students felt disillusioned and alienated, undermining their confidence and willingness to engage in academic pursuits. This systemic bias not only stifled their potential but also perpetuated an ongoing cycle of socio-economic disadvantage. The repercussions echoed through generations, entrenching inequalities within the educational framework and society at large. Therefore, addressing these disparities is crucial for creating a more equitable system where diverse languages and experiences can be validated rather than dismissed, ultimately fostering an environment where all students can thrive and realize their full potential.
