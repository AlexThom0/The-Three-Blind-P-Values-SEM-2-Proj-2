A broader understanding of happiness also incorporates sociocultural dimensions that challenge purely psychological or economic explanations. For instance, culturally defined values about happiness can shape how individuals perceive and pursue well-being. In collectivist societies, where group harmony and familial obligations prevail, personal happiness may be subordinated to social norms, reflecting a communal rather than an individualistic orientation. This impact becomes evident when contrasting societies—those that prioritize individual success versus those that emphasize social cohesion—yield divergent outcomes in happiness studies. Additionally, the role of community support and social networks cannot be ignored; strong relationships and a sense of belonging often contribute significantly to an individual's overall happiness. Various studies suggest that social capital—the networks of relationships among people—correlates positively with subjective well-being. These elements suggest that while individual traits and material circumstances are important, the social context in which individuals find themselves plays a critical formative role in shaping their happiness. Thus, a multifaceted examination that integrates psychological, economic, and sociocultural factors becomes essential for a comprehensive understanding of what drives happiness in diverse populations.
