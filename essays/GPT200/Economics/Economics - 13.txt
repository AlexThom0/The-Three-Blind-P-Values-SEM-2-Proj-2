However, Patman and his supporters viewed the Act as essential for preserving market diversity and ensuring that the voice of the small business owner remained loud in the face of corporate dominance. They argued that without such protections, the American Dream would be reserved solely for those with substantial means, leaving small operators vulnerable to extinction. The Act introduced a framework that curbed predatory pricing tactics and discouraged exclusive supply agreements, aiming to restore a level playing field. This shift in policy underscored the broader ideological battle between progressivism and economic concentration, with small businesses advocating for regulations that would safeguard not only their own interests but also the fabric of localized economies. Campaigns led by figures like Patman galvanized communities, creating a grassroots movement that spurred consumers to support small businesses directly. As a result, the discourse around economic equity began to evolve, emphasizing the vital role these smaller enterprises played in enhancing consumer choice and fostering innovation.
