Newton posited that this lost wisdom had profound implications for both religion and science, suggesting a harmonious relationship between the sacred and the empirical. He argued that the first religious practices were deeply intertwined with an understanding of natural phenomena, folding astronomy, geometry, and even ethics into a broader narrative about the cosmos. The altars, as symbols of devotion, were not merely sites of worship but functioned as educational tools for understanding celestial mechanics and the divine order. This intertwining of faith and knowledge was essential for early humanity, as it provided a framework through which to decipher the complexities of their world. As societies evolved, however, this unified vision fractured, giving rise to a duality between scientific inquiry and religious belief. Newton lamented this separation, emphasizing that his own quest for truth was not just a thirst for knowledge but a spiritual endeavor—one he believed was crucial for reclaiming the ancient wisdom that could restore humanity’s connection to the cosmos as intended by its Creator.
