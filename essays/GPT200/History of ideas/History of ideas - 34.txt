Instead of relying on rigid economic equations, it delved into the lived experiences of individuals and their disconnection from both labor and community. This emphasis on human experience provided a refreshing avenue for critique and reflection, drawing attention to the often-overlooked social dimensions of economic life. The appeal of the early Marx to the New Left lay in his ability to articulate the discomforts and estrangements wrought by modern capitalist societies. His insights transcended mere economic theory, speaking to a broader spectrum of human conditions that resonated deeply with the disaffected youth of the time. As protests and countercultures emerged, the notion of alienation served as a rallying point, encapsulating frustrations that were not solely economic but deeply rooted in social and existential crises. Thus, the ‘humanist Marx’ not only reclaimed Marx’s relevance for a new generation but also highlighted the limits of conventional economic discourse that failed to account for the emotional and social realities of workers. In doing so, it set the stage for a revival of interest in Marxist thought that would emphasize the significance of human relationships over abstract economic models.
