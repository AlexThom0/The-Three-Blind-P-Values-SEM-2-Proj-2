This focus on narrow definitions of key concepts restricts intellectual engagement with diverse traditions that offer rich, alternative frameworks for understanding. The consequences of such intellectual exclusion are profound, as they not only limit the scope of philosophical discourse but also perpetuate a cycle in which non-Western philosophies remain relegated to the periphery. As scholars have increasingly recognized, this 'philosophical neocolonialism' stifles genuine intercultural dialogue, fostering an environment where critical perspectives from non-Western traditions are often dismissed outright. Moreover, by ignoring the contributions of these diverse philosophies, the broader academic community risks losing invaluable insights into foundational questions about existence, ethics, and social organization. Engaging with these varied philosophical traditions is not merely an act of inclusion; it is essential for the evolution of philosophy as a discipline that claims to grapple with the complexities and diversities of human experience. Only by recognizing and valuing this plurality can contemporary philosophy hope to address its own shortcomings and achieve a more comprehensive understanding of the world.
