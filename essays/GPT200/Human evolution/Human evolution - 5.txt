The uniqueness of each individual's neural architecture emphasizes the complexity of disorders like schizophrenia and autism. Even if brain organoids can be engineered to resemble specific aspects of the human brain, they remain a far cry from wholly replicating the dynamic interplay between neurons that occurs in a living organism. This interplay is essential not only for typical brain function but also for understanding the myriad ways in which developmental disruptions can lead to behavioral and cognitive anomalies. Recent studies have begun to explore how various environmental factors, including stress and nutrition, may induce significant alterations in neural network formation. Such findings suggest that while organoids can provide insights into certain biological mechanisms, they must be interpreted with caution, particularly when it comes to understanding the complexities of neurodevelopmental disorders. Thus, the limitations of organoid models highlight the ongoing need for innovative approaches that account for the multifaceted nature of brain development and its dysregulations.
